## 2026-04-27

- [[British Consols]] (`04 - Early Modern/`) — new topic note. The British Consolidated Annuities, 1751-2015. Henry Pelham's 1749-57 consolidation of nine ragged 3-4% annuity issues into a single 3% perpetual; mechanics (perpetual, redeemable at par at government's option only); wartime workhorse (Seven Years', American Revolutionary, French Revolutionary, Napoleonic — debt £75M in 1751 to £844M in 1815, peak yield 6.4% in 1797 with consols at £48); risk-free benchmark for 19th-century economists (Ricardo, Bagehot, Marx, Marshall, Keynes) and cultural anchor (Austen, Trollope); Goschen Conversion 1888 (3% to 2.75%, £558M, 99% acceptance); WWI War Loans; Chamberlain's 1932 War Loan conversion (5% to 3.5%, £2.1B, 92% acceptance); George Osborne's 2014-2015 final redemption ending a 263-year run. The yield series (1729-2015) is one of the longest unbroken financial time series in existence. Comparison table vs French Rentes makes the fiscal-state argument empirical.
- [[French Rentes]] (`04 - Early Modern/`) — new topic note. French perpetual annuities, 1522-1958. Francis I's 1522 rentes sur l'Hôtel de Ville (8.33% backed by gabelle, routed through Paris municipality because royal credit was already untrustworthy); the eight major Ancien Régime defaults (Henri II 1557, Henri IV 1598, Mazarin 1648 → Fronde, Visa 1721 post-Mississippi, Silhouette 1759, Terray 1770, Brienne 1788, Ramel-Nogaret tiers consolidé 1797 — two-thirds repudiated outright); Napoleon's 5% Rente rebuilt from the surviving third; Villèle 1825 conversion to 3% Rente; Belle Époque rentier era (Balzac, Zola, ~2 million holders); Thiers's 1871-72 Liberation Loans (5 billion gold francs to pay German indemnity, oversubscribed 14× on the second tranche); WWI inflation as ninth de facto default (Poincaré 1928 stabilization at ~22% of pre-war real value); Pinay 1952 (gold-indexed, last consequential rente); replaced by OAT format 1985+. Comparison with British Consols is the canonical fiscal-state natural experiment — same formal instrument, same economic scale, utterly different institutional architecture.

## 2026-04-07

- [[Tiananmen Square]] (`05 - Modern/`) — new event note. Economic reframing of the 1989 protests drawing on Adam Tooze's "Hidden Economic Histories" series (Ones and Tooze Ep. 236, Foreign Policy, April 3 2026). The conventional reading — heroic liberal students crushed by tanks — misses half the story. The protests were social democratic demands *about* ongoing economic reforms: China was growing at ~11%/year, inflation had hit 20% by 1988, a dual-track price system created endemic corruption (resources siphoned from stable-price to liberalized sectors), and when comprehensive price liberalization was proposed in summer 1988 it triggered bank runs. The protests drew students, workers, civil servants, and intellectuals — all groups losing the inflationary redistribution. The crackdown (June 3-4, 1989) resolved the question of the CCP's power monopoly; the post-crackdown austerity + international sudden stop (World Bank, FDI) forced a classic emerging-market contraction. Deng's Southern Tour (January-February 1992) doubled down on marketization: "Development, comrades, is the hard truth." The Chinese New Left reading (Wang Hui): neoliberalism came to China *because of* Tiananmen, not despite it — the crackdown depoliticized distributional questions and cleared the path for accelerated reform. The Clinton pivot (1993-94): Biden and Pelosi led a coalition to condition MFN trade status on human rights; Beijing refused; Clinton delinked trade from human rights in spring 1994, setting the course toward WTO accession. Tooze's four-phase framework: (1) reform and overheating (1978-89), (2) doubling down with rising inequality (1992-2003), (3) social democratic stabilization and housing boom (2003-~2012), (4) Xi's new normal and reasserted party autonomy (~2020-present). The note's deeper point: the protesters' diagnosis was correct — rapid marketization without democratic participation would produce lopsided, corrupt outcomes — but the regime's answer (authoritarian marketization + demonstrated willingness to use force) produced the most dramatic economic transformation in modern history.

- [[Regulatory Backfire]] (`Themes/`) — new theme note. The recurring pattern where regulation produces the harm it was designed to prevent. Eight cases spanning 300 years: British opium prohibition (1729) → smuggling empire, American Prohibition → organized crime, Smoot-Hawley → global trade collapse, rent control → housing shortages, drug war → mass incarceration, Sarbanes-Oxley → IPO drought, federal policing investigations → excess homicides (Fryer-Devi 2020), AI hiring laws → companies abandoning meritocratic algorithms. Structural explanation: asymmetric information (Baron-Myerson 1982), political incentive to legislate rather than design, strategic adaptation by regulated agents. Deeper Point: the pattern persists because regulators optimize for observable compliance (filings, audits, disclosures) rather than outcomes — and political incentives reward passing laws over designing incentive-compatible mechanisms.
