# Sound Dues

In 1429, Denmark built a fortress at the narrowest point of the Øresund strait and started charging every ship that passed — then collected those tolls for 428 years, generating up to two-thirds of state revenue, provoking five wars, and proving that geographic chokepoints could make second-tier states disproportionately powerful. The system survived the [[Hanseatic League]]'s challenge (1370), multiple Swedish invasions, the rise and fall of Dutch commercial supremacy, and only ended in 1857 when the United States refused to pay and a multilateral buyout paid Denmark to stop. The Copenhagen Convention that abolished the dues became a foundational document of international maritime law — and the principle that straits used for international navigation cannot be subject to unilateral tolls was later codified in UNCLOS.

## Geography

The Øresund is the narrow strait between the Danish island of Zealand and the Scanian coast (Swedish since 1658, previously Danish). It connects the North Sea and Atlantic to the Baltic Sea — the only practical commercial route. At its narrowest point, approximately 4km wide. Denmark controlled both shores until 1658, making the strait effectively an internal waterway.

Enforcement centered on **Kronborg Castle** at Helsingør (Elsinore) — the fortress Shakespeare used as the setting for *Hamlet* — with guns covering the entire crossing.

## Establishment (1429)

Introduced by King Eric of Pomerania (Eric VII), who ruled Denmark, Sweden, and Norway under the Kalmar Union. He built the fortress Krogen at Helsingør specifically to enforce the toll. Frederick II rebuilt and expanded it into the Renaissance fortress Kronborg between 1574 and 1585.

**Motivation:** Declining revenues from Øresund herring fisheries, which had provided substantial royal income but collapsed due to overfishing and environmental shifts.

**Predecessor:** Tolls in the Great Belt had been collected by the Danish Crown for at least a century before 1429.

## Revenue — the economic engine

The dues were the single most important revenue source for the Danish Crown for centuries:

| Period | Share of state income |
|--------|----------------------|
| 16th-17th centuries | Up to **two-thirds** (~66%) |
| At abolition (1850s) | ~one-eighth (~12.5%) |
| Annual revenue at abolition | ~1.3 million rigsdaler/year |

**Political consequence:** This revenue made Danish kings **relatively independent of the aristocracy** — no need to negotiate tax grants when the strait generated income autonomously. The Crown's fiscal independence from domestic elites was a direct product of geographic rent.

**Key reform (1567):** Changed from a flat per-ship charge to a **1-2% ad valorem tax on cargo value**, tripling revenue overnight.

## Enforcement

1. Ships required to lower topsail or dip flag when approaching Helsingør
2. Mandatory stop — captains went ashore to declare cargo and pay
3. Warning shot from castle cannons if a ship failed to stop (ammunition cost added to the toll)
4. Gunboat pursuit or shelling for non-compliance

**Christian IV's anti-fraud mechanism:** Captains self-declared their cargo value for the ad valorem tax. Denmark reserved the **right to purchase the entire cargo at the declared price** — creating a self-enforcing incentive for honest declaration. Declare too low, and the Danes buy your goods at a bargain. Physical searches were also conducted.

All alternative routes through the Great Belt and Little Belt were subject to Danish oversight, though the Øresund was the primary commercial route.

## Who paid

**General rule:** All foreign ships, whether or not Denmark was origin or destination.

**Key exemptions:**

| Party | Status |
|-------|--------|
| **[[Hanseatic League]]** | Free passage via Treaty of Stralsund (1370) — the League's greatest diplomatic achievement. Eroded as the Hansa declined. |
| **Sweden** | Initially exempt under Kalmar Union; contested after exit; restored at Peace of Bromsebro (1645) after winning the Torstenson War |
| **Dutch Republic** | Negotiated reduced rates but never full exemption; most persistent opponents |
| **All others** | Paid. By 1840s, Britain alone sent ~4,000 ships/year |

## Trade through the Sound

Virtually all commercial traffic between the North Sea/Atlantic and the Baltic passed through the Danish straits. Shipping volumes from the Sound Toll Registers:

- Late 16th century: 4,000-5,000 vessels annually
- By 1840: Britain alone ~4,000 ships/year
- Modern: 40,000+ ships/year (larger than 50 gross tons)

**Trade composition:**
- **Outbound from Baltic:** Polish rye and wheat (often 50%+ of outbound cargo value), Scandinavian timber and iron, Russian hemp, tar, and potash
- **Inbound to Baltic:** Processed goods, cloth, manufactures from the Netherlands, England, and France

The **Sound Toll Registers** (1497-1857, complete series from 1557) are now a **UNESCO Memory of the World** document — one of the most complete records of early modern European trade, digitized in the Sound Toll Registers Online database (Danish National Archives / University of Groningen).

## Conflicts

The Sound Dues were a recurring casus belli or contributing cause of multiple wars:

### Hanseatic War (1361-1370)
War between the [[Hanseatic League]] and Denmark over Baltic trade routes. Resulted in the **Treaty of Stralsund (1370)** — the high-water mark of Hanseatic power. The League gained:
- Free passage through the Sound for all Hanseatic ships
- Veto power over Danish royal succession

### Dutch-Danish tensions (1611-1614)
Christian IV escalated the dues, prompting Dutch diplomatic and naval pressure. Treaties of 1613-1614 forced Denmark to cancel the increases. The Dutch used naval supremacy as leverage.

### Torstenson War (1643-1645)
Sound Dues were an **official casus belli**. Christian IV had been taxing Swedish export ships despite Sweden's claimed exemption. Sweden invaded Denmark. Ended with the **Peace of Bromsebro (1645)**, which restored Swedish exemption.

### Battle of the Sound (November 8, 1658)
The most strategically revealing episode. Sweden invaded Denmark and besieged Copenhagen, aiming to seize control of both shores and monopolize the strait.

The **Dutch Republic sent 41 warships** to prevent Sweden from controlling access. The Dutch defeated the Swedish fleet (45 ships) and broke the siege of Copenhagen.

**The strategic logic:** The Dutch did not want the tolls abolished — they wanted Denmark, a weak state they could pressure, to keep collecting them rather than Sweden, a strong state they couldn't control. They preferred a *manageable* chokepoint power to an *unmanageable* one.

Denmark lost Scania to Sweden in the subsequent Treaty of Roskilde (1658), meaning it now controlled only one shore — yet the tolls continued for another two centuries through diplomatic inertia.

### US challenge (1855)
The United States contested the dues' legality under international law and **refused to pay**, creating bilateral tensions that helped precipitate the 1857 abolition.

## Abolition (1857)

The **Copenhagen Convention** (March 14, 1857) abolished the Sound Dues permanently and declared all Danish straits international waterways free to all commercial shipping.

**Why Denmark accepted:** By the 1850s, the dues delivered only one-eighth of state income while increasingly harming Copenhagen's own port and merchants. The US challenge forced negotiation.

**Buyout:** ~33-35 million rigsdaler paid as a one-time lump sum, apportioned by historical shipping volume:

| Nation | Approximate share |
|--------|-------------------|
| Great Britain | ~30% (~10-12 million rigsdaler) |
| Russia | ~30% |
| Prussia | ~13% |
| Others (Belgium, France, Netherlands, etc.) | Remainder |
| United States | Separate deal: $393,000 |

## Legal legacy

The Copenhagen Convention established the precedent that **straits used for international navigation cannot be subject to unilateral tolls** — later codified in UNCLOS. The 428-year history also tracks the evolution from *mare clausum* (closed seas — Denmark's position) to *mare liberum* (free seas — Hugo Grotius's 1609 doctrine, championed by the Dutch).

## Economic significance

The Sound Dues illuminate several economic themes:

1. **Geographic rent as state finance**: A state can fund itself entirely from geographic position, bypassing the need to tax domestic production or negotiate with elites
2. **Ad valorem innovation**: The 1567 switch from flat fees to percentage-of-cargo taxation was a fiscal technology that tripled revenue — an early example of tax base optimization
3. **Self-enforcing compliance**: Christian IV's cargo purchase right is a mechanism design achievement — the incentive structure automatically prevents fraud without requiring enforcement
4. **The chokepoint as institution**: The dues persisted for 428 years not because Denmark was powerful but because no single power could unilaterally end them. Abolition required a multilateral convention.

## Modern parallel

The Sound Dues are the historical prototype for what the 2026 Iran conflict demonstrates at the Strait of Hormuz: a second-tier power extracting disproportionate leverage — whether as revenue (Denmark) or as deterrence (Iran) — from controlling a geographic bottleneck. The structural logic is identical; the mechanism differs. Denmark charged tolls; Iran imposed a blockade. Both proved that the chokepoint outlasts any individual crisis.
