# Jeffrey Ding

Jeffrey Ding is a political scientist whose 2024 book *Technology and the Rise of Great Powers* reframes how technological revolutions translate into national power. Against the intuition that the country which *pioneers* a breakthrough industry wins — the leading-sector logic running from Schumpeter through most innovation policy — Ding argues that economic-power transitions are decided by the capacity to *diffuse* a general-purpose technology across the entire economy, a slow process that rewards broad institutional adaptation (mass technical skills, standards, complementary capital) over frontier monopoly. The thesis recasts the rise of the United States over Britain, the durability of American leadership, and the contemporary US-China contest in artificial intelligence as diffusion races rather than invention races.

## Core thesis

A great power's economic ascent tracks its ability to spread a general-purpose technology (GPT) widely and quickly across many sectors, not its ability to dominate the breakthrough industry that introduces it. Innovation primacy is neither necessary nor sufficient; what matters is the "GPT diffusion deficit" a society does or does not close. Because diffusion is slow and institution-heavy, it is also durable: a lead built on economy-wide adoption compounds, where a lead built on a single leading sector can be leapfrogged.

## Mechanisms

- GPTs in the Bresnahan-Trajtenberg sense — wide applicability, scope for continual refinement, strong complementarities with other technologies — deliver their productivity payoff only after a long diffusion phase. This is the productivity-paradox lag Paul David captured in "The Dynamo and the Computer": electrified factories did not raise measured productivity until plants were physically reorganized around the new power source decades after the dynamo existed.
- "GPT skill infrastructure" is the decisive institution: broad systems that spread engineering and applied competence across the workforce (mass technical education, professional associations, standard-setting bodies) outperform elite concentration, because diffusion happens in a thousand ordinary firms, not a few frontier labs.
- The contrast mechanism is the leading-sector (LS) account, in which power flows from monopolizing the new industry's rents. Ding's claim is that the LS mechanism is the exception and GPT diffusion the rule for the largest transitions.

## Cases and evidence

| GPT era | Frontier pioneer | Who captured the economic-power gain | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Steam, machine tools | Britain | Britain | Led both invention and diffusion |
| Electricity, interchangeable manufacturing | Mixed (Europe + US) | United States | Economy-wide electrification + mass production; see [[Second Industrial Revolution]], [[Electricity and Power Systems]] |
| Computing / IT | United States | United States | Deep, broad diffusion across services and manufacturing; see [[Digital Revolution]] |
| Artificial intelligence | Contested | Open question | The diffusion race now underway |

The electrification case is the cleanest historical instance of diffusion beating invention, and it sits beneath the vault's [[Great Divergence]] and [[Market Cap Dominance Cycles]] notes. The mirror image — societies that invented or imported advanced technology but failed to diffuse it — is documented in [[Technology Diffusion Failures]] and [[Why China Didn't Industrialize]].

## Implications

- The US-China AI competition is best read as a diffusion contest: the side that embeds AI across the most sectors, not the side holding the single best frontier model, compounds the advantage. China's "AI Plus" program (2025) and the open-weight-model vector are diffusion strategies in exactly Ding's sense.
- The contest is winnable below the frontier. A follower nation that will never build a frontier model can still capture a GPT's gains by adopting and reorganizing around it — the basis for a "middle-power" technology strategy.

## Critiques and limits

- Diffusion vs. invention is a matter of degree, not a dichotomy; some inputs reward holders strongly. High-appropriability chokepoints (EUV lithography, leading-edge fabrication) can be fenced for a decade — see [[Technology Fencing]] — which is why export controls work on some targets and backfire on others.
- "Diffusion capacity" is hard to measure ex ante; the thesis is cleaner as retrospective explanation than as a forecasting instrument.
- Selection risk: choosing the transitions to explain and then reading diffusion into them. The framework is strongest where the diffusion metric is independent of the outcome.

## Relationship to other frameworks

- Foil to [[Creative Destruction|Schumpeterian]] leading-sector logic, which locates power in pioneering and monopolizing the new industry.
- Complement to [[Technology Fencing]] (appropriability — whether a lead can be kept) and [[Technology Diffusion Failures]] (the institutional reasons diffusion stalls).
- Sits alongside [[Growth Theory]] and [[Innovation Economics]] as an account of where sustained advantage comes from.
