# Thucydides

Thucydides was the Athenian historian whose *History of the Peloponnesian War* turned war writing from heroic narrative into structural analysis. His importance to this vault is not biography but method: he explained the [[Peloponnesian War]] through power, fear, interest, alliance pressure, and material capacity. That makes him the deep ancestor of realist international-relations thinking and the source for the modern [[Thucydides Trap]] frame.

## Core Contribution

| Contribution | Why It Matters |
|--------------|----------------|
| Structural causation | War is explained by shifting power, not only immediate incidents |
| Fear as a historical force | Sparta's fear of [[Athens]] becomes a causal variable |
| Material power | Fleets, tribute, alliances, and logistics matter as much as speeches |
| Human nature under pressure | Crisis reveals ambition, fear, honor, and self-interest |
| Analytical history | History becomes a tool for recognizing recurring patterns |

## Key Work

| Work | Focus |
|------|-------|
| *History of the Peloponnesian War* | The 431-404 BCE conflict between [[Athens]] and [[Sparta]] |

Thucydides' most famous structural claim is that the growth of Athenian power and the fear this created in Sparta made war likely. The modern phrase [[Thucydides Trap]] turns that claim into a general theory of rising-power and established-power conflict.

## Critiques

| Critique | Point |
|----------|-------|
| Elite perspective | Focuses on leaders, assemblies, and strategic decisions more than ordinary people |
| Retrospective coherence | The structural argument can make contingency look inevitable |
| Realist overuse | Modern readers sometimes flatten him into a deterministic power-politics theorist |
| Greek scope | His framework is powerful but rooted in a specific polis world |
