# Kandiaronk

Kandiaronk was a Wendat (Huron) statesman, warrior, diplomat, and intellectual active in the Great Lakes region in the late 17th century. He is significant to economic and political history as the likely source of one of the most influential critiques of European civilization ever recorded—a critique that, through the writings of the Baron de Lahontan, shaped Enlightenment debates about freedom, inequality, and the social contract. His arguments influenced Voltaire, Diderot, Rousseau, and others, yet standard histories of the Enlightenment treat these ideas as European inventions.

## The Debates Near Montreal

Multiple independent sources describe a series of intellectual exchanges between Kandiaronk and the French governor Frontenac in the area around Montreal. These were not casual encounters—they were sustained debates about the proper organization of human societies.

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Setting | Something like an "enlightenment salon before the Enlightenment" |
| Participants | French colonial officials, Jesuit missionaries, indigenous leaders |
| Topics | Human freedom, property, religion, marriage, material inequality, governance |
| Character | Formal debates with rules of discourse—not mere conversation |
| Context | Tumultuous struggles between French, English, Dutch, and indigenous nations over the fur trade |

## Kandiaronk's Critique

His arguments against European civilization, as recorded by multiple sources:

### On Poverty and Inequality

| Argument | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Sharing | "If I have food, I will share it with somebody" |
| Destitution | Scandalized that Europeans let people fall between the cracks—visible poverty in French towns was incomprehensible |
| Material wealth as power | Marveled that Europeans could turn material goods into authority — having more stuff allowed you to make people work for you |
| Wage labor | An alien principle to many indigenous societies—and historically uncommon |

### On Religion

| Argument | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Sectarian division | Christian sects are always arguing among themselves over theological matters |
| Logical challenge | If there is one omnipotent God who sent his son to earth, why did we never hear of him? Why did you have to come explain it to us? |
| Inconsistency | Why are you always divided about it if it's supposed to be the truth? |

### On Discourse

| Observation | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| French conversational style | Always cutting each other off, being rude to each other |
| Material equivalent | Just as they won't share food, they won't share speech |

### On Freedom

The Jesuits were frustrated that Iroquoian and Algonkian societies lacked structures of command. Kandiaronk and others saw a direct connection: where chiefs cannot command, the only way to engage people in any social project is through persuasion, discussion, and debate—the basis of participatory democracy.

## Lahontan's *Dialogues*

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Author | Baron de Lahontan—minor French aristocrat, self-styled title |
| Experience | ~10 years in New France, fluent in Iroquoian and Algonkian languages |
| Character | Troublemaker, constant conflicts with French authorities and Jesuits |
| Book | *Curious Dialogues with a Savage of Good Sense Who Has Traveled* |
| Structure | Battle of wits: Lahontan defends European civilization, "Adario" (based on Kandiaronk) demolishes every argument |
| Reception | Hugely popular, translated into multiple European languages, adapted into stage plays that ran for years |
| Lahontan's celebrity | Ended up at Hanoverian court, befriended Leibniz |

### The Leibniz Confirmation

| Evidence | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Source | Private letter from Leibniz to his friend Bering |
| Content | Mentions casually that Kandiaronk was real, had visited Europe, and thought his society was better |
| Significance | Confirms the debates were not purely fictional projections |
| Discovery | Found by [[David Wengrow]] after *[[The Dawn of Everything]]* was published |

## Influence on the Enlightenment

### The Genre That Followed

Lahontan's book spawned a genre of Enlightenment literature featuring exotic outsiders critiquing European civilization:

| Author | Work | Character |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Lahontan | *Curious Dialogues* (1703) | Adario/Kandiaronk (Wendat) |
| Graffigny | *Letters of a Peruvian Woman* | Zilia (Inca princess) |
| Voltaire | Various dialogues | Multiple exotic figures |
| Diderot | *Supplement to the Voyage of Bougainville* | Tahitians |

Later entries in this genre used purely imaginary figures. But the content of their critiques—the themes, the arguments—traces back through Lahontan to real encounters with indigenous thinkers.

### The Counter-Move: Turgot

| Figure | Role |
|--------|------|
| Anne Robert Turgot | Physiocrat who proposed "modes of subsistence" classification |
| His framework | Hunters → herders → farmers → commercial civilization |
| Effect | Acknowledged indigenous freedoms but classified them as appropriate to a "primitive" technological level |
| Interaction with Graffigny | Proposed she change her book's ending so Princess Zilia realizes that abolishing monarchy and money is impractical; Graffigny ignored him |
| Legacy | Created the intellectual framework that would become Marx's "modes of production"—a theory of liberation built on a conservative counter-move |

### The Standard Dismissal

| Claim | Counter |
|-------|---------|
| These dialogues were pure European projections | Lahontan's were based on real encounters; the content traces to documented indigenous positions |
| Europeans used exotic figures to avoid censorship | They were still publishing the books under their own names |
| "Noble savage" romanticism | The trope was weaponized in the 1930s (Chinard) to dismiss indigenous intellectual contributions; Rousseau didn't actually use it |
| Non-literate people couldn't have had such ideas | Kandiaronk was multilingual, had traveled to France, and debated in formal settings |

## Economic Significance

Kandiaronk's critique highlights several points relevant to economic history:

| Argument | Economic Implication |
|----------|---------------------|
| Material wealth shouldn't equal authority | Challenges the assumption that property → power is natural |
| Sharing is the default; hoarding is the aberration | Inverts standard economic assumptions about accumulation |
| Wage labor is not natural | It had to be created and imposed—see [[Institutions and Progress]] |
| Freedom and equality preceded capitalism | They weren't products of Enlightenment philosophy but indigenous realities that Enlightenment thinkers engaged with |
