# 1973 Oil Crisis

The 1973 oil crisis began as an Arab political weapon — [[OAPEC]] members led by [[Saudi Arabia]] embargoed nations supporting [[Israel]] in the Yom Kippur War — and ended as the event that restructured the global economy. Crude prices quadrupled from ~$2.90/bbl to ~$11.65/bbl between October 1973 and March 1974, triggering the first global recession tied to energy prices and introducing "stagflation" (simultaneous inflation and stagnation) as a permanent feature of the policy vocabulary. The crisis killed the post-war assumption that cheap energy was a constant, created the [[International Energy Agency|IEA]] (1974) and the [[Strategic Petroleum Reserve]] (1975), imposed a national 55 mph speed limit on US highways, and accelerated nuclear power programs in [[France]], [[Japan]], and elsewhere. Most consequentially, it shifted the global financial balance of power toward oil-producing states, triggering the recycling of petrodollars through Western banks and laying the groundwork for the [[Petrodollar System]] — [[Saudi Arabia]] pricing oil in USD, purchasing US weapons and Treasuries — that anchored dollar hegemony for 50 years.

## Timeline

- Oct 6, 1973: [[Egypt]] and [[Syria]] launch surprise attack on [[Israel]] (Yom Kippur War / Ramadan War).
- Oct 16: [[OAPEC]] raises posted oil price 70%. Oil ministers agree to production cuts.
- Oct 19: [[Nixon]] requests $2.2B in emergency aid for Israel. OAPEC institutes embargo against the [[United States]].
- Oct 20: [[Saudi Arabia]] declares total embargo on US oil shipments.
- Late Oct: Embargo extended to [[Canada]], [[Japan]], [[Netherlands]], [[United Kingdom]].
- Nov 23: Embargo extended to [[Portugal]], Rhodesia, [[South Africa]].
- Nov 27: Nixon signs Emergency Petroleum Allocation Act authorizing price, production, allocation, and marketing controls.
- Dec 1973: [[OPEC]] raises posted price to $11.65/bbl (~300% increase from pre-crisis).
- Jan 18, 1974: [[Kissinger]] negotiates First Egyptian-Israeli Disengagement Agreement (Sinai I). Saudi King [[Faisal]] persuaded that conditions for embargo have ended.
- Mar 18, 1974: OAPEC lifts embargo.

## The missed warning

[[Kissinger]] declined to meet with four oil company CEOs who warned of King [[Faisal]]'s serious threats about an embargo in summer 1973. The warnings were assessed as inconsequential. This intelligence failure — dismissing a capability as a bluff because exercising it seemed irrational — has recurred in every subsequent energy supply shock.

## Consequences

The embargo triggered stagflation across the Western world. The [[United States]] experienced simultaneous inflation and economic contraction, which persisted through the decade and shaped [[Federal Reserve]] policy under [[Arthur Burns]] and later [[Paul Volcker]].

Institutional responses created during or because of the crisis:

- [[International Energy Agency|IEA]] (1974): proposed by [[Kissinger]] to coordinate Western energy policy and maintain emergency oil reserves.
- [[Strategic Petroleum Reserve]] (1975): authorized by the Energy Policy and Conservation Act, the US government's emergency crude stockpile.
- National 55 mph speed limit (1974-1995).
- Accelerated nuclear power programs in [[France]], [[Japan]], and others.
- Dawn of energy conservation as policy priority.

The deeper structural consequence was financial. The crisis permanently shifted wealth to oil-producing states, and the mechanism for recycling that wealth — OPEC surpluses deposited in Western banks, then lent aggressively to developing countries — created the circular flow that became the [[Petrodollar System]]. That system solved three simultaneous problems (post-[[Bretton Woods]] dollar demand, OPEC surplus destination, trade deficit financing) but contained the seeds of the 1980s Third World debt crisis.

## As benchmark

The 1973 crisis remains the standard reference point for severe oil supply shocks. Every subsequent disruption — the 1979 Iranian Revolution, the 1990 Gulf War, and the 2026 Strait of Hormuz closure — is measured against it. The 2026 disruption removed ~20 mb/d from seaborne markets, roughly 4.5x the volume of 1973, though in a much larger global economy.
