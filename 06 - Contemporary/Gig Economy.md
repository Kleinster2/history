# Gig Economy

The gig economy—platform-mediated work where workers are classified as independent contractors—emerged as one of the defining labor market features of the 2010s. Uber, launched in 2009, became the model: an app connecting workers and customers, with the platform taking a cut but bearing none of the traditional employer obligations. By 2023, tens of millions worked in gig arrangements globally. The model raised fundamental questions about [[Labor History|labor]] protections, worker classification, and whether technology had created a new form of employment or a new form of exploitation.

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Platform | Connects workers and customers |
| Worker status | Independent contractor (typically) |
| Pricing | Algorithm-set, surge pricing |
| Matching | App matches supply/demand |
| Rating | Bilateral reputation systems |

### Platform Economics

| Revenue Source | Description |
|----------------|-------------|

| No benefits | Workers bear all costs |
| No minimum wage | Paid per task |
| Flexible supply | Scale workforce up/down |
| VC-subsidized | Below-cost service to gain market share |

## The Classification Debate

### Employee vs. Contractor

| Factor | Employee | Contractor |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Control | Employer directs work | Worker controls how |
| Integration | Core business function | Ancillary |
| Investment | Employer provides tools | Worker provides |
| Profit/loss | Employer bears | Worker bears |
| Relationship | Ongoing | Task-based |

### Why It Matters

| If Employee | If Contractor |
|-------------|---------------|
| Minimum wage | No minimum |
| Overtime | None |
| Benefits (ACA) | Buy own |
| Unemployment insurance | None |
| Workers' comp | None |
| Social Security (employer share) | Worker pays self-employment |
| Unionization rights | None |

### Legal Battles

| Jurisdiction | Status |
|--------------|--------|
| California AB5 | Required employee classification |
| Prop 22 | Carve-out for gig platforms |
| UK Supreme Court | Uber drivers are workers |
| EU | Proposed platform work directive |
| US federal | No comprehensive law |

## The Deeper Point

The gig economy represents a fundamental question about the [[Labor History\|employment relationship]]: is it being unbundled into its component parts, with flexibility the prize, or is it being eroded, with workers bearing risks that employers once managed? The platforms' rhetoric of "entrepreneurship" and "flexibility" obscures the reality of algorithmic control—workers may choose when to work, but have no say over pay, conditions, or continued access. This is autonomy over schedule, not autonomy over work. The gig economy also reveals the limits of [[Business Regulation\|existing labor law]], built for the factory and office, not the app. The deeper pattern connects to [[Capitalism\|capitalism's]] long-term tendency to commodify labor and shift risk from capital to workers. The question is whether new [[Institutions and Progress\|institutions]]—portable benefits, sectoral bargaining, platform cooperatives—can reconstruct protections without eliminating the genuine flexibility some workers value.
