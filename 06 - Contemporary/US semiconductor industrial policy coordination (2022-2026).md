# US semiconductor industrial policy coordination (2022-2026)

The 2022-2026 US semiconductor industrial-policy sequence is the most coordinated industrial-policy episode in the postwar US economy. Starting with the CHIPS and Science Act (August 2022, $52B in subsidies for domestic fabrication), it culminated in May 2026 with President Trump personally advocating Apple CEO Tim Cook to commit to Intel chip manufacturing during a White House meeting — the demand-side completion of supply-side intervention that built [[Intel]] into a viable foundry alternative to [[TSMC]]. The framework now operating resembles South Korean / Taiwanese / Japanese industrial-policy practice more than the laissez-faire norm of US 1980s-2020s technology policy.

## The sequence

Industrial-policy episodes work in phases. The 2022-2026 US semiconductor sequence had five distinct phases over four years:

| Phase | Year | Action | Side |
|-------|------|--------|------|
| Supply I | Aug 2022 | [[CHIPS Act]] signed: $52B in fab subsidies, $39B for manufacturing, $13B for R&D | Money |
| Supply II | 2024-2025 | First disbursements: Intel Ohio, Arizona, Ireland fabs; TSMC Arizona; Samsung Texas | Construction |
| Supply III | Aug 2025 | Trump administration takes ~10% equity stake in [[Intel]] | Ownership |
| Demand I | Apr 7, 2026 | [[TERAFAB]] anchor deal — Tesla / SpaceX / xAI commit to Intel 18A foundry | Commercial commitment |
| Demand II | May 8, 2026 | Apple-Intel preliminary chip-making agreement following Trump-Cook White House advocacy | Presidential demand-side intervention |

The earlier phases built capacity. The May 2026 phase filled it. The whole sequence required four years and required the administration to maintain industrial-policy direction across the 2024 election transition — a notable institutional fact in itself.

## What's novel

The supply-side phases (subsidies, equity stake) have US precedents:
- Hamilton's [[Report on Manufactures]] (1791): selective tariffs + manufacturing subsidies as deliberate industrial policy
- Post-WWII federal R&D spending: RAND, DARPA, National Labs as supply-side technological-leadership infrastructure
- 1980s Sematech consortium: government-coordinated semiconductor R&D + DRAM duties

What's novel in the 2022-2026 sequence is the explicit demand-side coordination via presidential advocacy. The Cook-Trump White House meeting represents direct executive intervention to steer America's most strategically important fabless customer toward a domestic foundry's order book. There is no postwar US precedent for that mechanism at this scale.

The closest historical parallels are foreign:

| Country | Comparable mechanism | Era |
|---------|---------------------|-----|
| Japan | MITI coordinating Toyota / Toshiba / NEC customer-supplier relationships | 1950s-80s |
| South Korea | Government-coordinated chaebol commercial-customer steering for Samsung / Hyundai / LG | 1960s-90s |
| Taiwan | Coordinated tax + procurement policy supporting TSMC's expansion | 1980s-present |

These three Asian models all combine state-funded production capacity with state-directed commercial demand. The US 2026 framework adopts that pattern — which makes the 2022-2026 sequence the first US adoption of the East Asian developmental-state playbook in semiconductor policy.

## Historical parallels with prior US industrial-policy episodes

### 1980s US-Japan semiconductor episode

The closest US precedent is the 1980s US-Japan semiconductor episode, which Japan eventually won (Japanese DRAM share peaked ~80% in 1986) before losing on the next cycle to Korean and Taiwanese entrants. The US response then:

| Mechanism | Result |
|-----------|--------|
| 1986 US-Japan Semiconductor Agreement | Bilateral managed trade — quotas, price floors, market-share targets |
| Sematech (1987) | $500M / 5-year government-industry R&D consortium |
| Section 301 / Super 301 trade pressure | Selective trade-tool deployment |

The 1980s response was reactive (responding to a foreign threat) rather than proactive (building domestic capability). The 2022-2026 sequence is the inverse: proactive supply-side construction followed by demand-side direction. It is also broader (touches more of the value chain) and longer-duration (multi-year vs the 1986-90 acute phase).

### Post-2008 Chinese industrial policy

The other recent comparable is post-2008 Chinese industrial-policy direction toward indigenous semiconductor capability:

| Mechanism | Period | Result |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| 2014 National IC Development Outline | 2014+ | First explicit semiconductor industrial-policy framework |
| Big Fund I (2014) | $20B | Supply-side capital deployment |
| Big Fund II (2019) | $29B | Continued supply-side support |
| Big Fund III (2024) | ~$48B | Largest single round in semiconductor industrial-policy history |
| "Made in China 2025" | 2015+ | Stated targets for indigenous semiconductor capability |

China's program is supply-side dominant — heavy capital deployment with limited commercial-customer coordination (SMIC has struggled to maintain customer access partly because export controls limit upstream equipment, not because demand-side coordination is absent — there isn't enough Chinese demand at advanced nodes yet to coordinate against). The US framework in 2022-2026 is comparable in capital scale (~$52B subsidies + 10% Intel equity + $50B+ private capex) but adds the demand-side coordination element China has not yet implemented.

### Hamilton's Report on Manufactures (1791)

The deepest historical precedent is [[Alexander Hamilton]]'s 1791 [[Report on Manufactures]], which argued for selective tariffs + manufacturing subsidies to develop domestic industrial capability against more established British manufacturers. Hamilton's report was politically blocked at the time but influenced the [[Friedrich List]] "infant industry" framework that became canonical in 19th-century continental European industrial policy (notably German under Bismarck and Japanese in the Meiji Restoration).

The structural parallel: in both cases, the US is the laggard relative to foreign incumbents at the technological frontier (UK textiles vs colonial America in 1791; Taiwan / Korea / Japan semiconductors vs US fabless in 2020s) and government uses subsidies + market-access advocacy to build domestic capability against the foreign incumbent. The Hamilton framework would have been instantly legible to anyone watching the 2022-2026 sequence.

## Why this took until now

The 2022-2026 sequence required a political environment where industrial-policy coordination was legible across both parties. Three factors converged:

1. China industrial-policy challenge (2014-present) made "denial of indigenous Chinese capability" a bipartisan consensus, which provided the political cover for US industrial-policy interventions that would have been blocked as "picking winners" in the 2008-2016 framework.
2. COVID-era supply-chain failures (2020-22) demonstrated that the laissez-faire framework had created brittle production geographies dependent on Taiwan + Korea + China. The CHIPS Act passed within months of the worst chip-shortage signals reaching the public.
3. Trump administration's willingness to use presidential authority for industrial-policy direction (2017-21 and 2025-present). The Aug 2025 Intel equity stake and May 2026 Cook-Trump advocacy required executive-branch tools that prior administrations had been hesitant to deploy at this scale.

The combination — bipartisan congressional consensus on the threat + acute supply-chain failure-driven legitimacy + executive willingness to act — created the policy window. The window may not stay open; future administrations may revert to the laissez-faire framework. But within the 2022-2026 window, the US adopted an East Asian developmental-state playbook and used it to recast its position in the global semiconductor industry.

## What the historical record suggests for 2026-2030

The 1980s US-Japan episode lasted from the 1986 agreement to roughly 1995 — about a decade from supply-side intervention to durable change in industry structure. The Chinese semiconductor industrial-policy episode is now in its 12th year (2014-2026) and indigenous capability at leading edge is still building. The US 2022-2026 sequence is four years in.

Historical precedent suggests:

1. Supply-side investment + demand-side coordination typically requires 8-15 years to produce durable industry-structure changes. Patience is required.
2. The most successful episodes (Japan 1955-1985, Taiwan 1980-2010, Korea 1970-2000) combined export-oriented demand discipline with domestic capability building. The US framework's analog of "export-oriented demand discipline" would be using US fabless customer commitments (Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, Qualcomm) to discipline Intel's process roadmap rather than letting Intel set its own roadmap by internal-IDM logic.
3. Industrial policy that turns into permanent rent-seeking destroys its own legitimacy (Argentina, multiple Latin American industrial-policy episodes). The US framework will need to demonstrate ongoing competitive discipline — not "Intel always wins because Apple committed" but "Intel wins iteratively by hitting yield + node milestones." Whether the 2022-2026 sequence is the start of a durable East Asian-style framework or a politically-contingent episode that reverts in 2028-2032 is the open question.
