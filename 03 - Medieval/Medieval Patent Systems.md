# Medieval Patent Systems

The Venetian patent statute of 1474—the first systematic patent law—created a revolutionary institutional bargain: inventors disclosed their innovations in exchange for temporary monopoly rights. This channeled entrepreneurial ambition toward disclosure rather than secrecy, enabling cumulative innovation. The Venetian model spread to England (1624), then the world, becoming a foundation of modern innovation systems.

| Factor | Role |

| Arsenal | Naval innovation |
| Foreign craftsmen | Need to attract skills |

### The Statute

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| Coverage | "New and ingenious devices" |
| Term | 10 years exclusive |
| Requirement | Must be workable and useful |
| Enforcement | Penalties for infringement |
| Registration | Must record with authorities |

| Breakthrough | Significance |
|--------------|--------------|
| General availability | Anyone could apply |
| Systematic process | Standard terms |
| Disclosure requirement | Knowledge entered public record |
| Time limit | Eventually became public |
| State enforcement | Government protected rights |

| Provision | Patents only for "new manufactures" |

## The Deeper Point

The Venetian patent statute solved a fundamental problem of [[Innovation Economics|innovation]]: how to reward inventors without permanent monopoly. Before patents, craftsmen faced a choice—keep innovations secret (preserving advantage but limiting diffusion) or reveal them (losing competitive edge). Patents offered a third way: temporary exclusivity in exchange for disclosure. This channeled [[Institutions and Progress|entrepreneurial ambition]] toward revelation rather than concealment. The genius was recognizing that knowledge eventually becoming free was a feature, not a bug—disclosed innovations entered the public domain, enabling cumulative improvement. [[Venice]] attracted foreign craftsmen specifically because it guaranteed they could profit from their innovations. The English Statute of Monopolies added another dimension: patents as constitutional limit on arbitrary royal power. The institutional package—disclosure, temporary protection, eventual public access—became the template for modern intellectual property. It worked because it aligned private incentive (monopoly profits) with public benefit (knowledge diffusion)—the same alignment that defines successful institutions everywhere.
