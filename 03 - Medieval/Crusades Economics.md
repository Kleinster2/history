# Crusades Economics

The economic dimension of holy war. The Crusades (1095-1291) required unprecedented financial mobilization, created new mechanisms for moving money across continents, and opened Mediterranean trade routes that enriched Italian merchants. Religious mobilization had lasting economic consequences.

## The Deeper Point

The Crusades show how [[Institutions and Progress|institutional innovation]] can emerge from unlikely sources. Holy war required solving practical problems—how to move money across continents, how to finance years-long expeditions, how to sustain forces far from home. The solutions—Templar banking, Italian transport contracts, crusade taxation—created infrastructure that outlasted the religious mission.

The economic consequences exceeded the military ones. The Crusader states lasted two centuries; the Italian merchant networks lasted five. The financial techniques spread from crusade finance to general commerce. The demand for Eastern goods that crusaders encountered continued after they lost access to the source.

The Fourth Crusade's diversion reveals the underlying dynamics. Venice redirected a crusade to commercial ends, sacking Constantinople for trade advantages. The religious framing couldn't contain the economic forces the Crusades had unleashed.

The [[Institutions and Progress|institutional]] lesson is that large-scale mobilization creates capabilities that persist after the original purpose ends. Crusade finance became European finance. Holy war became trade war. The Mediterranean integration that resulted—Italian ships, banking networks, luxury goods demand—shaped European development for centuries.
