# Medieval Technology Diffusion

The medieval world saw remarkable technology flows: paper from China to the Islamic world to Europe; the compass enabling oceanic navigation; gunpowder transforming warfare. But diffusion was not automatic—it required institutional channels: trade routes, warfare, migration, and translation movements. Societies that were institutionally open adopted faster; those with rigid institutions blocked or delayed adoption. The pattern reveals that technology alone is never enough—institutions determine whether innovations spread.

## Barriers to Diffusion

### Institutional Barriers

| Barrier | Example |
|---------|---------|
| [[Guild Economics Deepened\|Guilds]] | Protected techniques as secrets |
| State control | Venice banned glass worker emigration |
| Religious prohibition | Some Islamic resistance to printing |
| Class system | Caste restrictions on crafts |

### Knowledge Barriers

| Barrier | Character |
|---------|-----------|
| Tacit knowledge | Hard to transfer without persons |
| Language | Technical vocabulary |
| Context | Techniques needed specific inputs |
| Integration | Required complementary skills |

### Geographic Barriers

| Barrier | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| Distance | Slowed all transfer |
| Mountains | Isolated regions |
| Deserts | Limited movement |
| Oceans | Barrier until navigation improved |

## The Deeper Point

Technology diffusion reveals that [[Innovation Economics|innovation]] is only half the story—adoption matters equally. The compass existed in China for centuries before it transformed European navigation. Paper took a millennium to travel from China to Europe. Gunpowder's military potential was realized differently in different societies. The key variable was [[Institutions and Progress|institutional openness]]: trade routes that carried knowledge, translation movements that converted it, competitive environments that rewarded adoption, and absence of barriers that blocked it. The [[Islamic Golden Age]] was a hub of translation and adaptation; [[Venice]] banned its glassmakers from emigrating but eagerly imported foreign techniques; China invented printing but didn't have the alphabetic system or competitive environment that made European printing explosive. The same technology produced different outcomes in different institutional contexts. This pattern—invention in one place, adoption in another—would characterize the entire premodern period. It explains why the [[Great Divergence]] question isn't "who invented?" but "who adopted and improved?"
