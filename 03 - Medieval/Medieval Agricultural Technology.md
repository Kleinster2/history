# Medieval Agricultural Technology

The medieval agricultural revolution—heavy plows, horse collars, three-field rotation, water and wind mills—doubled European productivity and freed labor for cities and commerce. These innovations, spreading unevenly across regions from the 9th to 13th centuries, created the material foundation for the [[Medieval Economy|commercial revolution]]. Without agricultural surplus, no urban growth; without surplus labor, no specialization; without specialization, no progress.

## Overview

| Innovation | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| Heavy plow | Opened heavy clay soils |
| Horse collar | Faster, more efficient plowing |
| Three-field rotation | 50% more land under cultivation |
| Water mills | Mechanized grinding, fulling |
| Wind mills | Power where water unavailable |

### Technology

| Feature | Character |
|---------|-----------|
| Structure | Iron share, moldboard, wheels |
| Function | Cuts, turns heavy soil |
| Predecessor | Scratch plow (ard) |
| Requirement | Team of oxen or horses |
| Cost | Major capital investment |

### Significance

| Impact | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| New lands | Northern European clays cultivable |
| Settlement | Expansion into forest, marsh |
| Productivity | Higher yields per seed |
| Labor | Required cooperative teams |
| Lordship | Expensive tool → lord-provided |

### Spread

| Region | Timing |
|--------|--------|
| Northern France | 7th-8th centuries |
| England | 9th-10th centuries |
| Germany | 9th-11th centuries |
| Eastern Europe | Later, variable |
| Mediterranean | Often unnecessary (light soils) |

## Horse Collar and Harness

### The Innovation

| Feature | Character |
|---------|-----------|
| Problem | Old harness choked horses |
| Solution | Rigid collar on shoulders |
| Origin | Central Asia, via steppe |
| Effect | Horses could pull heavy loads |
| Complement | Horseshoes, new harness |

### Why It Mattered

| Comparison | Ox | Horse |
|------------|-----|-------|
| Speed | Slow | 50% faster |
| Endurance | Long | Less |
| Cost | Cheaper | More expensive |
| Feed | Grass | Oats (crop) |
| Plowing | Steady | Faster |

### Adoption Pattern

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Climate | Oat-growing regions favored horses |
| Wealth | Richer peasants adopted first |
| Crop mix | Required oat production |
| Regional | Northern Europe faster adoption |

## The Deeper Point

Medieval agricultural innovations were the material foundation for everything else—the [[Medieval Urban Development|urban revival]], the [[Trade Networks|commercial revolution]], the [[Medieval Financial Innovation|financial innovations]], the [[Guild Economics|craft guilds]]. Without surplus, no cities; without surplus labor, no specialization; without specialization, no progress. The heavy plow opened Northern Europe's heavy clay soils to cultivation. The horse collar made plowing faster. Three-field rotation put 50% more land under cultivation. Mills mechanized grinding and fulling, freeing labor. Together, these innovations doubled or tripled productivity from Carolingian levels. But adoption wasn't automatic—it required [[Institutions and Progress|institutions]]: lords who would invest in mills, villages that could coordinate field rotation, markets that rewarded surplus production, monasteries that transferred techniques. The regional variation in adoption—fast in Northern France and England, slow in the Mediterranean and East—reflected institutional variation, not technological ignorance. Agricultural technology reveals the fundamental constraint: progress requires feeding people. Every subsequent development—commerce, industry, urbanization—rests on the ability to produce food surplus.
