# Radhanites

The Radhanites were medieval Jewish long-distance traders who operated transcontinental routes from Western Europe to China during the 8th-11th centuries. At a time when Christendom and the Islamic world were often at war, Jewish merchants could cross both—uniquely positioned to connect fragmented trade networks. They represent the earliest known group to trade regularly between Western Europe and East Asia.

## Routes

### Four Routes Described

| Route | Path |
|-------|------|
| 1. Land-Sea | France → Spain → Morocco → Egypt → Red Sea → India → China |
| 2. Mediterranean-Euphrates | France → Antioch → Baghdad → Persian Gulf → India → China |
| 3. Land (northern) | France → Slavic lands → Khazaria → Central Asia → China |
| 4. Land (southern) | Spain → Morocco → North Africa → Egypt → Levant → Iraq → Persia → India |

---

## Decline

### End of the Radhanites

| Factor | Date | Effect |
|--------|------|--------|
| Khazar collapse | ~969 CE | Northern route closed |
| Fatimid Egypt | 969 CE | New middlemen |
| Italian city-states | 10th-11th c. | Competition |
| Crusades | 1095+ | Changed trade patterns |
| Mongol peace | 13th c. | New routes, new traders |

By 1000 CE, the Radhanite network had largely disappeared, replaced by Italian maritime trade and later Mongol-era routes.

## Economic Significance

| Significance | Description |
|--------------|-------------|
| Early globalization | Europe-China connection |
| Diaspora trade model | Network across political boundaries |
| Trust-based commerce | Reputation over law |
| Niche exploitation | Advantage from marginality |

The Radhanites show how marginal position can become economic advantage—their exclusion from settled roles enabled mobility across boundaries others couldn't cross.
