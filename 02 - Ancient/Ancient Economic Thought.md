# Ancient Economic Thought

What did ancients think about wealth, commerce, profit? Ancient attitudes toward economic activity shaped institutions—Aristotle's suspicion of trade influenced 2,000 years of anti-commercial bias in Western thought. Roman ambivalence about commerce coexisted with vast trading networks. Chinese schools debated whether merchants helped or harmed society. These ideas weren't merely academic—they shaped policies, laws, and the status of those who traded.

## Aristotle on Wealth

### Chrematistics: The Art of Acquisition

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| Natural chrematistics | Acquisition for household needs |
| Unnatural chrematistics | Acquisition for unlimited gain |
| Oikos | Household as natural unit |
| Polis | City as highest community |

### Natural vs. Unnatural

| Type | Characteristics |
|------|-----------------|
| Natural | Use value, limited, for life |
| Unnatural | Exchange value, unlimited, abstract |
| Agriculture | Natural (produces from nature) |
| Retail trade | Unnatural (profit from exchange) |
| Usury | Most unnatural (money breeding) |

### The Critique of Commerce

| Argument | Implication |
|----------|-------------|
| Unlimited desire | Trade seeks profit without limit |
| Against nature | Money doesn't reproduce |
| Corrupts character | Traders become grasping |
| Harms polis | Commercial motive vs. civic virtue |

### Why This Mattered

Aristotle's framework positioned commerce as morally suspect—necessary perhaps, but degrading. This influenced medieval scholastics, Islamic jurists, and early modern moralists. The distinction between "use" and "exchange" value reappears in Marx. Aristotle gave intellectual authority to anti-commercial sentiment.

## Plato on Economics

### Ideal State Economics

| Feature | Republic |
|---------|----------|
| Division of labor | Natural specialization |
| Producer class | Farmers, craftsmen (lowest) |
| Guardian class | Soldiers (middle) |
| Philosopher-kings | Rulers (highest) |
| Property | Common for guardians |
| Commerce | Necessary evil |

### The Problem of Wealth

| Concern | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| Wealth corrupts | Oligarchy emerges |
| Poverty corrupts | Desperate actions |
| Middle way | Moderate property best |
| Luxury | Expands city dangerously |

### Practical Implications

| Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------------|-----------|
| No gold/silver for citizens | Prevents corruption |
| Limit land accumulation | Equality preserves stability |
| Retail trade by foreigners | Citizens shouldn't trade |
| Price controls | Prevent exploitation |

## Roman Writers on Wealth

### Cato the Elder (234-149 BCE)

| View | Content |
|------|---------|
| Agriculture | Only honorable wealth source |
| Commerce | Risky, somewhat disreputable |
| Usury | Worse than murder |
| Paterfamilias | Estate management as virtue |

### Cicero (106-43 BCE)

| Activity | Assessment |
|----------|------------|
| Agriculture | Honorable, fitting for gentleman |
| Wholesale trade | Acceptable if large-scale |
| Retail trade | Sordid, demeaning |
| Crafts | Illiberal, servile |
| Professional services | Some acceptable (medicine, architecture, teaching) |
| Usury | Odious |

### Seneca (4 BCE-65 CE)

| Theme | Position |
|-------|----------|
| Wealth | Indifferent (Stoic view) |
| Proper use | Generous, not hoarding |
| Problem | Attachment, not possession |
| Commerce | Acceptable if not obsessive |

### Roman Paradox

Roman elite professed disdain for commerce while profiting enormously from it—through agents, freedmen, and silent partnerships. The ideology of agricultural virtue coexisted with commercial reality. This hypocrisy allowed commerce to flourish while maintaining aristocratic self-image.

## Chinese Schools

### Confucian View

| Ranking | Occupation |
|---------|------------|
| 1 | Scholars (shi) |
| 2 | Farmers (nong) |
| 3 | Artisans (gong) |
| 4 | Merchants (shang) |

| Concern | Reasoning |
|---------|-----------|
| Merchants produce nothing | Parasitic on producers |
| Profit motive | Corrupts moral character |
| Luxury | Destabilizes social order |
| Mobility | Threatens hierarchy |

### Legalist View

| Position | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| State interest | Commerce serves or threatens state |
| Monopolies | State should control key goods |
| Merchants | Useful if controlled |
| Regulation | Extensive market oversight |

### Daoist View

| Principle | Implication |
|-----------|-------------|
| Wu wei | Don't interfere with nature |
| Simplicity | Less commerce, simpler life |
| Small states | Localized, self-sufficient |
| Against accumulation | Moderation in all things |

### Mohist View

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| Universal benefit | Trade can help all |
| Against luxury | Waste harms society |
| Practical focus | Useful production valued |
| State neutrality | Let markets function |

### Practical Outcome

Despite Confucian ideology ranking merchants lowest, [[Ancient China|Chinese]] commerce flourished. Merchants couldn't hold office but could become wealthy. The ideology constrained but didn't prevent commercial development—merchants simply couldn't convert wealth to political power.

## Comparative Analysis

### Common Themes

| Theme | Greek | Roman | Chinese |
|-------|-------|-------|---------|
| Agriculture honored | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Commerce suspect | Yes | Yes (in theory) | Yes |
| Unlimited gain problematic | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Status hierarchy | Citizen > metic | Senator > equestrian > freedman | Scholar > farmer > artisan > merchant |

### Why Suspicion of Commerce?

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| Elite perspective | Writers were landowners |
| Political threat | Wealth enables power |
| Moral framework | Virtue vs. profit |
| Social order | Commerce disrupts hierarchy |
| Unlimited nature | No natural boundary |

### What Varied

| Dimension | Variation |
|-----------|-----------|
| Practice vs. ideology | Romans more hypocritical |
| State role | Chinese more regulatory |
| Religious dimension | Usury prohibitions varied |
| Merchant status | Metics vs. freedmen vs. shang |

## The Deeper Point

Ancient attitudes toward commerce shaped [[Institutions and Progress|institutions]]—Aristotle's suspicion of trade influenced 2,000 years of anti-commercial bias. Merchants were ranked low in [[Ancient Greece|Greek]], [[Roman Empire|Roman]], and [[Ancient China|Chinese]] status hierarchies. Commerce was seen as morally suspect—unlimited, corrupting, parasitic on real producers. This intellectual framework constrained but didn't prevent commerce—Romans traded despite professed disdain, Chinese merchants grew wealthy despite last-place ranking. The ideas mattered less for stopping trade than for shaping its institutional context: merchants couldn't convert wealth to political power, had to operate through proxies, faced status penalties. Modern economics emerged partly by challenging these assumptions—rehabilitating commerce, trade, and profit as socially beneficial. Adam Smith's defense of the merchant was revolutionary precisely because ancient prejudice remained powerful. Understanding [[Economic Convergence|why all societies]] eventually embraced markets requires understanding why they initially didn't—and ancient economic thought reveals the intellectual foundations of commercial suspicion.
