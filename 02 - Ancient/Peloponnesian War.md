# Peloponnesian War

The Peloponnesian War (431-404 BCE) was ancient Greece's great civil conflict—pitting [[Athens]] and its maritime empire against [[Sparta]] and its land-based allies. The war transformed Greek politics and economics: Athens lost its empire, Sparta gained temporary hegemony, and the entire Greek world was weakened. The conflict shows how war exhausts economies and how commercial power (Athens) can lose to military persistence (Sparta).

## The Persian Recovery (404-386 BCE)

Sparta's victory had been bought with Persian gold. The treaties of 411 BCE with the satrap [[Tissaphernes]] and the subsidies of [[Cyrus the Younger]] after 407 BCE funded the fleet that won the war, and the price was Spartan recognition of Persia's claim to the Greek cities of [[Ionia]] in Asia Minor. The bill came due once Athens fell. Over the next two decades [[Persian Empire|Persia]] recovered through diplomacy and subsidy nearly everything it had lost at Marathon, Salamis, and Plataea, never occupying mainland Greece but reclaiming the Asiatic Greeks and ending as the arbiter of the entire Greek state system.

### The King's Peace (387/386 BCE)

Negotiated by the Spartan envoy [[Antalcidas]] and imposed by Artaxerxes II, the King's Peace was the first *koine eirene*, or common peace: a general settlement binding all of Greece, and the first ever dictated by an outside power.

| Term | Effect |
|------|--------|
| The Greek cities of Asia Minor and Cyprus belong to the King | The Asiatic Greeks formally surrendered to Persia, reversing the founding purpose of the [[Delian League]] |
| All other Greek cities, great and small, to be autonomous (Lemnos, Imbros, and Scyros excepted, left to Athens) | A clause used to dissolve rival leagues and alliances |
| The King will make war on any who refuse the peace | Persia becomes guarantor and enforcer of the settlement |
| Sparta administers the terms | Spartan hegemony legitimized under a Persian umbrella |

### The Economics of the Recovery

Persia's instrument was not its army but its treasury. The Achaemenid state taxed its provinces in silver and hoarded the proceeds, holding a reserve of purchasing power that a war-drained, capital-poor Greece could not match. In a world where fleets were wage bills and factions were for hire, stored surplus was itself a weapon.

| Lever | Mechanism | Detail |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Naval payroll | Whoever paid the rowers held the sea | Cyrus the Younger raised Spartan rowers' pay from 3 to 4 obols a day and handed [[Lysander]] 10,000 darics; Athenian crews deserted to the better wage (Plutarch, *Lysander*) |
| The strategic bribe | A subsidy redirected the whole balance of power | Timocrates of Rhodes spread 50 talents of silver among Thebes, Corinth, Argos, and Athens to ignite the Corinthian War in 395; the darics' archer emblem gave Agesilaus his "ten thousand archers" line (Xenophon; Plutarch, *Agesilaus*) |
| Rebuilding the Long Walls | Persia financed its own counterweight | Pharnabazus funded Conon's reconstruction of the Long Walls and Piraeus (394-391), wages paid in Persian coin, rebuilding the very walls Sparta had razed in 404 |
| Restored tribute | The peace returned a revenue coast | The King's Peace put the cities of Ionia back under Persian tribute; the autonomy clause kept mainland fiscal capacity fragmented and unpoolable |
| Military labor | Cash bought professional armies | The Ten Thousand (401) marched for roughly a daric a month each; the 4th-century mercenary boom monetized Greek warfare ([[Greek Coinage]]) |

At 25 Attic drachmas to the daric, Plutarch's 10,000 darics (about 42 talents) and Xenophon's 50 talents describe one payment of roughly 1,300 kg of silver: about a third of one percent of the 14,560 talents [[Herodotus]] reckoned as the empire's annual tribute, or near a tenth of what the Ionian district alone paid (the weight standards are set out in [[Greek Coinage]]). The reversal of Spartan policy in Greece cost the King a rounding error on his budget, while Athens and Sparta had spent themselves near to ruin winning and losing the same war.

### Significance

Persia never stationed a governor in mainland Greece, and Athens, Sparta, and Thebes remained self-governing. But after 386 BCE no Greek settlement carried legitimacy without the Great King's sanction, and Persian gold, not Persian armies, had become the decisive currency of Greek politics. Beaten in arms in 490 and 480-479 BCE, Persia recovered by exploiting Greek disunity what it could never hold by conquest. The arrangement framed the next half-century, through Spartan and then Theban hegemony after Leuctra (371 BCE), until [[Philip II of Macedon]] unified Greece at Chaeronea (338 BCE) and [[Alexander the Great]] destroyed the Achaemenid Empire (334-330 BCE), inverting the relationship for good.
