# Corinthian War

The Corinthian War (395-387 BCE) was the conflict in which a Persian-funded coalition of [[Thebes]], [[Athens]], [[Corinth]], and [[Argos]] checked the hegemony [[Sparta]] had won in the [[Peloponnesian War]] — and which ended by handing the Persian king the role of arbiter of Greece. It began with Persian gold: the satrap [[Pharnabazus]] sent [[Timocrates of Rhodes]] with 50 talents of silver to buy an anti-Spartan front and pull [[Agesilaus II]]'s army out of Asia Minor. [[Lysander]] died at Haliartus (395), Sparta won costly land battles at Nemea and Coronea (394) that settled nothing, and then lost its fleet — and command of the sea — to a Persian-funded squadron under the Athenian [[Conon]] at Cnidus (394). Persian money rebuilt Athens' Long Walls (393), and the Athenian [[Iphicrates]] showed at Lechaeum (390) that light-armed peltasts could destroy a Spartan hoplite regiment. But when a reviving Athens began to look like the greater threat, Persia switched its gold back to Sparta, and the war ended on the Great King's terms in the [[King's Peace]] (387/386). Persia had funded both sides in turn, and emerged the only winner.

## Background

After 404 Sparta governed Greece heavy-handedly, through garrisons and puppet oligarchies, and then carried the war to the Persian satrapies when Agesilaus II invaded Asia Minor (396-394). Rather than beat Agesilaus in the field, [[Persian Empire|Persia]] bought a war in his rear: Pharnabazus financed an anti-Spartan league. The bribe carried by Timocrates — 50 talents of silver (Xenophon) — found ready takers in Thebes, Corinth, Argos, and Athens, all chafing under Spartan dominance.

| 387/386 | King's Peace | Persia, now backing Sparta again, dictates the settlement |

## The Persian Pivot

The war's defining feature was Persia's willingness to switch the recipient of its gold to keep Greece divided and weak.

| Phase | Persian policy | Aim |
|-------|----------------|-----|
| 412-404 | Funded Sparta against Athens | Break Athenian power, recover Ionia |
| 395-394 | Funded the coalition against Sparta | Pull Agesilaus out of Asia |
| 387-386 | Switched back to Sparta | Check a reviving Athens; impose peace as arbiter |

## Economic Dimensions

| Lever | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| Subsidy | Persian darics financed fleets and politicians on whichever side served the King |
| Mercenary infantry | Cash sustained professional peltasts; Iphicrates' Lechaeum victory was a hired-light-infantry milestone (see [[Greek Coinage]]) |
| Naval payroll | Conon's Persian-funded fleet revived Athenian sea power, then rebuilt its walls |
| Restored tribute | The closing peace returned the Ionian cities to Persian tribute |

## Aftermath

The [[King's Peace]] of 387/386 ended the war by surrendering the Asiatic Greeks to Persia and declaring every other city autonomous — a clause Sparta used to break up rival leagues while keeping its own [[Peloponnesian League]] intact. Persia became the guarantor of the Greek state system. The settlement bought only a pause: Spartan abuses under the peace drove the rise of Thebes, whose victory at Leuctra (371) would end Spartan hegemony altogether.

## Significance

The Corinthian War showed that no Greek city could master Greece without the Great King's money, and that the decisive theater had shifted from the battlefield to the treasury. It also marked a military turning point: the success of Iphicrates' peltasts foreshadowed the combined-arms armies that [[Philip II of Macedon]] would perfect. Above all it set up the King's Peace, the first Common Peace and the formal beginning of Persian arbitration of Greece.
