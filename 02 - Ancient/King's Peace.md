# King's Peace

The King's Peace (387/386 BCE), also called the Peace of Antalcidas, was the settlement that ended the [[Corinthian War]] and made the Persian king the arbiter of the Greek state system. Negotiated by the Spartan [[Antalcidas]] at Susa and dictated by [[Artaxerxes II]], it surrendered the Greek cities of Asia Minor to [[Persian Empire|Persia]], declared every other Greek city autonomous — a clause that dissolved rival leagues while sparing [[Sparta]]'s own [[Peloponnesian League]] — and made the Great King the guarantor who would make war on any city that refused. It was the first *koine eirene*, or Common Peace, a template revived repeatedly over the next half-century, and the clearest sign that accumulated wealth, not battlefield power, had become the decisive currency of Greek politics. It held only as long as Persia and Sparta wished: its abuses fed the rise of [[Thebes]] and the end of Spartan supremacy at Leuctra (371).

## Background

The Corinthian War had reached deadlock: Sparta held the advantage on land but had lost the sea at Cnidus (394), while a Persian-funded [[Conon]] rebuilt Athens' fleet and Long Walls. When a reviving Athens — aiding the rebel Evagoras of Cyprus and reaching back toward the Hellespont grain route — began to look like the greater danger, Persia switched its support back to Sparta. Antalcidas secured the King's backing, and Spartan command of the seas and the grain lanes forced the exhausted combatants to terms.

## The Terms

| Term | Effect |
|------|--------|
| The Greek cities of Asia Minor, with Clazomenae and Cyprus, belong to the King | The Asiatic Greeks surrendered to Persia, reversing the aim of the [[Delian League]] |
| All other Greek cities, great and small, to be autonomous | Leagues and federations to be dissolved |
| Lemnos, Imbros, and Scyros remain Athenian | A concession protecting Athens' grain route |
| The King will make war on any who reject the peace | Persia as guarantor and enforcer |

The autonomy clause was the instrument that mattered. Framed as freedom, it worked as a solvent: it broke up the Boeotian federation and the union of Corinth and Argos, yet Sparta's own Peloponnesian League was exempted, leaving Sparta the only organized power in Greece. Autonomy became a hegemon's tool, invoked selectively.

## Economics

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Restored tribute | Ionia and the Asiatic coast returned to Persian tribute — a revenue coast recovered without a campaign |
| Cheap arbitration | Persia bought the outcome with subsidy, not armies (the figures are worked out in [[Peloponnesian War]] and [[Greek Coinage]]) |
| Fragmented rivals | Dissolving leagues kept Greek fiscal and military capacity unpoolable |

## Legacy

| Consequence | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| Persian arbitration | For half a century no Greek settlement was legitimate without the King's sanction |
| The Common Peace | The *koine eirene* model was revived in 375, 371, and after — rarely with more success |
| Spartan overreach | Sparta abused the peace, seizing Thebes' citadel in 382, provoking the backlash that produced Theban hegemony |
| End of the system | Persian arbitration lasted until [[Philip II of Macedon]] unified Greece (338) and [[Alexander the Great]] conquered Persia (334-330) |

## Significance

The King's Peace is the clearest ancient case of a financial power dictating a political order it could not occupy. Persia placed no governor in mainland Greece, yet set the terms for every Greek city, because it held the surplus that Greece, after decades of war, did not. It is the endpoint of the lesson the Peloponnesian War taught — external funding decides — and a precedent for two recurring patterns: arbitration by wealth rather than arms, and autonomy as a tool of hegemony, invoked wherever a creditor or reserve-currency power shapes the politics of those who depend on it.
