# Why China Didn't Industrialize

The world's most advanced economy that didn't make the leap. China had technology but not institutions that rewarded its application to production—the Great Divergence was institutional, not technological. This question dominates economic history debates: why did Britain industrialize first when China had printing, compass, gunpowder, and iron-casting centuries earlier?

## The Deeper Point

China had technology but not [[Institutions and Progress|institutions that rewarded]] its application to production—the [[Great Divergence]] was institutional, not technological. [[Song Dynasty|Song China]] invented printing, compass, gunpowder, and advanced iron production. But inventions didn't compound into industrialization because merchants remained subordinate, property was insecure, corporate forms didn't exist, and the examination system channeled talent toward bureaucracy. Britain industrialized not because of superior inventions but because its institutions—secure property, merchant influence, corporate form, patent protection, competitive markets—rewarded applying technology to production. The Pomeranz-Allen debate (similar until 1750 vs. different earlier) matters less than the institutional question: why did similar technologies produce different trajectories? The answer lies in [[Qing China Economic Model|how institutions channeled]] entrepreneurial ambition. In China, the ambitious became scholars and officials; in Britain, some became inventors and industrialists. [[Innovation Economics|Innovation requires not just inventions]] but institutions that make innovating profitable. China's experience shows that technology alone doesn't produce progress—[[Business Firm|institutions]] determine whether potential becomes reality.
