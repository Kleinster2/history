# Standardization

Standardization — agreeing on common technical specifications so that separate systems interoperate — is one of the quiet engines of economic progress and a recurring arena of power. It converts incompatible local arrangements into networks, unlocking scale, trade, and the division of labor: interchangeable parts made mass production possible, common railway gauges knit national markets, and the shipping container underwrote late-twentieth-century globalization. But which standard wins is often decided by early, contingent choices that then lock in for a century (path dependence), and the power to set the standard has been a tool of states and empires since the first international standards body, the **International Telegraph Union** of 1865. The 2020s fights over internet architecture and AI-agent protocols are the newest chapter of a very old story.

## Why standardization drives progress

- **Interoperability and network effects.** A shared standard lets separate systems connect, and the value of belonging rises with every additional adopter. This is what turns a patchwork of local solutions into a single market.
- **Division of labor and scale.** Interchangeable parts — pioneered in armory production and generalized as the "American System of Manufactures" in the nineteenth century — let unskilled labor assemble complex goods, the precondition for mass production. The container did the same for freight: a standard box that any ship, crane, truck, and railcar could handle collapsed handling costs and made global supply chains viable.
- **Lower transaction costs.** A standard is an institution in [[Douglass North]]'s sense: it reduces the cost of exchange by removing the need to negotiate compatibility every time. Standardization is therefore part of the institutional story of why some economies grew ([[Institutions and Progress]]).

## The recurring pattern: standards wars and lock-in

| Case | Era | The contest | Resolution / lock-in |
|------|-----|-------------|----------------------|
| Railway gauge | 1830s–1880s | Brunel's 7-ft broad gauge vs Stephenson's 4-ft 8½-in | Britain's Gauge Act (1846) fixed 4'8½"; US had ~9 gauge regions by the 1860s, consolidated in the Southern railroads' ~2-day conversion of thousands of miles in 1886 |
| Telegraph | 1865 | Incompatible national telegraph systems | International Telegraph Union (Paris, May 17 1865) — the first intergovernmental standards body, ancestor of today's ITU |
| Weights and measures | 1795–1875 | Metric vs imperial and local units | Metric system (France, 1795); the Metre Convention (1875) made it an international treaty standard |
| Time | 1883–1884 | Local solar time vs unified "railway time" | US standard time zones (1883); the Prime Meridian Conference (1884) set Greenwich as the global reference |
| Electric current | 1880s–1890s | Edison's DC vs Westinghouse/Tesla's AC | AC won the "War of the Currents"; electrical units (volt, ohm, ampere) standardized internationally |
| Shipping container | 1950s–1970s | Competing box dimensions | ISO standard sizes (ISO 668/6346) after Malcom McLean's 1956 first voyage — the enabler of modern globalization |
| Keyboard / video | 20th c. | QWERTY; VHS vs Betamax | Textbook path-dependence cases — the surviving standard was not obviously the best |

## Path dependence: why the best standard does not always win

The deepest lesson of standardization history is that outcomes are sticky and not always optimal. Economists Paul David ("Clio and the Economics of QWERTY," 1985) and W. Brian Arthur (increasing returns) argued that early, sometimes accidental choices lock in through network effects and switching costs, surviving long after better alternatives appear. Douglas Puffert's study of railway gauge found that idiosyncratic early decisions "affected choices of gauge ever since, despite changing technology and understandings of what gauge is optimal." The implication runs straight to the present: standard-setting is high-stakes precisely because it is so hard to reverse — the moment of choice carries decades of consequence.

## Standardization as power

Standards travel with empire and trade, and setting them confers durable, low-visibility advantage:

- British railway and engineering practice spread through the empire; the metric system spread with the French Revolution and Napoleonic conquest; the American System of interchangeable parts became a model exported through US industry.
- The **International Telegraph Union** (1865) was the first instance of states collectively regulating a strategic network — the template for standards bodies (later ISO, IEC, and the modern ITU) as arenas of national rivalry rather than neutral technical forums.
- The enduring distinction is between **de jure** standards (set by treaty or standards body) and **de facto** standards (set by market dominance). Both are levers of advantage, and great powers have always reached for whichever they could control.

## Throughline

Standardization is a hinge of the [[Great Divergence]] and of globalization. Interchangeable parts underwrote industrial mass production; common gauges and the telegraph knit national markets; the container underwrote the late-twentieth-century trade boom. Each leap in interoperability was also a contest over whose specification became everyone else's — and the winner captured rents, set the terms others built on, and embedded its own assumptions into the system. The contemporary fight over digital and AI-agent standards is the same dynamic operating at a new layer of the economy.
