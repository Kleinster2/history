# Leisure, Work, and Human Flourishing

How much did people work? How much time for rest, family, culture? The question matters because work hours reveal institutional bargains—who captured productivity gains. Pre-industrial workers had more holidays than Victorian factory workers. The eight-hour day was won through struggle, not given. Modern productivity could enable far more leisure than we take. What we do with time freed from subsistence is, ultimately, what progress is for.

## The Deeper Point

Work hours reveal [[Institutions and Progress|institutional]] bargains: who has power to capture productivity gains. Pre-industrial workers had more holidays and more control over their time than Victorian factory workers—industrialization initially *worsened* leisure before eventually expanding it. The eight-hour day wasn't a gift of progress; it was won through organized struggle against employers who preferred 12-hour days. The reduction in hours from 1870 to 1970 represents labor gaining power to convert productivity into leisure rather than pure income. Since then, patterns diverged: Europeans chose more leisure (shorter hours, longer vacations), Americans chose more income. Both are valid responses to abundance. The deeper question: what is progress for? If it only produces more goods without more time to enjoy them, have we really progressed? Keynes imagined that his grandchildren would work 15-hour weeks and spend the rest on art and friendship. Instead, we work similar hours and spend them on screens. The institutional choice—how to allocate productivity gains—remains open. What [[Material Living Standards Through History|living standards]] ultimately mean isn't just consumption but time: time for rest, family, culture, community. That's what human flourishing requires, and whether institutions enable it is the final test.
