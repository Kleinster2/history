# Woolen Cloth Trade

Woolen cloth was medieval Europe's most important manufactured export — the commodity that drove the first international supply chains, connecting English sheep pastures to Flemish looms to Florentine finishing workshops to Mediterranean markets. While the [[Spice Trade]] moved luxury goods from East to West, the cloth trade moved manufactured goods from North to South — and in doing so created proto-industrial production systems, guild-regulated quality control, and the financial infrastructure that funded the Renaissance. [[Florence]]'s Arte della Lana employed over 30,000 workers; the Medici financed wool merchants before they became bankers. Cloth, not spices, was medieval Europe's homegrown engine of economic progress.

## Overview

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Period | ~1100-1500 (peak); continues in altered form after |
| Raw material | English wool (highest quality), also Spanish, Scottish |
| Weaving centers | Flanders (Bruges, Ghent, Ypres) |
| Finishing centers | [[Florence]] (dyeing, fulling, napping) |
| Markets | Mediterranean, Levant, Northern Europe |
| Employment | Florence's wool guild: ~30,000 workers (~30% of city population) |
| Trade infrastructure | [[Champagne Fairs]], Venetian galleys to Bruges, Hanseatic shipping |

## The Supply Chain

The woolen cloth trade was a genuinely international production system — raw material, processing, finishing, and sale each occurred in different regions, linked by merchant networks and credit.

### Stage by Stage

| Stage | Location | Activity | Controlled By |
|-------|----------|----------|---------------|
| 1. Raw wool production | England (Cotswolds, Yorkshire, Welsh Marches) | Sheep farming, shearing, baling | Monasteries (Cistercians), landlords |
| 2. Export | English ports → Staple town (Calais after 1363) | Quality inspection, customs duty | Company of the Staple; English Crown taxed |
| 3. Rough weaving | Flanders (Bruges, Ghent, Ypres) | Carding, spinning, weaving into rough cloth | Flemish weavers' guilds |
| 4. Finishing | Florence | Dyeing, fulling, napping, shearing | Arte della Lana (wool guild), Arte di Calimala (cloth finishers) |
| 5. Distribution | Mediterranean, Levant, Northern Europe | Sale of finished luxury cloth | Italian merchant networks, [[Champagne Fairs]], Venetian galleys |

### Why This Division of Labor?

| Specialization | Reason |
|----------------|--------|
| England grew the best wool | Climate, sheep breeds (Cistercian monasteries improved breeding), pasture quality |
| Flanders wove it | Urban concentration of skilled weavers; proximity to English wool supply; guild tradition |
| Florence finished it | Chemical expertise in dyeing (access to alum, oriental dyes via [[Levant Trade]]); labor force; capital |
| Each stage added value | Raw wool → rough cloth → finished luxury textile: each transformation multiplied price |

## The Three Centers

### England: Raw Wool Supply

For the English side — the [[Wool Trade|Staple system]], export duties, and the shift from raw wool to cloth exports — see the dedicated note.

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Annual export (peak) | ~30,000 sacks/year (~1280); declining thereafter |
| Best breeds | Cotswold (long-staple), Ryland, Lincoln |
| Producers | Cistercian monasteries were largest commercial producers |
| Crown revenue | Wool customs financed Hundred Years' War |
| Shift | By 1400s, England increasingly exported finished cloth rather than raw wool |

### Flanders: Weaving Center

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Key cities | Bruges, Ghent, Ypres — the "Big Three" |
| Population | Ghent: ~65,000 (1300); Bruges: ~46,000; Ypres: ~28,000 |
| Urbanization | Flanders was the most urbanized region in northern Europe |
| Organization | Guild-regulated production; strict quality standards |
| Raw material dependency | Almost entirely dependent on imported English wool |
| Products | Medium-to-high quality broadcloth |
| Markets | Sold at [[Champagne Fairs]], then via Italian galley routes |

#### Why Flanders Declined

| Factor | Period | Effect |
|--------|--------|--------|
| English cloth production grew | 14th-15th c. | England kept more wool for domestic weaving |
| Raw material squeeze | Post-1350 | English wool exports declined; Flemish looms went idle |
| Labor conflicts | 1280s-1380s | Weavers' revolts in Bruges, Ghent, Ypres |
| Hundred Years' War | 1337-1453 | Disrupted trade routes and political alliances |
| Italian sea route | 1270s+ | Venetian galleys sailed directly to Bruges, bypassing [[Champagne Fairs]] |
| Competition | 15th c. | Brabant (Brussels, Leuven), Holland, and England captured market share |

### Florence: Finishing and Finance

Florence's genius was not weaving raw wool but *finishing* rough cloth — turning a middling textile into a luxury product through dyeing, fulling, and napping.

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Two guilds | **Arte di Calimala**: imported and refinished foreign cloth. **Arte della Lana**: manufactured cloth from raw wool |
| Employment | Arte della Lana: 30,000+ workers in ~200 workshops |
| Process | Imported rough cloth or raw wool → 26 separate finishing steps → luxury product |
| Dyes | Scarlet (kermes), blue (woad, then indigo via Levant), other oriental dyes |
| Alum | Essential mordant (fixative) for dyes; imported from Asia Minor, later Tolfa (Papal States) |
| Value added | Finished Florentine cloth sold at 3-5x the price of rough Flemish cloth |
| Scale | Florence produced ~100,000 bolts of cloth per year at peak (early 14th century) |

#### The Ciompi Revolt (1378)

The wool industry's labor conditions generated medieval Europe's most significant workers' revolt:

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Who | The *Ciompi* — unskilled wool workers (carders, combers, washers) |
| Grievance | No guild representation; lowest wages; no political voice |
| Demand | Recognition as a guild with political rights |
| Outcome | Temporary success — Ciompi guild briefly recognized |
| Result | Crushed within months; oligarchy restored; guild disbanded |
| Significance | Earliest large-scale labor revolt in European industrial history |

## The Economics

### Value Chain

| Stage | Approximate Value Multiplier |
|-------|------------------------------|
| Raw English wool (per sack) | 1x (base) |
| Rough Flemish cloth | 2-3x |
| Finished Florentine cloth | 6-15x |
| Final retail in Mediterranean | 10-20x |

The margins were large enough to sustain an entire chain of intermediaries, each adding value or extracting rent.

### Financing the Trade

| Mechanism | Provider | Function |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| Advance purchases | Italian merchants → English monasteries | Bought wool before shearing; de facto lending against future production |
| Bills of exchange | Florentine bankers | Financed shipments between stages; avoided moving coin |
| Partnership contracts | Italian merchant houses | Shared risk across the supply chain |
| Bardi/Peruzzi loans | Florentine banks → English Crown | Massive loans secured against wool customs; both banks collapsed when Edward III defaulted (1340s) |
| Fair credit | [[Champagne Fairs]] clearing system | Debts netted across parties at fair settling periods |

The wool trade and banking were inseparable. Florence's banking revolution grew directly from the need to finance international cloth transactions. The Medici Bank's earliest business lines included wool trade finance.

### Putting-Out System

The cloth trade pioneered the putting-out system — proto-capitalist production organized by merchant capitalists:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Merchant provides | Raw materials (wool, dyes) to workers |
| Workers perform | Specific tasks (spinning, weaving, dyeing) at home or workshop |
| Merchant collects | Finished product, pays piece rates |
| Merchant sells | At distant markets for profit |
| Control | Merchant controls capital, raw material supply, and market access |
| Workers | Dependent on merchant for materials and wages; no capital of their own |

This was the organizational form that the [[Industrial Revolution]] would later mechanize.

## Trade Routes

### North-South Flow

| Route | Goods Moving | Period |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| England → Flanders (sea) | Raw wool | 12th-15th c. |
| Flanders → Florence (overland via Champagne) | Rough cloth | 12th-early 14th c. |
| Flanders → Florence (sea via Venice/Genoa) | Rough cloth | Late 13th c.+ (replaced Champagne route) |
| Florence → Mediterranean markets | Finished luxury cloth | 13th-15th c. |
| [[Hanseatic League]] routes (Baltic → Flanders) | Flemish cloth eastward; raw materials westward | 13th-15th c. |

### The Champagne Fairs Connection

The [[Champagne Fairs]] existed primarily to exchange northern cloth for southern goods:

| Direction | Goods |
|-----------|-------|
| Northward (from Italy) | Spices, silk, dyes, alum, luxury goods |
| Southward (from Flanders) | Woolen cloth — the fairs' primary northern commodity |

When Italian galleys began sailing directly to Bruges (1270s+), the Champagne Fairs lost their raison d'etre and declined.

## The Deeper Point

The woolen cloth trade was medieval Europe's closest approximation to industrial production. It had international supply chains, division of labor across regions, quality-controlled manufacturing, and a merchant-capitalist class that organized production without owning the means of production. The putting-out system — merchant provides materials, workers process them, merchant sells the product — was the organizational template that the Industrial Revolution would later mechanize with power looms and factories.

The trade also demonstrates how comparative advantage operated in medieval commerce. England had the wool; Flanders had the weavers; Florence had the finishing expertise and the capital. No single region could produce the final luxury product alone. The supply chain existed because each stage required different inputs — natural resources, skilled labor, chemical knowledge, financial capital — that were geographically distributed.

The connection between wool and banking is essential. Florence's banking houses — the Bardi, Peruzzi, and later the Medici — grew out of the need to finance wool transactions across borders and currencies. The bills of exchange, letters of credit, and partnership contracts that defined medieval finance were developed to solve practical problems in the cloth trade. When the Bardi and Peruzzi collapsed in the 1340s, it was because they had lent Edward III enormous sums secured against English wool customs — and the king defaulted. The financial crisis that followed was, at its root, a wool-trade crisis.

The Ciompi revolt reveals the social cost of proto-industrial production. Florence's 30,000 wool workers were a proletariat in all but name — dependent on merchant-capitalists for raw materials and wages, denied guild representation, and subject to wage suppression. Their revolt and its suppression prefigure later industrial labor conflicts by four centuries.
