# Chinese Diaspora Capitalism

How overseas Chinese networks created parallel institutional infrastructure across Southeast Asia. The "bamboo network" demonstrates that effective economic institutions can emerge from trust networks rather than formal legal systems—explaining why ethnic Chinese dominate Southeast Asian business despite being minorities.

## The Deeper Point

Chinese diaspora capitalism demonstrates that [[Institutions and Progress|economic institutions]] can emerge from social networks when formal institutions are weak or exclusionary. The trust-based credit, reputation enforcement, and information-sharing of dialect networks substituted for the courts, banks, and contracts that Western capitalism relies on.

This has profound implications for development theory. The standard prescription—build formal institutions—assumes institutions must be state-created. The Chinese diaspora shows an alternative: institutions can be network-based, informal, and effective. Overseas Chinese achieved economic dominance in societies with weak formal institutions precisely because their networks provided the institutional infrastructure that governments couldn't or wouldn't supply.

But network-based institutions have limits. They work within the network but create barriers for outsiders. They enable trust among Chinese but can't incorporate indigenous populations. They're effective for commerce but struggle with large-scale industry requiring formal contracts and outside investment.

The evolution toward formalization—listed companies, professional management, governance reforms—reflects these limits. As firms grow, family networks can't provide sufficient capital or expertise. The transition from network capitalism to formal capitalism is underway but incomplete. Whether ethnic Chinese business can fully formalize while maintaining the trust advantages that made them successful is the open question.
