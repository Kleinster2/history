# Creative Destruction

Schumpeter's "perennial gale of creative destruction" is capitalism's defining process: new products, methods, and organizations displace old ones, destroying incumbent wealth while creating new value. But creative destruction isn't unique to capitalism—it's visible whenever a society permits entry and competition. The question every economy faces is whether to allow it: creative destruction drives progress but creates losers, and losers fight back through politics, guilds, cartels, and regulation. The institutional choice to permit or block creative destruction may be the single most important determinant of long-run economic performance.

## Overview

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Definition | New innovations displacing established ones |
| Origin | Schumpeter (1942), drawing on Marx |
| Mechanism | Temporary monopoly → imitation → new disruption |
| Requirement | Open entry, competition, failure permitted |
| Result | Rising productivity, continuous instability |

## Schumpeter's Framework

### The Process

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Entrepreneur sees opportunity | New product, method, market, source, organization |
| Temporary monopoly | Innovator earns excess profit |
| Imitation | Others copy, compete away profits |
| New entrepreneur | Sees next opportunity |
| Cycle repeats | "Perennial gale" |

### Five Types of Innovation

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| New product | Automobile displacing horse |
| New production method | [[Mass Production\|Assembly line]] displacing craft |
| New market | [[Age of Exploration\|Colonial trade]] opening |
| New supply source | [[Oil Economy\|Petroleum]] replacing whale oil |
| New organization | [[Corporate Evolution\|Corporation]] displacing partnership |

### Not Invention—Commercialization

| Distinction | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| Invention | Creating something new |
| Innovation | Making it commercially viable |
| Entrepreneur | Person who commercializes |
| Example | Watt didn't invent steam—he made it work commercially |
| Implication | Institutions matter more than genius |

## When Societies Block Creative Destruction

### The Incumbents' Toolkit

| Method | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Guild restrictions | Medieval craft guilds blocking entry |
| Patents of monopoly | Crown-granted exclusive rights |
| Regulatory capture | Incumbents writing rules |
| Cartels | Price-fixing to prevent competition |
| Political lobbying | Protect existing industries |
| Violence | Luddites, union resistance |

### Societies That Blocked It

| Society | Mechanism | Result |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Ming China | Banned maritime trade | Fell behind Europe |
| [[Ottoman Economy]] | Guild system, state control | Missed industrialization |
| Spanish Empire | Monopoly, guild protection | Dutch/British overtook |
| Soviet Union | Central planning, no entry | Stagnation after catch-up |
| Pre-Meiji Japan | Tokugawa restrictions | 250 years of stasis |

### Societies That Permitted It

| Society | Mechanism | Result |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Classical Athens | Open to metics, trade | Commercial flourishing |
| [[Dutch Golden Age]] | Low barriers, tolerance | Golden age of commerce |
| Industrial Britain | Patent reform, open entry | [[Industrial Revolution]] |
| Post-Civil War US | Minimal regulation | [[Gilded Age]] dynamism |
| Modern US | Bankruptcy tolerance, VC | Silicon Valley |

## The Political Economy of Destruction

### Winners and Losers

| Group | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| Entrepreneurs | For (they disrupt) |
| Consumers | For (better/cheaper products) |
| Incumbent firms | Against (they're displaced) |
| Incumbent workers | Against (jobs destroyed) |
| Politicians | Ambivalent (growth vs. stability) |

### The Mancur Olson Problem

| Insight | Implication |
|---------|-------------|
| Losers are concentrated | Easy to organize |
| Winners are diffuse | Hard to organize |
| Losers lobby effectively | Block disruption |
| Over time | Sclerosis accumulates |
| Olson's thesis | Stable societies calcify |

### Baumol's Channel

| Direction | Character |
|-----------|-----------|
| Productive | Innovation creates new value |
| Unproductive | Rent-seeking protects old value |
| Destructive | Crime, corruption, warlordism |
| Key insight | Same talent, different channels |
| Institutional role | Rules determine which direction |

## Creative Destruction and Growth

### The Evidence

| Finding | Source |
|---------|--------|
| Firm turnover correlates with growth | OECD data |
| Entry rates predict innovation | Aghion et al. |
| Incumbents rarely self-disrupt | Christensen |
| Monopoly reduces innovation | Except Schumpeterian "Mark II" |
| Bankruptcy tolerance enables risk | US vs. Europe comparison |

### Schumpeter Mark I vs. Mark II

| Model | Character |
|-------|-----------|
| Mark I (early Schumpeter) | Individual entrepreneur disrupts |
| Mark II (later Schumpeter) | Large firms with R&D labs innovate |
| Tension | Does competition or scale drive innovation? |
| Evidence | Both, depending on industry |
| Resolution | Competition to enter; scale to develop |

### The Innovator's Dilemma (Christensen, 1997)

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| Sustaining innovation | Improving existing products |
| Disruptive innovation | New, initially worse products that improve |
| Incumbent response | Can't abandon existing customers |
| Result | New entrants capture new markets |
| Examples | Disk drives, steel minimills, digital photography |

## The Deeper Point

Creative destruction is the operational test of [[Institutions and Progress|institutional]] quality—the question every society answers, explicitly or implicitly, every day: will you let the new displace the old? Societies that say yes get growth but instability; societies that say no get stability but stagnation. There is no third option. The [[Guild Economics|guilds]] that blocked entry preserved craft quality and worker livelihoods—and ensured that industrialization happened in Britain (which broke them) rather than France or Germany (which didn't, until later). The [[Ottoman Economy|Ottoman]] state that protected established trades maintained social order for centuries—and fell behind a Europe that tolerated the chaos of [[Entrepreneurship|entrepreneurial]] entry. The deeper insight is that creative destruction isn't just an economic process—it's a *political* choice disguised as a market outcome. Every patent, every regulation, every licensing requirement, every zoning law is a decision about how much destruction to permit. The societies that grew fastest—[[Dutch Golden Age|Dutch Republic]], Industrial Britain, [[Gilded Age|Gilded Age]] America, postwar Japan—all found temporary institutional settlements that permitted enough destruction to drive [[Innovation Economics|innovation]] while providing enough stability to maintain political legitimacy. But these settlements are inherently unstable: today's disruptors become tomorrow's incumbents lobbying to pull up the ladder. The "perennial gale" is perennial only where institutions prevent its victims from extinguishing it—and that requires continuous institutional renewal, not a one-time constitutional settlement.
