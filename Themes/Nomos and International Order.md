# Nomos and International Order

**Nomos and International Order** traces [[Carl Schmitt]]'s framework for understanding how spatial ordering -- the division and appropriation of land -- created the legal, economic, and political foundations of international life. The core claim: law does not precede space; space precedes law. Every legal and property system begins with an act of land appropriation (Landnahme) that establishes who owns what, who rules whom, and under what rules conflict is conducted. This connects directly to the vault's throughline: property rights and spatial ordering are preconditions for trade, accumulation, and growth. Without a settled nomos -- a recognized division of territory -- there is no stable commerce, no enforceable contracts, no capital formation.

Schmitt developed this framework primarily in *Der Nomos der Erde im Volkerrecht des Jus Publicum Europaeum* (1950) and *Land und Meer* (1942), though the ideas pervade his earlier work on sovereignty and the Grossraum. The framework is historical, not purely theoretical: it periodizes international order from the late medieval period through the post-1945 American-dominated system, asking at each stage what spatial structure made law possible and what happened when that structure collapsed.

## The Concept of Nomos

Schmitt recovers "nomos" from its Greek root (nemein: to divide, to pasture, to distribute), arguing that the word originally meant the foundational act of spatial ordering -- not "law" in the abstract but the concrete division of land that creates law. Three acts constitute the original nomos:

| Act | German | Function |
|-----|--------|----------|
| Appropriation | Nahme | Taking land, establishing ownership |
| Division | Teilung | Distributing property among members |
| Production | Weidung | Using the land, generating economic value |

This sequence -- take, divide, produce -- is the precondition for everything the vault covers. No [[Economic Persistence|persistent economic activity]] without recognized property. No [[Trade Networks|trade]] without a spatial order that makes contracts enforceable. No [[State Capacity|state capacity]] without territorial sovereignty. The nomos is not a theory about law; it is a theory about how material order comes into being.

Schmitt contrasts this with legal positivism, which treats law as a system of norms detached from spatial reality. For Schmitt, a norm without a spatial anchor is empty: the question is always "whose territory, whose rules?" This is why, in his view, universal legal systems (the [[League of Nations]], the [[United Nations]]) that claim authority without controlling territory are structurally unstable -- they lack a nomos.

## The Jus Publicum Europaeum (1648-1914)

The European public law system that emerged from the [[Peace of Westphalia]] (1648) represents, for Schmitt, the most successful nomos in modern history. Its achievement was not abolishing war but limiting it -- making war a regulated institution between recognized sovereign states rather than a moral crusade or existential struggle.

### The Bracketing of War (Hegung des Krieges)

The central innovation: war as a duel between equals.

| Feature | Pre-Westphalian War | Jus Publicum Europaeum |
|---------|---------------------|------------------------|
| Legal status of war | Just war doctrine -- one side is right, the other sinful | Both belligerents are legitimate (justus hostis) |
| Combatants | Irregular -- mercenaries, civilians, religious militias | Uniformed state armies |
| Civilian distinction | Blurred | Clear -- civilians are non-combatants |
| War termination | Annihilation or submission | Peace treaty between sovereign equals |
| War aims | Destruction of evil / conversion / total claim | Limited -- territorial adjustment, trade concessions |
| Legal framework | Theological -- just cause required | Formal -- declaration of war, rules of engagement |

This bracketing did not make war humane -- it made war limited. A state could fight another state without seeking to destroy its political existence. Wars ended in treaties, not exterminations. Defeated states retained sovereignty. This predictability enabled the economic expansion of the 18th and 19th centuries: merchants, financiers, and insurers could calculate risk because the rules of conflict were known. [[War Finance Innovations|War finance]] itself became possible because wars had foreseeable endpoints and costs.

The system depended on a shared framework: European states recognized each other as sovereign equals (at least in legal theory), fought with professional armies, and accepted that war was a regulated instrument of policy rather than a moral imperative. This is the world [[Clausewitz]] described -- war as the continuation of politics by other means -- and it was a specific historical achievement, not a universal condition.

### The Spatial Division: European Soil vs. "Beyond the Line"

The jus publicum Europaeum rested on a geographic partition that made its internal restraint possible:

- Within Europe: sovereign states, regulated war, diplomatic recognition, the sanctity of treaties
- Outside Europe: open territory for colonization, conquest, and exploitation -- no rules applied

The "amity lines" (the earliest dating to the Treaty of Cateau-Cambresis, 1559) drew a literal boundary: conflicts between European powers in the colonial world did not count as breaches of European peace. France and Spain could fight in the Caribbean while remaining at peace in Europe. England and the [[Dutch Golden Age|Dutch Republic]] could slaughter each other in the East Indies without triggering a continental war.

This spatial logic served an economic function. European colonial expansion -- the land appropriation that built the Atlantic economy, funded the [[Fiscal State Emergence|fiscal-military states]], and generated the capital flows behind the [[Great Divergence]] -- was legally sanctioned precisely because it occurred "beyond the line." The violence of colonialism was not a failure of the system; it was the system's enabling condition. The restraint within Europe was purchased by the absence of restraint everywhere else.

### The Concert of Europe as Institutional Expression

After the Napoleonic Wars, the Congress of Vienna (1815) and the Concert of Europe formalized what had been implicit: a small group of great powers (Britain, France, Austria, Prussia, Russia) managed the European spatial order through periodic congresses. This was not a world government but a cartel of sovereigns -- each maintaining its sphere, collectively preventing any single power from overturning the balance. The Concert preserved the bracketing of war for another century, though the Crimean War (1853-56), the wars of German unification (1864-71), and colonial scrambles tested its limits.

Schmitt sees the Concert as the institutional peak of the jus publicum Europaeum: a spatial order with recognized custodians. Its weakness was its exclusivity -- it governed Europe and the European colonial world but could not accommodate rising non-European powers or the ideological pressures that would blow the system apart in 1914.

## The New World and the First Global Nomos

The discovery of the Americas created the conditions for the first genuinely global spatial order. Before 1492, the European nomos was essentially Mediterranean -- a regional system with limited reach.

### The Papal Donation and the Treaty of Tordesillas

The initial response to the New World was a medieval one: [[Pope Alexander VI]]'s Inter Caetera bull (1493) divided the non-Christian world between [[Spain]] and [[Portugal]], drawing a line 100 leagues west of the Cape Verde Islands. The Treaty of Tordesillas (June 7, 1494) adjusted this line to 370 leagues west, giving Portugal what would become [[Brazil]] and the eastern route to Asia.

This was the last great act of medieval spatial ordering -- a pope distributing territory on the basis of theological authority. It reflected a world where spiritual and temporal power were still intertwined, where the right to appropriate land derived from a divine mandate to Christianize. The Tordesillas line was the first attempt to impose a nomos on the entire globe, but it depended on a papal authority that other European powers (England, France, the Dutch Republic) would soon reject.

### From Mediterranean to Atlantic

The shift from Mediterranean to Atlantic trade routes was a spatial revolution with economic consequences that the vault tracks extensively: the decline of [[Venice]] and the Italian city-states, the rise of [[Iberian Peninsula|Iberian]] and then northern European maritime empires, the creation of the Atlantic triangular trade, the emergence of plantation economies, the flow of New World silver through Spain to the rest of Europe and ultimately to China.

Schmitt reads this transition not as simple geographic expansion but as a transformation of the nomos itself. The Mediterranean world was a sea surrounded by land -- a bounded space. The Atlantic opened an unbounded horizon, creating new categories of territory (colonies, trading posts, slave markets) that did not fit the old spatial concepts. The legal and political frameworks that emerged to manage this new space -- the [[Chartered Company Model|chartered companies]], the mercantilist system, the plantation complex -- were improvisations born of a spatial revolution that outran existing law.

## The Collapse of the Jus Publicum Europaeum

Schmitt's periodization of collapse runs roughly from 1914 to 1945, with the critical turning points concentrated in the interwar period.

### World War I: The End of Limited War

The [[World War I|First World War]] shattered the bracketing of war. The scale of mobilization (mass conscription, industrial warfare, total economic commitment), the breakdown of the combatant/civilian distinction (blockades, unrestricted submarine warfare, bombing), and the moral language of the conflict (a "war to end all wars," a crusade for democracy) all violated the principles of the jus publicum Europaeum. War was no longer a regulated duel between sovereign equals; it was a struggle between good and evil.

### The League of Nations: Universalizing European Law

The [[League of Nations]] (1920) attempted to extend the European system globally, but in doing so it transformed it. The League claimed universal jurisdiction and declared aggressive war illegal -- a move that, in Schmitt's analysis, destroyed the essential structure of the old system without replacing it. The jus publicum Europaeum had worked because it accepted war as a legitimate institution and regulated it; the League tried to abolish war as a legal category while lacking the power to actually prevent it.

### The Criminalization of War

The critical steps in Schmitt's periodization:

| Date | Event | Schmitt's Reading |
|------|-------|-------------------|
| 1919 | Versailles Treaty, "war guilt" clause | War becomes a moral crime, not a policy instrument |
| 1920 | League of Nations Covenant | Aggression redefined as illegal |
| 1928 | Kellogg-Briand Pact | War formally outlawed |
| 1945 | UN Charter | Use of force prohibited except in self-defense or Security Council authorization |
| 1945-46 | Nuremberg Trials | "Crimes against peace" -- waging aggressive war is a personal criminal offense |

Schmitt's argument is counterintuitive but structurally coherent: making war illegal paradoxically makes it more total. Under the jus publicum Europaeum, both sides in a war were recognized as legitimate combatants (justus hostis). The enemy was an adversary, not a criminal. This recognition enabled limited war aims, prisoner-of-war protections, and peace treaties between equals.

When the enemy becomes a criminal rather than a legitimate combatant, the restraints dissolve. If aggression is a crime, then the aggressor has no rights. There is no negotiated peace with a criminal -- only unconditional surrender, regime change, and punishment. The language of justice replaces the language of regulation, and the result is total war: wars of annihilation fought in the name of peace.

The Nuremberg Trials crystallized this transformation. Schmitt viewed them -- unsurprisingly, given his own interrogation there -- as victor's justice lacking a genuine legal foundation. His objection was not that the Nazis' crimes were excusable but that the legal category "crimes against peace" was retroactive and politically motivated, designed to criminalize the losing side while exempting the victors' own uses of force. The structural point: once war is a crime, the distinction between war and punishment collapses, and the "international community" becomes a prosecution apparatus rather than a system of coexistence.

### The Post-1945 Order: Hegemony Without Nomos

The UN system, in Schmitt's reading, is a disguised American hegemony that lacks a genuine spatial nomos. It claims universality but depends on the particular power of the United States to enforce (or not enforce) its rules. The Security Council veto structure ensures that the great powers exempt themselves from the rules they impose on others. The result is not a global legal order but a hierarchy disguised as law -- what Schmitt would call a "discriminatory concept of war" in which some states can use force with impunity while others are sanctioned for the same behavior.

This analysis, whatever its origins in Schmitt's self-serving post-war positioning, has proven analytically durable. The selective enforcement of international law -- invoked for Iraq's invasion of Kuwait (1990) but not for NATO's bombing of Yugoslavia (1999), cited against Russia in Ukraine but not against the US in Iraq (2003) -- fits Schmitt's prediction that a universal legal order without a spatial nomos produces hypocrisy rather than justice.

## Land and Sea: Two Civilizational Models

*Land und Meer* (1942) frames world history as a contest between telluric (land-based) and thalassocratic (sea-based) civilizations, each generating distinct economic, legal, and political orders.

### The Land-Power Model

Land powers think in terms of fixed territory, borders, and spatial control. Their economic base is agriculture, extraction, and overland trade. Their legal systems emphasize territorial sovereignty, defined borders, and the land-based nomos of appropriation-division-production. Historical examples:

- The Roman Empire: roads, provinces, fixed frontiers (limes)
- Medieval Christendom: feudal land tenure, bounded estates
- The continental European states: territorial sovereignty, standing armies
- Russia: continental expansion across Eurasia, "heartland" power

### The Sea-Power Model

Maritime powers think in terms of movement, connectivity, and control of trade routes rather than territory. Their economic base is commerce, finance, and the exploitation of maritime chokepoints. Their legal frameworks emphasize freedom of navigation, open seas, and commercial law. Historical examples:

| Sea Power | Period | Key Innovation |
|-----------|--------|----------------|
| [[Venice]] | 13th-16th c. | Arsenal system, maritime commercial law, galley trade |
| [[Dutch Golden Age\|Dutch Republic]] | 17th c. | [[Hugo Grotius\|Mare Liberum]], VOC, financial instruments |
| [[English Commercial Empire\|Britain]] | 18th-19th c. | Naval supremacy, free trade, sterling zone |
| United States | 20th-21st c. | Air/sea power, dollar system, global basing |

Each successor inherited and transformed the maritime model. The Dutch displaced Venice by opening the Atlantic; the British displaced the Dutch by building a permanent navy; the Americans displaced the British by adding air power and a global financial architecture.

### The British "Spatial Revolution"

Schmitt identifies Britain's transformation from a European island to a global maritime empire as the decisive spatial revolution of modernity. England's choice -- after the defeat of the Spanish Armada (1588) and especially after the Navigation Acts (1651) -- was to become an "island" in a new sense: not a piece of European land surrounded by water, but a sea power that happened to have a territorial base. Britain controlled oceans rather than territory, trade routes rather than provinces.

This created a fundamentally different economic order. The British system -- free trade, gold standard, naval supremacy, financial intermediation through the City of London -- was a maritime nomos, not a territorial one. Its strength was flexibility and commercial reach; its vulnerability was dependence on command of the sea. The [[Fiscal State Emergence|fiscal-military state]] that Britain built was designed to fund the navy that secured the commercial system that funded the state -- a self-reinforcing loop that held until the world wars exhausted it.

[[Hugo Grotius]]'s *Mare Liberum* (1609) provided the legal theory for this maritime order: the sea cannot be owned, therefore it is free for all to navigate and trade upon. This was not disinterested jurisprudence -- it was a legal weapon deployed by the Dutch Republic against Portuguese and Spanish claims to oceanic monopoly. Britain later adopted the principle when it served British interests (naval superiority made "freedom of the seas" a British advantage) and modified it when it did not (the Navigation Acts restricted colonial trade to British ships).

## The Grossraum as Historical Pattern

Schmitt's Grossraum ("great space") theory, developed in *Volkerrechtliche Grossraumordnung* (1939), proposes that international order after the collapse of the jus publicum Europaeum would reorganize around regional blocs, each dominated by a leading power that sets the norms for its space.

### The Monroe Doctrine as Template

The analytical model is the [[US Hemisphere Defense Strategy|Monroe Doctrine]] (1823): the United States declared the Western Hemisphere closed to European colonial intervention. This was, in Schmitt's reading, the first modern Grossraum -- a spatially defined zone under the hegemony of a single power, with a ban on interference by "spatially foreign" powers (raumfremde Machte).

Schmitt saw the Monroe Doctrine as a genuine spatial principle: it defined a geographic zone and assigned political responsibility within it. What he objected to was the Wilsonian transformation of this spatial principle into a universal ideology -- the claim that American principles (democracy, self-determination, open markets) should govern the entire world rather than just a hemisphere. This universalization, in his view, destroyed the spatial logic of the Grossraum by making American claims limitless.

### Grossraum as Successor Nomos

Schmitt proposed -- and this was the politically loaded aspect, given the 1939 publication date -- that a new nomos might emerge from the formation of multiple Grossraume: a European space under German leadership, an East Asian space under Japanese leadership, an American hemisphere under US leadership. Each would be internally ordered by its leading power and externally protected by the principle of non-interference.

Stripped of its 1939 political context, the Grossraum framework describes a pattern that recurs throughout the vault's timeline: regional powers establishing economic and legal orders within their sphere. The [[Chartered Company Model]] organized trade within imperial spaces. The [[Mercantilist Institutions|mercantilist system]] created exclusive economic zones tied to metropolitan powers. The Cold War's division into American and Soviet blocs was, structurally, a two-Grossraum system. The question of whether the post-Cold War "unipolar moment" represented a genuine global nomos or merely a temporary American Grossraum masquerading as universalism is precisely the question Schmitt's framework asks.

## Implications for Economic History

Schmitt's framework, though developed by a jurist for political-legal purposes, has direct relevance to how the vault understands economic development:

1. Property rights presuppose spatial ordering. No nomos, no enforceable property. The original Landnahme creates the conditions for all subsequent economic activity.

2. The bracketing of war enabled European commercial expansion. Predictable, limited wars allowed long-term investment, insurance markets, and international finance to develop. The [[War Finance Innovations|innovations in war finance]] were possible precisely because the jus publicum Europaeum made war calculable.

3. Colonialism was spatially authorized. The "beyond the line" doctrine gave legal cover to the land appropriations that drove the Atlantic economy, the plantation system, and the capital accumulation behind the [[Industrial Revolution]].

4. Maritime vs. land-based orders produce different economic systems. Sea powers develop commercial, financial, insurance-based economies; land powers develop extractive, agricultural, bureaucratic economies. The vault's notes on [[Venice]], the [[Dutch Golden Age]], and the [[English Commercial Empire]] describe the economic side of what Schmitt theorizes as the thalassocratic model.

5. The collapse of spatial order disrupts economic calculation. When the nomos breaks down -- as in 1914-45 -- trade, finance, and investment contract because the rules governing conflict and property are no longer predictable. The [[Interwar Economy]] demonstrates this directly.
