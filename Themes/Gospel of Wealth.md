# Gospel of Wealth

[[Andrew Carnegie]]'s "Wealth" (June 1889, *North American Review*) argued that industrial civilization inevitably concentrates wealth in the hands of the capable, and that the rich are therefore trustees obligated to redistribute their surplus during their lifetimes for the public good. The essay offered the American elite a bargain: keep your fortunes, but give them away before you die—"the man who dies thus rich dies disgraced." It simultaneously defended inequality as the engine of progress and condemned inheritance as its corruption, proposing voluntary philanthropy as the alternative to both inherited dynasties and state confiscation. The doctrine shaped [[John D. Rockefeller]]'s foundation-building, influenced a century of American mega-philanthropy, and provided the intellectual framework for the Gates-Buffett Giving Pledge (2010)—while its critics, from the [[Progressive Era]] to the present, have argued that voluntary redistribution is no substitute for institutional guarantees.

## The Argument

### Three Dispositions of Surplus Wealth

| Option | Carnegie's verdict |
|--------|--------------------|
| Leave to heirs | Worst — creates idle dynasties, "generally demoralized" children |
| Bequeath at death | Acceptable but wasteful — the dead man's judgment is gone |
| Give during lifetime | Best — the man of wealth as active trustee of the community |

### The Logic

| Step | Claim |
|------|-------|
| 1. Inequality is inevitable | Industrial progress requires concentration of capital |
| 2. Concentration is beneficial | The capable allocate resources better than the mass |
| 3. But inheritance corrupts | Heirs didn't earn it — "fortunes dwindle in three generations" |
| 4. The state taxes badly | Government redistribution is wasteful and misdirected |
| 5. Therefore: give it away wisely | The rich man must be the community's trustee |
| 6. Give institutions, not alms | Libraries, universities, hospitals — not direct charity |

### What Counts as Worthy Philanthropy

| Recommended | Rejected |
|-------------|----------|
| Libraries and education | Direct cash to the poor |
| Universities and research | Indiscriminate almsgiving |
| Hospitals and medical research | "Encouraging the slothful" |
| Parks and public spaces | Inherited wealth for heirs |
| Concert halls and museums | Government redistribution |

Carnegie was explicit: "It were better for mankind that the millions of the rich were thrown into the sea than so spent as to encourage the slothful, the drunken, the unworthy." The paternalism was the point—the wealthy trustee decides what the community needs.

## The Context

### Why 1889?

| Factor | Relevance |
|--------|-----------|
| Peak inequality | Top 1% owned ~45% of national wealth |
| Homestead in the future | Carnegie hadn't yet faced his worst labor crisis (1892) |
| Populist movement | Farmers demanding graduated income tax, free silver |
| Labor unrest | Great Railroad Strike (1877), Haymarket (1886) still fresh |
| Socialist threat | Growing labor radicalism in Europe and America |
| Henry George | *Progress and Poverty* (1879) argued for land value taxation |
| Edward Bellamy | *Looking Backward* (1888) imagined socialist utopia |

### The Strategic Dimension

The Gospel of Wealth was not only a moral argument—it was a political one. By proposing voluntary redistribution, Carnegie was preempting mandatory redistribution:

| Threat | Carnegie's counter |
|--------|-------------------|
| Income tax | The rich will give voluntarily — no need to tax |
| Estate tax | The rich should give during life — the state needn't seize at death |
| Socialist redistribution | Capitalism works — the trustee model preserves both wealth creation and social benefit |
| Populist revolt | The elite will self-correct — trust us |

The implicit promise: if the rich fulfill their obligations as trustees, society won't need to confiscate their wealth. The implicit threat: if they don't, society will.

## Influence

### Who Listened

| Figure | Response |
|--------|----------|
| [[John D. Rockefeller]] | Built the Rockefeller Foundation (1913), General Education Board — professionalized philanthropy on Carnegie's model |
| Andrew Mellon | National Gallery of Art, Mellon Foundation |
| Julius Rosenwald (Sears) | 5,300 schools for Black students in the South |
| Russell Sage Foundation (1907) | Social science research |
| Pattern | Every major Gilded Age/Progressive Era fortune created a foundation |

### The Modern Heir: The Giving Pledge (2010)

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Founders | Bill Gates and Warren Buffett |
| Commitment | Signatories pledge to give majority of wealth to philanthropy |
| Participants | 240+ billionaires worldwide (by 2024) |
| Model | Explicitly echoes Carnegie's doctrine |
| Carnegie's test | "The man who dies thus rich dies disgraced" → give during lifetime |

### Institutional Philanthropy

Carnegie didn't just give money—he professionalized giving. The Carnegie Corporation of New York (1911) was designed to outlast its founder, with professional staff making grant decisions based on strategy rather than whim. This model—the endowed foundation with professional management—became the standard:

| Foundation | Established | Focus |
|------------|-------------|-------|
| Carnegie Corporation | 1911 | Education, international peace |
| Rockefeller Foundation | 1913 | Public health, science, agriculture |
| Ford Foundation | 1936 | Social justice, democracy |
| Gates Foundation | 2000 | Global health, education |

## The Critique

### From the Left

| Critic | Argument |
|--------|----------|
| Populists | Why should one man decide what a community needs? |
| Socialists | Philanthropy legitimizes the system that creates inequality |
| Labor | Carnegie preaches generosity while crushing unions at Homestead |
| Modern critics (Giridharadas) | Philanthropy as "reputation laundering" — solves symptoms, preserves structures |

### The Homestead Problem

Three years after publishing the Gospel of Wealth, Carnegie authorized [[Henry Clay Frick]] to break the Homestead union with Pinkertons. The contradiction was immediate and devastating:

| Gospel of Wealth (1889) | Homestead (1892) |
|--------------------------|-------------------|
| The rich must serve the community | Workers shot by private army |
| Wealth as trusteeship | Wealth extracted from labor |
| Paternalistic benevolence | Paternalistic violence |

Critics then and since have argued that the Gospel was self-serving: it justified accumulation (inequality is natural and beneficial) while making redistribution voluntary (no enforceable obligation). Carnegie got to decide both how wealth was extracted and how it was returned—the workers had no voice in either decision.

### The Enforcement Problem

| Voluntary model | Mandatory model |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Depends on individual conscience | Depends on institutional design |
| Uneven — some give, some don't | Universal — everyone pays |
| Donor chooses priorities | Democratic process chooses |
| No accountability | Electoral accountability |
| Pro: directed by competence | Pro: directed by consent |
| Con: paternalistic | Con: bureaucratic |

The [[Progressive Era]]'s answer to the Gospel of Wealth was the 16th Amendment (1913) and the federal estate tax (1916)—not because Carnegie's philanthropy failed, but because not everyone was Carnegie. Voluntary redistribution works only if all wealthy people are generous; mandatory redistribution works regardless.

## The Deeper Point

The Gospel of Wealth articulated the fundamental tension in capitalist societies between wealth creation and wealth distribution. Carnegie's insight—that inequality is inherent to industrial progress but morally unsustainable without redistribution—remains the central problem. His proposed solution—noblesse oblige professionalized into institutional philanthropy—is still the preferred model of American elites four generations later.

But the essay's deeper function was political: it offered the [[Gilded Age]] elite a narrative that justified both their fortunes and their consciences. You could be fabulously rich AND morally righteous—provided you gave it away eventually. This made the Gospel simultaneously radical (no American magnate had previously claimed a *duty* to redistribute) and conservative (it preserved the right to accumulate without limit, asked no structural questions about how the wealth was obtained, and left redistribution entirely to the discretion of the wealthy).

The Progressive Era's institutional response—income tax, estate tax, antitrust, regulation—didn't reject Carnegie's premise. It accepted that voluntary redistribution was insufficient and built mandatory mechanisms. The tension between these two models—voluntary philanthropy and mandatory taxation—has defined American political economy ever since. Every tax debate, every foundation grant, every pledge by a tech billionaire echoes the argument Carnegie made in 1889: should the rich give, or should society take? Carnegie said give. The 16th Amendment said: both.
