# Post-Communist Transition

The largest economic transformation in history: 30 countries, 400 million people, shifting from planned to market economies simultaneously. Outcomes varied dramatically—from EU convergence (Poland, Czech Republic) to oligarchic stagnation (Russia, Ukraine) to authoritarian development (China, Vietnam). The variation reveals that institutions mattered more than policies.

|---------|--------|

| Central planning | No price signals |
| Full employment | Overstaffing, hidden unemployment |

| Education | High, but distorted |

## The Deeper Point

The post-communist transition revealed that markets require [[Institutions and Progress|institutions]]—and that institutions can't be created by decree. The "Washington Consensus" assumed that liberalizing prices and privatizing assets would spontaneously generate functioning markets. It didn't. Without property rights enforcement, contract law, banking regulation, and competitive markets, privatization became theft and liberalization became chaos.

The dramatic variation in outcomes—Poland thriving while Ukraine stagnated, despite similar starting points—proves that policies alone don't determine results. The EU-anchored countries built institutions because external conditionality required it and membership rewarded it. Russia and Ukraine, without this anchor, saw elites capture the transition for extraction rather than production.

The transition also reveals the persistence of [[Economic Persistence|historical institutions]]. Countries with Habsburg heritage (Czech Republic, Hungary, Poland) transitioned more successfully than those with Russian/Byzantine heritage. Communist rule was a 40-year interruption, but deeper patterns persisted beneath it.

The deepest lesson: creating markets is an institutional project, not a policy choice. Getting prices right matters, but getting institutions right matters more. The transition's winners understood this—or stumbled into EU membership that forced institutional development. The losers either didn't understand it or faced conditions (resource wealth, no EU anchor, state weakness) that enabled capture by extractive elites.
