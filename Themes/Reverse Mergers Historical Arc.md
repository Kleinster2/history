# Reverse Mergers Historical Arc

How did the reverse merger — a private company taking over the listing of a public shell — go from a niche workaround in the 1970s to the dominant alternative listing mechanic in the 2020s? The arc is a four-cycle story of capital markets routing around the friction of the conventional IPO: each cycle produces its own variant of the mechanic, each variant draws abuse and regulatory backlash, and each backlash produces the next variant. The current 2025-26 [[Trump|Trump-era]] sponsored reverse merger pipeline is best understood not as a novel financial innovation but as the fourth iteration of a recurring pattern. This note traces that pattern from 1934 (Securities Exchange Act) through May 2026 (the [[Cove Kaz Capital]] / [[Skyline Builders]] tungsten merger), with comparison tables across cycles.

## The four cycles

| Cycle | Years | Dominant variant | Mechanism | Volume / signature deal |
|-------|-------|-------------------|-----------|--------------------------|
| 1 | 1970s-1980s | Shell mergers | Existing dormant public companies (often defunct mining or oil shells from earlier booms) acquired and recapitalized by private operating companies | Hundreds of small-cap deals; no canonical signature |
| 2 | Late 2000s-early 2010s | Chinese-issuer reverse mergers | US-listed shells acquired by Chinese operating companies, often via a third-country holding structure | ~600 deals 2007-2012; signature collapses Sino-Forest (2012), China MediaExpress (2011) |
| 3 | 2020-2022 | SPACs (Special Purpose Acquisition Companies) | Newly-floated blank-check trusts seek and merge with private operating companies | 613 SPAC IPOs in 2021 raising ~$162B (per SPAC Insider); signature collapses Lordstown, Nikola, WeWork via SPAC |
| 4 | 2024-2026 | Sponsored reverse merger pipeline | Same broker-dealer underwrites a fresh small-cap shell IPO, recapitalizes it through an affiliated SPV, and merges in a chosen operating company | [[Cove Kaz Capital]] / [[Skyline Builders]] (tungsten, April 30, 2026); [[XTEND]] / [[JFB Construction Holdings]] (Israeli AI defense, February 17, 2026) |

The four cycles share three structural features: they bypass the conventional IPO bookbuild / S-1 review process, they shift liability and disclosure obligations away from the operating company, and each in turn drew SEC regulatory tightening that did not eliminate the underlying mechanic but pushed it into the next variant.

## Cycle 1 — Shell mergers (1970s-1980s)

The legal foundation for the modern reverse merger predates these cycles by half a century: the Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 created a US public-listing regime in which the *listing itself* — once obtained — was an asset that could be sold or transferred independently of the operating business that originally obtained it. Once a company was registered and listed, the registration could outlive the operating business. Mining and oil shells from the 1920s-1940s booms — companies whose original gold or petroleum prospects had failed but whose listings remained nominally active on the OTC and pink-sheet markets — sat as inventory.

In the 1970s and 1980s, a class of broker-dealers built a niche business identifying these dormant shells, recapitalizing them, and merging private operating companies into them. The motivations on the operating-company side were:

- Cost (a reverse merger could be done for $250K-$1M vs. $5-15M for a conventional IPO of the era)
- Speed (3-6 months vs. 12-24 months)
- Avoidance of underwriter syndicate due-diligence pressure
- Access to a public listing for companies too small or too unconventional for the syndicates of the era

The SEC's posture in this period treated the practice as a curiosity rather than a systemic problem. Rule 144 (1972) governed the resale of restricted securities after reverse mergers; SEC enforcement focused on egregious individual fraud cases rather than the mechanic itself. The dominant story of corporate finance in the 1980s was [[American Corporate Restructuring|leveraged buyouts and hostile takeovers]] (Drexel, KKR, RJR Nabisco), and the reverse-merger niche stayed small.

## Cycle 2 — Chinese-issuer reverse mergers (2007-2012)

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002) raised the cost of a US IPO for emerging companies — particularly foreign issuers without US auditor relationships. Beginning around 2005, a class of US broker-dealers, accountants, and legal advisers built a pipeline matching dormant US shells with Chinese operating companies seeking US listings without the SOX-era IPO friction.

Per academic and SEC studies, approximately 600 Chinese companies obtained US listings via reverse merger between 2007 and 2012. The common structure: a Chinese operating company would form a Cayman or BVI holding company, the holding company would acquire the US shell, and the operating company would merge into the structure. The combined entity inherited the US listing and reporting obligations.

Signature collapses:

| Company | Year | Pattern |
|---------|------|---------|
| RINO International | 2010 | Auditor (Frazer Frost) resigned; SEC found bank account fraud |
| China MediaExpress | 2011 | Citron Research short report; auditor (Deloitte) resigned; SEC delisted |
| Sino-Forest | 2012 | Muddy Waters short report; bankrupt; CAD$8B+ in shareholder losses |
| Longtop Financial | 2011 | Auditor (Deloitte) resigned; SEC enforcement |

The SEC's response built up across 2011-2014:

- Trading halts on dozens of Chinese reverse-merger issuers (2011-2012)
- Stricter listing standards on NYSE and Nasdaq for reverse-merger companies (2011 — required seasoning periods of 1 year on a US OTC market before applying for a major-exchange listing)
- The PCAOB-China audit access dispute (2012-2022, eventually resolved by the Holding Foreign Companies Accountable Act)
- Auditor enforcement actions

The crackdown ended the cycle's volume but did not eliminate the mechanic. The 2017-2024 period saw a steady-state level of Chinese small-cap IPOs through US broker-dealers — a population that became the focus of the [[House Select Committee on China]]'s March 8, 2026 letter on "ramp-and-dump" practices, which named [[Dominari Holdings]] / [[Dominari Securities]], [[Revere Securities]], and [[D Boral Capital]] as primary underwriters.

## Cycle 3 — SPACs (2020-2022)

The SPAC mechanic is structurally distinct from the prior cycles: instead of acquiring a dormant shell, sponsors created and IPO'd a new blank-check trust whose only asset was cash held in escrow, with a 24-month deadline to identify and merge with a private operating company. The 2020-2021 boom was the largest single-cycle of alternative listings in US capital-markets history:

| Year | SPAC IPOs | Capital raised | Notable de-SPACs |
|------|-----------|----------------|--------------------|
| 2019 | 59 | $13.6B | — |
| 2020 | 248 | $83.4B | DraftKings, QuantumScape, Virgin Galactic |
| 2021 | 613 | $162.5B | Lucid Motors, SoFi, Grab, Polestar |
| 2022 | 86 | $13.4B | (boom collapsed mid-2022) |
| 2023 | 31 | $3.8B | (post-rule cycle) |

(Source: SPAC Insider, SPAC Research)

The 2020-2021 cycle drew abuse on three vectors:

1. **The 20% sponsor promote** — sponsors received roughly 20% of the post-IPO equity for nominal consideration, diluting common shareholders from $10 nominal to ~$6.67 net cash per share at de-SPAC.
2. **Forward-looking safe harbor** — the 1995 Private Securities Litigation Reform Act forward-looking safe harbor was assumed to apply to de-SPAC merger projections, allowing emerging companies to publish revenue forecasts that conventional IPOs could not. This produced the now-canonical pattern of de-SPAC companies missing first-year projections by 50-90%.
3. **Redemption mechanics** — public common shareholders had the right to redeem at $10 per share at the de-SPAC vote, which meant the de-SPAC trust frequently delivered far less cash than projected. By the 2022-2023 cohort, redemption rates of 80-90% were standard.

The post-cycle data (per SEC studies, NYU Stern, and SPAC Research) found that 77% of merged de-SPAC companies traded below $10 within a year of merger; the cohort underperformed both the broader market and conventional IPOs of the same period by margins of 30-60%.

The SEC's response, finalized January 2024, addressed all three vectors:

- The de-SPAC target was treated as a co-registrant, exposing it to full Securities Act Section 11 liability (eliminating the prior structure where only the SPAC sponsor bore registration liability)
- The forward-looking safe harbor was eliminated for SPAC projections
- Sponsor compensation, dilution, and conflicts had to be specifically disclosed
- Underwriter status was clarified (banks involved in the SPAC IPO are now generally treated as underwriters of the de-SPAC merger as well, exposing them to Section 11 liability)

The rules effectively ended the SPAC boom. The combined volume in 2024 was a fraction of the 2021 peak, and SPAC IPO activity dropped to pre-2020 levels.

## Cycle 4 — Sponsored reverse merger pipeline (2024-2026)

The January 2024 SEC SPAC rules created the regulatory gradient that made Cycle 4 attractive. The same emerging-company pool that had used SPACs in 2020-2021 — pre-revenue defense, drone, mining, crypto, AI ventures unable to clear conventional IPO bookbuild and S-1-projection liability — needed a new listing mechanic. The variant that emerged uses a fresh broker-underwritten small-cap shell IPO, recapitalizes it through an affiliated SPV factory, and merges in the operating company.

Distinct from Cycles 1-3 in three ways:

| Feature | Cycle 1 (1970s-80s) | Cycle 2 (2007-12) | Cycle 3 (2020-22) | Cycle 4 (2024-26) |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Shell origin | Found (dormant pre-existing) | Found (dormant pre-existing) | Made (fresh blank-check IPO) | Made (fresh small-cap IPO with operating fiction) |
| Sponsor compensation | Advisory fees | Advisory + retained shares | 20% promote | Deal fees + SPV management fees + PIPE placement fees |
| Deadline | None | None | 24 months | None |
| Redemption rights | None | None | Yes ($10/sh) | None |
| Forward-looking safe harbor | n/a | n/a | Yes pre-2024, then no | Status uncertain — SEC has not reclassified the underwritten-shell-then-merger as a de-SPAC equivalent |

The canonical 2024-2026 case is [[Dominari Holdings]] / [[American Ventures]] — a Nasdaq-listed broker-dealer ([[Dominari Securities]]) and 90%-owned SPV factory operating five named pipeline deals through May 2026:

| Listed shell | Operating co. | Sector | Status |
|--------------|----------------|--------|--------|
| [[SRM Entertainment]] | [[Tron]] | Crypto | Closed July 2025 |
| [[JFB Construction Holdings]] | [[XTEND]] | Israeli AI defense robotics | Pending; mid-2026 close, $1.5B |
| [[Aureus Greenway Holdings]] | [[Powerus]] | Drone roll-up | Pending; future ticker PUSA |
| [[Skyline Builders]] | [[Cove Kaz Capital]] | Kazakh tungsten | Announced April 30, 2026; $1.6B EXIM/DFC LoIs |
| [[Dogehash Technologies]] | [[Thumzup Media]] | Crypto / data center | Announced |

The Cycle 4 distinguishing feature is *deliberate origination*: shells are created with future reverse-mergers in mind, rather than salvaged after operating-business failure. That deliberate origination is what makes the pipeline an industrial process and what creates the regulatory exposure — the closer the pattern looks to a de facto SPAC, the more likely the SEC reclassifies it.

## Recurring patterns across cycles

Five patterns recur across the four cycles:

### Pattern 1 — IPO friction is the upstream driver

Each cycle's volume correlates with the disclosure / liability cost of the conventional IPO of the era. Cycle 1 was driven by 1970s underwriter syndicate concentration; Cycle 2 by SOX-era foreign-issuer audit costs; Cycle 3 by tech-era forecast-projection liability; Cycle 4 by the post-January-2024 SEC SPAC rules. The mechanic does not appear in periods when conventional IPOs are cheap and fast (most of the 1990s, 2014-2018).

### Pattern 2 — Sector concentration follows the policy frontier

Each cycle's deals concentrate in the sectors with the largest gap between operating-stage immaturity and capital demand. Cycle 1: small-cap industrials. Cycle 2: Chinese consumer / industrial growth stories. Cycle 3: pre-revenue tech (EVs, space, fintech, crypto). Cycle 4: Trump-administration-priority verticals (drones, AI defense, mining, crypto). The sector concentration is what produces the federal-policy-overlap signal in Cycle 4 — three of the five named [[Dominari Holdings]] pipeline deals received federal procurement orders or financing letters of interest within weeks of merger announcements.

### Pattern 3 — Concentration of broker-dealer infrastructure

Each cycle is operated by a small handful of broker-dealers, accountants, and legal advisers. Cycle 1 was dominated by a few specialty shops; Cycle 2 by [[D Boral Capital|EF Hutton]] and successors, plus a Chinese-focused tier; Cycle 3 by Cantor, Goldman, Citi, Deutsche; Cycle 4 by [[Dominari Securities]], [[Revere Securities]], [[D Boral Capital]]. Concentration creates research-and-banking conflicts that are the canonical regulatory failure mode.

### Pattern 4 — Each cycle ends with a regulatory tightening that does not eliminate the mechanic

The regulatory response to each cycle pushes the mechanic into the next variant rather than eliminating it. Cycle 1 → Rule 144 + listing-standard tweaks → Cycle 2. Cycle 2 → seasoning rules + auditor enforcement → quiescence then Cycle 3. Cycle 3 → January 2024 SPAC rules → Cycle 4. Cycle 4's regulatory endpoint is not yet visible in May 2026, but the [[House Select Committee on China]] investigation and the latent SEC reclassification authority are the most likely vectors.

### Pattern 5 — Political alignment as the load-bearing marketing wedge

The Cycle 2 alignment was the late-2000s narrative of Chinese growth as an absolute bull case. The Cycle 3 alignment was the 2020-2021 retail-investor enthusiasm and the Trump 1.0 / Biden infrastructure-policy alignment. The Cycle 4 alignment is the second [[Trump]] administration: pipeline deals concentrate in administration-priority verticals, [[Donald Trump Jr.]] and [[Eric Trump]] are anchor LPs in the SPV factory, and federal procurement / financing events follow merger announcements at correlated intervals. The political-alignment wedge is what attracts the LP base and what creates the political-counterparty risk on the back end.

## Synthesis

The reverse merger is older than its current public profile suggests. It has been a continuously-available alternative to the conventional IPO since the mid-20th century, surfacing in waves whenever the disclosure or liability cost of the conventional IPO rises faster than emerging-company capital demand. Each wave produces its own pattern of regulatory backlash, and each backlash produces the next variant. The 2025-26 cycle is the fourth such wave, distinguished by deliberate shell origination, post-SPAC-rule regulatory gradient exploitation, and an explicit political-alignment wedge embedded in the operating-company sector mix and SPV LP base.

The [[Cove Kaz Capital]] / [[Skyline Builders]] tungsten merger and the [[XTEND]] / [[JFB Construction Holdings]] AI-defense merger are the canonical 2026 examples. Whether Cycle 4 ends with a regulatory tightening that produces Cycle 5, or with the [[Sponsored reverse merger pipeline]] surviving as a normalized listing mechanic, depends on whether the SEC reclassifies the underwritten-shell-then-merger as a de facto SPAC under the January 2024 rules, and on whether the [[House Select Committee on China]] investigation expands beyond Chinese-issuer concerns into the underwriting-pipeline conflicts the same broker-dealer infrastructure presents.
