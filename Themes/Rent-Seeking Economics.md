# Rent-Seeking Economics

The systematic study of getting rich without producing. Rent-seeking—using resources to capture wealth transfers rather than create new value—is the dark twin of productive entrepreneurship. Understanding rent-seeking illuminates why some societies channel ambition into building while others channel it into taking.

## What Is Rent-Seeking?

### Definition (Tullock, 1967)

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Rent | Payment above competitive return |
| Seeking | Expending resources to capture rent |
| Waste | Resources spent seeking, not producing |
| Sum | Often negative (costs exceed rent) |

### Examples

| Activity | What It Captures |
|----------|------------------|
| Lobbying for tariffs | Monopoly profits |
| Bribing officials | Government contracts |
| Regulatory capture | Protection from competition |
| Patent litigation | Others' innovation |
| Political contributions | Favorable treatment |

### Rent-Seeking vs. Profit-Seeking

| Profit-Seeking | Rent-Seeking |
|----------------|--------------|
| Creates value | Transfers value |
| Competitive return | Above-market return |
| Benefits society | Costs society |
| [[Entrepreneurship]] | Political entrepreneurship |

## Tullock's Insight

### The Original Problem

| Observation | Puzzle |
|-------------|--------|
| Monopoly creates rents | Known |
| Why not more lobbying? | Rents exceed lobbying costs |
| Competition for rents | Dissipates them in seeking |
| Social cost | Deadweight loss + rent-seeking cost |

### The Calculation

| Monopoly Welfare Loss | Traditional View |
|-----------------------|------------------|
| Deadweight triangle | Standard analysis |
| Plus rent-seeking costs | Tullock addition |
| Resources wasted | Lawyers, lobbyists, bribes |
| True social cost | Much larger |

## Institutional Determinants

### What Encourages Rent-Seeking

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Weak rule of law | Capture easier |
| Concentrated government power | Rents available |
| Discretionary authority | Officials can sell |
| Opacity | Corruption hidden |
| Weak competition | Monopoly sustainable |

### What Discourages Rent-Seeking

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Strong rule of law | Capture harder |
| Limited government | Fewer rents available |
| Transparent processes | Corruption visible |
| Competition | Rents eroded |
| Voice and exit | Accountability |

## The Deeper Point

Rent-seeking economics formalizes a central insight of [[Institutions and Progress|institutional economics]]: what matters is not whether people are ambitious but what they're ambitious for. Every society has people who want to get ahead. The question is whether the rules reward building or taking.

Baumol's framework makes this precise: entrepreneurs are everywhere, but their direction depends on relative payoffs. If rent-seeking offers higher returns than production, ambitious people will seek rents. If production offers higher returns, they'll produce. The same human drive leads to different social outcomes depending on [[Economic Persistence|institutional rules]].

This explains the persistence of poverty in resource-rich countries. When a government controls valuable resources, ambitious people compete to control the government rather than build businesses. The most talented organize militias, not startups. The resources that could have funded development fund conflict instead.

The rent-seeking lens also illuminates rich-country pathologies. The growth of finance, lobbying, and regulatory complexity in developed economies suggests rent-seeking may be increasing even in institutionally strong countries. When financial sector profits exceed returns from production, ambitious people enter finance. When lobbying returns exceed innovation returns, firms lobby rather than research.

The deepest lesson is that rent-seeking is not a bug but a feature of any system with valuable rents. The only cure is making rents unavailable—through competition, transparency, and limited government discretion. Institutional design must assume that people will seek rents if rents are available.
