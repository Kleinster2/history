# Services Economy

The shift from manufacturing to services represents the most significant structural transformation of advanced economies in the late 20th century. In 1950, manufacturing employed more Americans than services; by 2020, services employed 80% of workers. This transition—sometimes called deindustrialization or post-industrial economy—reshaped work, geography, politics, and economic thinking. Whether it represents progress, decline, or simply change remains contested.

## Causes of the Shift

### Productivity Differences

| Sector | Productivity Growth |
|--------|---------------------|
| Manufacturing | High (automation) |
| Services (some) | Low (Baumol's disease) |
| Effect | Manufacturing produces more with fewer workers |

### Baumol's Cost Disease

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Observation | String quartet always needs 4 people |
| Problem | Service productivity hard to increase |
| Result | Service costs rise relative to goods |
| Examples | Education, healthcare, live entertainment |

### Demand Patterns

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Income elasticity | Services have higher elasticity |

| Experience economy | Consumers shift to experiences |
| Demographics | Aging populations need healthcare |

### Globalization

| Process | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| Manufacturing offshoring | Moved to lower-wage countries |
| Services less tradable | (Until recently) |
| Comparative advantage | Developed countries specialize in services |

## The Deeper Point

The services transition raises fundamental questions about [[Economic Measurement\|what we're measuring]] and what we value. Manufacturing produced tangible things; services often produce experiences, coordination, or care. GDP captures market transactions, but much service value is harder to price—does a teacher's contribution equal their salary? The [[Baumol's Cost Disease]] insight suggests that service-dominated economies face permanently rising costs in healthcare and education, since these activities resist productivity improvement. This isn't necessarily a problem—we're richer, so we spend more on things that require human attention. But it does mean that the growth patterns of the industrial era won't repeat. The political economy matters too: manufacturing unions gave workers power; service sector atomization undermines it. The geographic polarization—thriving cities, declining industrial towns—maps onto political divides across the developed world. The services economy isn't a destination but a transition, and where it leads depends on whether we find new [[Institutions and Progress\|institutions]] to manage an economy of human interaction rather than material production.
