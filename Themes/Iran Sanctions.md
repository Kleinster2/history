# Iran Sanctions

Sanctions against Iran constitute the longest-running and most comprehensive economic warfare campaign of the modern era — four decades of escalating financial isolation that failed to change the regime's behavior but profoundly transformed Iranian society. From Carter's initial asset freezes (1979) through Obama's multilateral SWIFT cutoff to Trump's "maximum pressure," each escalation squeezed the economy while consistently producing the same paradox: weakening the pro-Western middle class and strengthening the [[IRGC]], the very institution sanctions were meant to constrain. A pattern of broken American promises — the Algiers Accord, hostage release reciprocity, Clinton-era oil contracts, Obama-era sanctions relief, Biden's prisoner deal — reinforced the hardliner narrative that engagement was a trap, making each subsequent diplomatic opening more fragile than the last. Ali Vaez, co-author of *How Sanctions Work: Iran and the Impact of Economic Warfare*, argues the evidence is unambiguous: containment through sanctions "has not resolved the problem" and by every metric — nuclear, missile, proxy, domestic repression — "has made it worse."

## Overview

### The Escalation Ladder

| Phase | Period | Key Measures | Effect |
|-------|--------|-------------|--------|
| Initial freezes | 1979–80 | Asset freeze after hostage crisis | Diplomatic rupture |
| Reagan era | 1980s | Trade restrictions, arms embargo | Contained but Iran busy with Iraq war |
| Clinton era | 1990s | ILSA (Iran Libya Sanctions Act); cancelled oil contract offered to US firms | Khatami's reformist opening punished |
| Bush era | 2000s | Post-9/11 "Axis of Evil"; nuclear-related sanctions | Cooperative Iran recast as threat |
| Obama multilateral | 2010–15 | SWIFT disconnection, EU oil embargo, UN sanctions | Brought Iran to table → [[JCPOA]] |
| JCPOA relief | 2016–18 | Partial sanctions lifted | Economy began recovering |
| Maximum pressure | 2018–20 | Trump reimposed and escalated all sanctions | Economy crushed; nuclear program resumed |
| Biden era | 2021–25 | Partial enforcement, prisoner deal ($6B) | Pulled back after October 7th |
| War era | 2025–26 | Full wartime sanctions | Economy under simultaneous sanctions and bombing |

## The Mechanisms

### Financial Warfare

| Tool | Function |
|------|----------|
| SWIFT disconnection | Cut Iran from global banking system — the nuclear option of financial sanctions |
| Secondary sanctions | Any foreign entity doing business with Iran risks losing access to US financial system |
| Oil export restrictions | Reduced Iran's primary revenue source |
| Asset freezes | Iranian government and central bank assets held abroad |
| Designation lists | Specific entities (IRGC, banks, individuals) sanctioned |

### The SWIFT Weapon

The 2012 disconnection of Iranian banks from SWIFT (Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication) was unprecedented — the first time a country was cut off from the global financial messaging system. It made routine international transactions nearly impossible and demonstrated that the US-dominated financial architecture could be weaponized.

## Economic Impact

### Macroeconomic

| Indicator | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Currency | Iranian rial collapsed — lost ~80% of value under maximum pressure |
| Inflation | Severe, particularly food and medicine |
| GDP | Major contraction |
| Oil exports | From ~2.5 million bpd to under 500,000 bpd at trough |
| Foreign investment | Effectively zero |

### Who Sanctions Actually Hurt

| Group | Effect | Political consequence |
|-------|--------|----------------------|
| **Middle class** | Squeezed hardest — savings evaporated, businesses closed, brain drain accelerated | The West's "best ally" in Iran weakened |
| **Moderates/reformists** | Engagement strategy discredited; couldn't deliver economic results | Hardliners vindicated |
| **Private sector** | Couldn't navigate sanctions regime | IRGC-linked entities filled the vacuum |
| **[[IRGC]]** | Controlled smuggling networks, government contracts, hard currency access | *Profited* from sanctions |
| **Regime elites** | Insulated by parallel economy, bonyads, black market | Survived |

The former Secretary of State Mike Pompeo articulated the theory: Iran should "choose between feeding its own people or continuing the policies that are problematic." Vaez's assessment: this "turned the concept on its head — weakened our best allies and strengthened our worst adversaries."

## The Pattern of Broken Promises

Each failed reciprocity made the next diplomatic opening more fragile:

| Episode | Promise | Outcome |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Algiers Accord (1981) | US: non-interference, return frozen assets | Neither fully delivered |
| Lebanon hostages (1990s) | Iran helped release US hostages | Bush Sr. didn't reciprocate |
| Clinton era | Iran offered oil contract to US companies | Clinton imposed sanctions instead (ILSA) |
| Post-9/11 cooperation | Iran provided intelligence, [[Qasem Soleimani\|Soleimani]] prepared Afghanistan for US jets | Bush named Iran in "Axis of Evil" (2002) |
| JCPOA (2015) | Nuclear restrictions for sanctions relief | Obama didn't fully deliver relief; Trump withdrew entirely |
| Biden prisoner deal | $6B of Iranian assets unfrozen (South Korea → Doha) | Biden froze access after October 7th, though money was unrelated to proxies |

This history is why, by the 2020s, no Iranian official trusted American commitments — and why "the only thing more perilous than suffering a US strike would be surrendering to US terms."

## Comparative Context

### Sanctions as a Tool

| Case | Target | Duration | Outcome |
|------|--------|----------|---------|
| Iran | Regime change / behavior change | 45+ years | Regime survived; behavior worsened |
| Iraq (1990–2003) | Containment of Saddam | 13 years | Society destroyed; regime survived until invasion |
| South Africa | Apartheid | ~20 years | Regime changed (but many other factors) |
| Cuba | Regime change | 60+ years | Regime survived |
| Russia (post-2014) | Behavior change | 10+ years | War expanded |
| Libya (1990s) | Behavior change | ~10 years | Gaddafi gave up WMD; later overthrown anyway |
| North Korea | Denuclearization | 20+ years | Became a nuclear power |

The pattern: sanctions rarely achieve regime change or major behavior change in authoritarian states with alternative economic networks. They are most effective when paired with credible diplomatic off-ramps — precisely what maximum pressure eliminated.

## The Nuclear Lessons

Iran's nuclear calculations were shaped by watching other sanctioned or threatened states:

| Country | Lesson for Iran |
|---------|----------------|
| Iraq | Had no nuclear weapons → invaded and destroyed (2003) |
| Libya | Gave up nuclear program → Gaddafi overthrown, killed in a ditch |
| North Korea | Built nuclear weapons → treated with "tremendous respect" by Trump |
| Ukraine | Gave up nuclear arsenal for security guarantees → invaded by Russia |

Every precedent pointed in the same direction: nuclear weapons are the only reliable deterrent against regime change.

## The Deeper Point

Iran sanctions represent the most sustained experiment in economic coercion in modern history — and the results are damning for sanctions as a primary policy tool against entrenched authoritarian regimes. The theory was straightforward: economic pain forces political change. The reality was the opposite: economic pain was absorbed by the civilian population while the security establishment profited from the conditions sanctions created.

The mechanism is structural. Sanctions disrupt formal economic channels, which hurts private businesses, the middle class, and anyone dependent on transparent markets. But they create enormous rents in informal channels — smuggling, sanctions evasion, black-market currency trading — that are controlled by the very security institutions (the [[IRGC]]) that sanctions are meant to weaken. Maximum pressure was, inadvertently, a massive subsidy to Iran's military-industrial complex.

The broader lesson connects to the [[Institutions and Progress|institutional]] framework of this vault: economic isolation doesn't just freeze development — it actively selects for the worst institutional arrangements. The Iranian middle class that was "the West's best ally" shrank not because of Iranian government policy but because of Western sanctions. The IRGC didn't grow powerful despite sanctions but *because of them*. This is the deepest irony of four decades of economic warfare: the policy designed to change Iran's institutions instead produced exactly the institutions it was designed to prevent.
