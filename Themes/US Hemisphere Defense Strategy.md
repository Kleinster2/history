# US Hemisphere Defense Strategy

The United States has maintained some form of Western Hemisphere defense doctrine since 1823 -- the longest continuous strategic commitment in American history. The arc runs from James Monroe's declaratory warning (enforced by someone else's navy), through Theodore Roosevelt's conversion of that warning into an interventionist license, to the pre-WWII "quarter sphere" defensive perimeter, through Cold War covert operations, and into the 2020s revival. Each phase reflects the balance between American ambitions and American capabilities at a given moment.

## The Monroe Doctrine (1823)

### Context

By the early 1820s, most of Spain's American colonies had achieved or were achieving independence. Mexico (1821), Peru (1821), Gran Colombia (1819), Chile (1818), Argentina (1816), and Brazil (from Portugal, 1822) had all broken free. The United States, itself only four decades old, saw a hemisphere of fellow republics emerging -- and feared that the [[Imperialism|European monarchies]] might try to restore colonial control.

The threat was not hypothetical. The Congress of Verona (November 1822) saw France, Austria, Prussia, and Russia -- the "Holy Alliance" powers -- discuss intervention to restore Spanish authority in the Americas. France had already intervened in Spain itself in 1823 to restore Ferdinand VII to absolute power (the "Hundred Thousand Sons of Saint Louis"). An extension of this interventionism across the Atlantic seemed plausible.

Russia presented a separate concern: Tsar Alexander I had issued a ukase in September 1821 claiming exclusive Russian rights along the Pacific coast down to the 51st parallel, extending Russian America's reach southward.

### Canning's Proposal

In August 1823, British Foreign Secretary George Canning approached Richard Rush, the American minister in London, with a proposal for a joint Anglo-American declaration. Canning suggested three points: (1) Spain could not recover its former colonies; (2) neither Britain nor the US would seek to acquire any of those territories; (3) neither would obstruct peace between Spain and the new states.

Canning's motives were largely commercial. Britain had become the dominant trading partner with the newly independent Latin American states and wanted to prevent any restoration of Spain's mercantilist monopoly. A joint declaration would also constrain American territorial ambitions -- which is precisely why Secretary of State [[John Quincy Adams]] opposed it.

### Adams's Rejection

Adams argued forcefully against Canning's proposal in cabinet discussions. His reasoning, as recorded in his diary, was strategic:

- He did not think it dignified for the United States "to come in as a cock-boat in the wake of the British man-of-war"
- He believed Britain wanted the joint declaration not to deter the Holy Alliance but to prevent the United States from acquiring former Spanish territories (particularly Cuba and Texas)
- He recognized that Britain would act to prevent European reconquest regardless of American participation -- because it served British commercial interests
- He argued the United States should make its own unilateral statement

Former presidents Jefferson and Madison, consulted by Monroe, actually favored accepting Canning's offer. Adams prevailed.

### The Declaration

On December 2, 1823, in his seventh annual message to Congress, President Monroe articulated the doctrine (drafted primarily by Adams). The key passages:

> "The American continents, by the free and independent condition which they have assumed and maintain, are henceforth not to be considered as subjects for future colonization by any European powers."

> "We owe it, therefore, to candor and to the amicable relations existing between the United States and those powers to declare that we should consider any attempt on their part to extend their system to any portion of this hemisphere as dangerous to our peace and safety."

### Four Principles
1. Non-colonization: No new European colonies in the Americas
2. Non-intervention: European powers must not interfere in independent American states
3. Non-interference: The US pledges not to interfere in existing European colonies or in European affairs
4. Separate spheres: The political systems of the Americas and Europe are fundamentally distinct

### The Enforcement Problem

The Monroe Doctrine was a bluff backed by someone else's fleet. In 1823, the US Navy had fewer than a dozen significant warships. The doctrine's real enforcement mechanism was the Royal Navy, which patrolled Atlantic and Caribbean waters to protect British commercial interests. This convergence of British and American interests -- preventing European reconquest -- meant the doctrine functioned for decades without the US needing to enforce it directly.

When the doctrine was tested, it was often found wanting. France intervened in Mexico in 1838 (the Pastry War) and again in 1861-67, installing Maximilian as emperor -- the most flagrant violation of Monroe's principles. The US, consumed by its own Civil War, could do nothing until 1865, when it massed troops on the Mexican border and pressured Napoleon III to withdraw.

## Roosevelt Corollary (1904)

### The Venezuela Trigger

The Venezuelan crisis of 1902-03 transformed the Monroe Doctrine from defensive shield to interventionist warrant. President Cipriano Castro had refused to pay Venezuela's foreign debts and compensate European nationals for losses in civil wars. In December 1902, Great Britain, Germany, and Italy imposed a naval blockade, seized Venezuelan vessels, and bombarded coastal forts.

President Theodore Roosevelt pressured all parties toward arbitration, and by February 1903 Venezuela agreed to reserve 30% of customs duties for debt repayment. The critical aftermath: on February 22, 1904, the Permanent Court of Arbitration at The Hague ruled that the blockading powers were entitled to preferential treatment in debt claims. This created a perverse incentive -- European powers could gain advantages by using force against Latin American debtors.

### Roosevelt's Annual Message (December 6, 1904)

In his fourth annual message to Congress, Roosevelt announced what became known as the Roosevelt Corollary:

> "Chronic wrongdoing, or an impotence which results in a general loosening of the ties of civilized society, may in America, as elsewhere, ultimately require intervention by some civilized nation, and in the Western Hemisphere the adherence of the United States to the Monroe Doctrine may force the United States, however reluctantly, in flagrant cases of such wrongdoing or impotence, to the exercise of an international police power."

### The Transformation

This inverted Monroe's logic completely. Monroe had said: Europe, stay out. Roosevelt said: We will go in -- to prevent Europe from having reason to go in. Defensive became offensive. Non-intervention became intervention. The doctrine shifted from protecting Latin American sovereignty to justifying its violation.

### The Banana Wars (1898-1934)

The Roosevelt Corollary and its afterlife justified a generation of Caribbean and Central American interventions:

| Country | Dates | Form |
|---------|-------|------|
| Cuba | 1898-1902, 1906-1909 | Military occupation, Platt Amendment |
| Panama | 1903 | Engineered independence from Colombia |
| Dominican Republic | 1905, 1916-1924 | Customs receivership, then full military occupation |
| Nicaragua | 1909-1910, 1912-1925, 1926-1933 | Multiple occupations; Augusto Sandino insurgency |
| Haiti | 1915-1934 | Military occupation, 19 years |
| Honduras | 1903, 1907, 1911, 1912, 1919, 1924, 1925 | Repeated short interventions |
| Mexico | 1914 (Veracruz), 1916-17 (Punitive Expedition) | Limited interventions |

## Dollar Diplomacy (1909-1913)

President William Howard Taft and Secretary of State Philander Knox attempted to substitute economic for military leverage. The idea: replace European loans to Latin American and Caribbean states with American private bank loans, giving the US financial control without military occupation. Taft called it "substituting dollars for bullets."

In practice, Dollar Diplomacy often led to more intervention, not less. When Nicaragua defaulted on American-arranged loans, US Marines were sent in (1912). The approach treated Latin American states as financial protectorates -- sovereignty constrained by debt obligations to American banks, enforced by American troops when borrowers failed to perform.

## Clark Memorandum (1928)

On December 17, 1928, Undersecretary of State J. Reuben Clark completed a 236-page memorandum on the Monroe Doctrine for Secretary of State Frank Kellogg. The document was kept secret until the Hoover administration published it in 1930.

Clark's key argument: the Roosevelt Corollary was not a legitimate extension of the Monroe Doctrine. The Monroe Doctrine addressed conflicts between the United States and European powers over their conduct in the Americas. It said nothing about US relations with Latin American states themselves. Therefore, the Roosevelt Corollary -- which claimed a right to intervene in Latin American domestic affairs -- had no basis in Monroe's original principles.

This was a careful legal and historical distinction, not an anti-interventionist manifesto. Clark did not say the US lacked the right to intervene -- only that such a right derived from general principles of international law (self-defense, protection of nationals), not from the Monroe Doctrine specifically. But the practical effect was significant: it separated the most toxic element (interventionism) from the doctrinal framework, clearing the path for FDR's Good Neighbor Policy.

## Good Neighbor Policy (1933-1945)

### FDR's Pledge

In his inaugural address on March 4, 1933, Roosevelt declared:

> "In the field of world policy I would dedicate this Nation to the policy of the good neighbor -- the neighbor who resolutely respects himself and, because he does so, respects the rights of others."

At the Seventh International Conference of American States in Montevideo (December 1933), Secretary of State Cordell Hull signed the Convention on the Rights and Duties of States, which included Article 8: "No state has the right to intervene in the internal or external affairs of another." The US ratified it with reservations, but the direction was clear.

### In Practice

The Good Neighbor Policy manifested as:
- Abrogation of the Platt Amendment (1934), ending the legal right to intervene in Cuba
- Withdrawal of Marines from Haiti (1934) and Nicaragua (completed 1933)
- Non-intervention when Mexico nationalized American oil companies (1938) -- Roosevelt chose negotiation over gunboats
- Reciprocal Trade Agreements Act (1934), reducing tariffs bilaterally
- Export-Import Bank loans as soft-power tools
- Cultural diplomacy (Nelson Rockefeller's Office of Inter-American Affairs)

The policy was partly strategic: with European war approaching, the US needed Latin American cooperation. The 1936 Buenos Aires Conference produced a mutual consultation pact, and the 1938 Declaration of Lima affirmed hemispheric solidarity. These frameworks proved essential when war came.

## Quarter Sphere Defense (1938-1941)

### The Munich Trigger

The September 1938 Munich crisis fundamentally reoriented American defense planning. The realization was technological: modern land-based air power could, for the first time, threaten the continental United States from bases in the Western Hemisphere. German or Japanese forces operating from airfields in Brazil's northeastern bulge, the Caribbean, or Mexico could reach American cities.

On September 12, 1938, after listening to Hitler's broadcast, President Roosevelt directed Harry Hopkins to survey the West Coast aviation industry's capacity for expansion. On November 14, 1938, Roosevelt convened a conference that set a goal of 10,000 military aircraft and declared that America's defense "machine...was weakest in Army planes." He demanded "a large air force in being to protect any part of the North or South American continent."

Intelligence reports amplified the alarm: Japanese "fishing" reconnaissance along the Mexican and Central American Pacific coasts, German interest in Saman Bay (Dominican Republic), and German plots to foment revolutions in Brazil, Uruguay, and Argentina.

### The North-South Axis

Assistant Secretary of State Adolf A. Berle termed the emerging diplomatic strategy the "north-south axis" -- building Pan-American cooperation as a strategic counterweight to European instability. This produced the Declaration of Lima (December 1938) and the Panama Conference (September-October 1939), which declared a 300-mile neutrality zone around the Americas.

### Rainbow War Plans (1939)

In June 1939, the Joint Board directed development of five new war plans, designated "RAINBOW" to distinguish them from the older single-color plans (War Plan Orange for Japan, etc.). Each assumed a different strategic scenario:

| Plan | Scenario | Hemisphere Scope |
|------|----------|------------------|
| Rainbow 1 | US alone, defensive | North of 10deg S latitude |
| Rainbow 2 | US allied with France/UK | Same as Rainbow 1 |
| Rainbow 3 | US alone | Secure Western Hemisphere, then advance westward to Pacific |
| Rainbow 4 | US alone, full hemisphere | Entire Western Hemisphere |
| Rainbow 5 | US allied with UK/France | Hemisphere defense + offensive into Europe/Africa |

Rainbow 1, approved August 14, 1939, defined the "quarter sphere" -- the defensive perimeter north of latitude 10 degrees south, a line bisecting South America just below the Peruvian and Brazilian bulges. This was the minimum viable defense: protect the continental US and prevent hostile air bases from being established within striking range.

Rainbow 4 extended the commitment to the full hemisphere. Rainbow 5, which eventually became the basis for actual wartime strategy, added transatlantic offensive operations.

### Key Geography

The strategic logic centered on two points:
- The "bulge of Brazil" at Natal -- the closest point in the Americas to Africa (approximately 1,600 miles to Dakar). A hostile air base at Natal could threaten the entire South Atlantic and serve as a stepping stone from Africa. Conn and Fairchild document that "a base at Natal was essential for the effective defense of the South American continent."
- The Galapagos Islands -- approximately 1,000 miles southwest of Panama, controlling Pacific approaches to the Canal Zone.

### Marshall and the Transition

General George C. Marshall became Army Chief of Staff on September 1, 1939 -- the day Germany invaded Poland. In February 1940, Marshall warned Congress: "If Europe blazes in the late spring or summer, we must put our house in order."

After the fall of France (June 1940), hemisphere defense became urgent. The Act of Havana (July 1940) authorized collective action against any transfer of European colonies in the Americas to another European power -- directly addressing the risk that German-occupied France and the Netherlands might lose their Caribbean and South American colonies to Germany. The Destroyers-for-Bases Agreement (September 1940) gave the US basing rights in British Caribbean territories.

### Superseded by Global War

Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941) rendered the quarter-sphere concept obsolete within hours. The US shifted from hemisphere defense to global offensive strategy. Rainbow 5 -- the plan predicated on alliance with Britain and offensive operations in Europe -- became the operative framework. The quarter-sphere defensive perimeter was never tested as a standalone strategy.

The authoritative source on this period is Stetson Conn and Byron Fairchild, *The Framework of Hemisphere Defense* (Washington: Office of the Chief of Military History, 1960), CMH Pub 4-1, part of the "United States Army in World War II" official history series.

## Cold War Hemisphere Operations (1947-1991)

### Institutional Architecture

The postwar hemisphere defense system was formalized through two instruments:

The Inter-American Treaty of Reciprocal Assistance (Rio Treaty), signed September 2, 1947, established collective defense: an attack on one signatory was an attack on all. Signed by 21 American states, it was the first collective defense treaty the US entered after WWII -- predating NATO (1949) by two years.

The Organization of American States (OAS), chartered April 30, 1948, at the Ninth International Conference of American States in Bogota, Colombia. Secretary of State George Marshall led the US delegation. The OAS provided the institutional framework for hemispheric cooperation, dispute resolution, and -- in practice -- legitimation of US anti-communist policies.

### Monroe Doctrine as Anti-Communist Shield

The Cold War recast the Monroe Doctrine: keeping European powers out became keeping Soviet influence out. The methods shifted from overt military occupation to covert operations, proxy forces, and economic pressure.

| Operation | Year | Method | Outcome |
|-----------|------|--------|---------|
| Guatemala | 1954 | CIA coup (Operation PBSUCCESS) | Overthrew Jacobo Arbenz; installed Carlos Castillo Armas |
| Cuba | 1961 | CIA-organized invasion (Bay of Pigs) | Failed; strengthened Castro |
| Dominican Republic | 1965 | 42,000 US troops (Operation Power Pack) | Prevented leftist government; OAS provided cover |
| Chile | 1973 | CIA covert destabilization | Pinochet coup; Allende killed |
| Grenada | 1983 | Military invasion (Operation Urgent Fury) | Removed Marxist government |
| Panama | 1989 | Military invasion (Operation Just Cause) | Captured Noriega |
| Central America | 1980s | Reagan Doctrine: Contra funding, El Salvador military aid | Prolonged civil wars; Iran-Contra scandal |

### SOUTHCOM

The US military command structure for Latin America evolved from the Caribbean Command (established 1947, based in the Panama Canal Zone) to United States Southern Command (SOUTHCOM), formally redesignated June 6, 1963, by President Kennedy and Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara. The renaming reflected the shift in focus from Caribbean to continent-wide operations. SOUTHCOM was elevated to a four-star command at the same time.

SOUTHCOM's missions expanded beyond Canal defense to include contingency planning against communist expansion, administration of the Military Assistance Program in Latin America, and civic action projects under the Alliance for Progress. The command relocated from Panama to Miami (Doral, Florida) in 1997 after the US returned the Canal Zone.

## Post-Cold War Decline (1991-2016)

With the Soviet threat eliminated, hemisphere defense lost its strategic rationale. The 1990s and 2000s saw:

- SOUTHCOM's mission shifted to counter-narcotics, disaster relief, and partnership building
- The Monroe Doctrine was widely considered obsolete -- Secretary of State John Kerry declared in November 2013: "The era of the Monroe Doctrine is over"
- Multilateralism and institutional frameworks (OAS, Summits of the Americas) replaced unilateral action
- China's economic engagement in Latin America expanded with limited US objection -- bilateral trade grew from $12 billion (2000) to $315 billion (2022)
- Russia maintained limited military engagement (arms sales to Venezuela, Cuba, Nicaragua) without triggering Monroe-style responses
- The "Pink Tide" of left-wing governments (Chavez, Morales, Correa, Kirchner, Lula) diverged from US preferences without provoking intervention

## Revival (2017-Present)

### Trump 1.0 (2017-2021)

Secretary of State Rex Tillerson invoked the Monroe Doctrine by name in February 2018, calling it "as relevant today as it was the day it was written." National Security Adviser John Bolton declared the "Monroe Doctrine is alive and well" in 2019, explicitly targeting Cuba, Venezuela, and Nicaragua as a "troika of tyranny."

### Trump 2.0 (2025-2026)

The 2025-2026 period represents the most aggressive reassertion of hemisphere dominance since the Banana Wars era. Key developments:

- US Army Western Hemisphere Command (USAWHC) activated December 5, 2025, at Fort Bragg, North Carolina. Gen. Joseph A. Ryan commanding. USAWHC serves as theater army for both NORTHCOM and SOUTHCOM -- effectively unifying the hemisphere under one Army command for the first time. Initial operating capability February 2026; full capability summer 2026.
- Secretary of War Pete Hegseth's "Greater North America" strategic map (March 2026), defining an immediate security perimeter from Greenland to Ecuador, north of the equator
- Hegseth explicitly invoked the WWII quarter-sphere defense parallel: "This is what we did in World War II. We called it the 'quarter-sphere defense,' and we will do this again"
- Venezuela intervention (Operation Absolute Resolve) -- direct military capture of Maduro
- The "Donroe Doctrine" -- Trump's personalized fusion of Monroe Doctrine principles with unilateral force

## Patterns Across Two Centuries

| Phase | Dates | Posture | Enforcement | Real Capability |
|-------|-------|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Monroe Doctrine | 1823-1898 | Declaratory, defensive | Royal Navy (not US) | Minimal |
| Roosevelt Corollary | 1904-1928 | Interventionist | US Marines, Navy | Regional |
| Good Neighbor | 1933-1941 | Non-intervention | Diplomacy, trade | Growing |
| Quarter Sphere | 1938-1941 | Defensive perimeter | Air power planning | Continental |
| Cold War | 1947-1991 | Anti-communist | CIA, proxies, invasions | Global |
| Post-Cold War | 1991-2016 | Dormant | Counter-narcotics, aid | Overwhelming but unused |
| Revival | 2017-present | Assertive, unilateral | Direct military action | Overwhelming and used |

The constant: the United States has never accepted a peer competitor's strategic presence in the Western Hemisphere. The variable: whether it acts defensively (Monroe), interventionally (Roosevelt), cooperatively (FDR), or unilaterally (Trump).
