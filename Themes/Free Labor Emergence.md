# Free Labor Emergence

When and why did wage labor become dominant? Free labor emerged where institutions constrained coercion—it wasn't morally inevitable, but institutionally determined. From medieval towns where "city air makes free" to industrial factories with wage contracts, the transition from bound to free labor required specific conditions: alternatives for workers, constraints on employers, and legal frameworks recognizing labor contracts.

## The Deeper Point

Free labor emerged where [[Institutions and Progress|institutions constrained coercion]]—it wasn't morally inevitable, but institutionally determined. Medieval towns offered freedom because they needed workers and had legal autonomy. Post-plague bargaining power shifted where workers had alternatives; [[Second Serfdom|Eastern Europe]] intensified bondage because nobles controlled alternatives. [[Proto-industrialization|Proto-industry]] created piece-rate systems that were formally free but economically constrained. Factory wages became dominant because industrial production required the discipline that bondage couldn't provide—machines needed attendance, quality required motivation. The triumph of free labor wasn't automatic or universal. It required institutional conditions: towns that could shelter runaways, legal frameworks recognizing contracts, competition among employers, and eventually political voice for workers. Where these conditions existed, labor markets emerged; where they didn't, coercion persisted. [[Labor and Unions|Labor movements]] fought to expand the conditions of freedom—limiting hours, requiring safety, enabling collective bargaining—recognizing that formal freedom without power was incomplete. The struggle between free and coerced labor continues: trafficking, forced labor, and exploitative conditions persist where institutions fail to constrain coercion.
