# Adele Spitzeder

Adelheid Luise "Adele" Spitzeder was a German actress who ran what may be the first documented large-scale [[Ponzi Schemes|Ponzi scheme]]. Her Spitzedersche Privatbank (known as the "Dachauer Bank") operated in Munich from 1869 to 1872, promising 10% monthly returns to working-class depositors. At its peak, she was considered the wealthiest woman in Bavaria. When it collapsed, 32,000 victims had lost 38 million gulden (~$430 million today), triggering a wave of suicides.

## The Deeper Point

Adele Spitzeder's scheme reveals that Ponzi fraud is not a 20th-century American invention but a universal pattern that emerges wherever three conditions exist: capital accumulation among ordinary people, inadequate regulation, and information asymmetry. In 1869 Bavaria, workers had savings but few safe investment options. Spitzeder offered what banks couldn't: high returns and accessibility.

Her innovation—acquiring newspapers to control coverage—anticipates modern reputation management. She understood that a Ponzi scheme lives on confidence, and confidence lives on narrative. By owning the press that covered her, she extended the scheme's life and expanded its reach. Only when mathematical inevitability (withdrawals > deposits) overtook narrative control did the scheme collapse.

The human cost—32,000 victims, waves of suicides, only 15% recovery—establishes the template for all subsequent Ponzis. The operator lives well while the scheme grows; victims face ruin when it ends. That Spitzeder served less than four years for destroying thousands of lives reflects both the era's legal inadequacy and the recurring pattern of fraudsters escaping proportionate punishment.
