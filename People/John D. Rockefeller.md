# John D. Rockefeller

John Davison Rockefeller built Standard Oil from a single Cleveland refinery into America's most powerful monopoly, controlling 90% of the nation's oil refining by 1880 through systematic consolidation, railroad rebates, and predatory pricing. [[Ida Tarbell]]'s exposé (1904) turned public opinion against him, and the Supreme Court ordered Standard Oil broken into 34 companies in 1911—but Rockefeller, holding proportional shares in every fragment, emerged richer than before, his fortune peaking near $900 million. He spent his last decades giving away $540 million through the Rockefeller Foundation and allied institutions, remaking his reputation from robber baron to philanthropist and establishing the template for industrial-scale giving that persists through Gates and Buffett today.

## Standard Oil Empire

### Entry into Oil

| Date | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1859 | Drake well at Titusville, PA — oil boom begins |
| 1863 | Rockefeller enters refining with Samuel Andrews |
| 1865 | Buys out Clark, gains full control |
| 1870 | Founds Standard Oil of Ohio, capitalized at $1 million |

### The Cleveland Massacre (1872)

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Context | South Improvement Company — secret railroad rebate scheme |
| Method | Offered competitors a choice: sell to Standard or be crushed |
| Result | Bought 22 of 26 Cleveland refiners in weeks |
| Leverage | Guaranteed railroad volumes in exchange for rebates and drawbacks |
| Drawbacks | Standard received rebates on competitors' shipments too |

### Building the Monopoly

| Year | Market share (refining) |
|------|------------------------|
| 1870 | 4% |
| 1874 | 25% |
| 1879 | 90% |
| 1880 | 90%+ |

| Method | Application |
|--------|-------------|
| Railroad rebates | Volume discounts, drawbacks on competitors |
| Predatory pricing | Undercut rivals in target markets, subsidized by profits elsewhere |
| Pipeline control | Built own pipelines, squeezed independents |
| Intelligence | Spied on competitors' shipments via railroad allies |
| Acquisition | Bought competitors at distressed prices |
| Vertical integration | Refineries, pipelines, barrel-making, shipping, marketing |

## Exposure and Breakup

### Ida Tarbell's Investigation

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Publication | *The History of the Standard Oil Company*, McClure's Magazine |
| Duration | 19 installments (1902-1904) |
| Method | Court records, testimony, interviews, Standard's own documents |
| Key revelations | South Improvement Company, rebate system, predatory pricing, espionage |
| Personal dimension | [[Ida Tarbell]]'s father was an Oil Region producer ruined by Standard |
| Impact | Transformed public opinion, built the political case for antitrust action |

### Standard Oil v. United States (1911)

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Filed | 1906 (Roosevelt administration) |
| Decided | May 15, 1911 |
| Ruling | Broken into 34 independent companies |
| Legal principle | "Rule of reason" — not all restraints illegal, only unreasonable ones |
| Successor companies | Jersey Standard (→ Exxon), Standard of New York (→ Mobil), Standard of California (→ Chevron), Standard of Indiana (→ Amoco), Standard of Ohio (→ Sohio/BP) |

### The Breakup Paradox

| Before breakup | After breakup |
|----------------|---------------|
| Rockefeller owned ~25% of Standard Oil | Rockefeller owned ~25% of each of 34 companies |
| Single monopoly, limited growth | 34 companies competing, all growing |
| Combined value constrained | Combined market cap exceeded original |
| Wealth estimate: ~$300 million | Wealth peaked: ~$900 million (1913) |

The breakup made Rockefeller richer because the fragments, freed to compete and grow independently, became worth more than the constrained whole. The same logic would apply to AT&T's 1984 breakup and is invoked in modern tech antitrust arguments.

### Philanthropy vs. Confiscation

Rockefeller faced the same question Carnegie articulated in the [[Gospel of Wealth]]: should the rich give voluntarily, or should society take? His answer was both strategic and sincere—the Baptist who tithed from age 16 genuinely believed in stewardship, but the industrialist who hired Ivy Lee (pioneer of public relations) also understood that philanthropy was reputation insurance. The Rockefeller Foundation was announced in 1913, two years after the breakup—the timing was not accidental.

## The Deeper Point

Rockefeller is the purest test case for whether monopoly and progress are compatible. Standard Oil genuinely reduced the price of kerosene—from 26 cents per gallon in 1870 to 8 cents by 1885—bringing light to millions of homes. His obsession with efficiency eliminated waste: Standard used every byproduct, standardized every process, and drove costs below what any competitor could match. By the consumer welfare standard that the [[Antitrust History|Chicago School]] would later champion, Standard Oil was a success.

But the methods that achieved this efficiency—rebates, espionage, predatory pricing, the systematic destruction of independent producers—demonstrated that [[Creative Destruction|creative destruction]] can be weaponized. Rockefeller didn't just outcompete rivals; he used structural advantages (railroad leverage, pipeline control, intelligence networks) to make competition itself impossible. The distinction matters: efficiency that comes from doing things better is productive; efficiency that comes from eliminating the possibility of alternatives is extractive, even if prices fall in the short run.

The breakup paradox—that dismantling the monopoly made its creator richer—reveals something important about the relationship between [[Antitrust History|antitrust]] and wealth. Antitrust doesn't confiscate; it restructures. The 34 Standard Oil fragments competed, innovated, and grew in ways the unified monopoly never would have. Rockefeller's personal fortune was a side effect of unleashing the competitive energy his monopoly had suppressed. The same logic applies to every subsequent breakup debate: the question isn't whether the monopolist is competent, but whether the system produces more value with or without competition.

His philanthropy established a pattern that persists: the [[Gilded Age]] fortune converted to institutional permanence through foundations. The Rockefeller Foundation didn't just give money away—it professionalized giving, applying the same systematic methods to philanthropy that Standard Oil had applied to refining. Gates and Buffett's Giving Pledge is a direct descendant of the model Rockefeller and [[Andrew Carnegie]] pioneered: the mega-fortune as a trust to be managed, not a hoard to be kept.
