# William Miller

William "520 Percent" Miller was a Brooklyn bookkeeper who in 1899 ran one of America's largest pre-Ponzi [[Ponzi Schemes|Ponzi schemes]]. His Franklin Syndicate promised 10% weekly returns—520% annually—and defrauded 13,000 investors of over $1 million before collapsing. Two decades later, the *Boston Post* interviewed Miller during its investigation of [[Charles Ponzi]], finding the schemes "remarkably similar."

## The Deeper Point

William Miller proves that the Ponzi structure is not about individual genius but about exploiting permanent features of human psychology: the desire for high returns, the power of social proof, and the tendency to believe what we want to believe. Miller was a 21-year-old bookkeeper with no special skills—just the insight that paying early investors creates credibility.

That Ponzi's name attached to the scheme while Miller was forgotten reflects media dynamics more than innovation. Ponzi operated in the era of national wire services and aggressive newspaper competition; Miller operated in local Brooklyn before mass media amplified financial scandals. The structure was identical; the publicity was not.

Miller's post-prison trajectory—a quiet grocery store on Long Island—contrasts with Ponzi's continued scheming (Florida land fraud) and eventual penniless death. Perhaps the smaller-scale fraudster found it easier to disappear into ordinary life. The larger lesson is that these schemes recur not because operators are sophisticated but because the conditions that enable them (regulatory gaps, community trust, hunger for returns) persist.
