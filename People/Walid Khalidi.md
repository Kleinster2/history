# Walid Khalidi

Walid Khalidi — scion of one of Jerusalem's oldest families, Oxford-educated, lifelong scholar of Palestinian history — spent seven decades reconstructing the society that was destroyed in 1948. Born in Mandate Palestine, displaced by the war that created Israel, he co-founded the Institute for Palestine Studies (1963) and produced the definitive documentation of what was lost: *All That Remains* (1992) cataloged 418 Palestinian villages depopulated and destroyed during the 1948 war, recording their populations, economies, architecture, and fates with the methodical precision of a civilization's inventory clerk. His earlier *Before Their Diaspora* (1984) assembled the photographic record of Palestinian life from 1876 to 1948 — urban professionals, citrus groves, schools, markets — against the narrative that Palestine had been empty land awaiting settlement. From the American University of Beirut to Harvard, Khalidi built the scholarly infrastructure of Palestinian historical memory while advocating a two-state solution decades before it became international consensus. He died in 2023, having outlived most of the world he documented but not the conflict that destroyed it.

## The Khalidi Family and Jerusalem's Institutional Capital

The al-Khalidi family represents centuries of accumulated institutional capital in Jerusalem. Under Ottoman governance, family members served as Islamic judges (*qadis*), scholars, diplomats, and municipal leaders. The Khalidi Library (*Maktabat al-Khalidi*), formally established around 1900 in the Old City, housed manuscripts and books collected over generations — one of the oldest family libraries in the Middle East.

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Documented presence | At least 13th century onward |
| Ottoman-era roles | Judges, scholars, municipal officials |
| Khalidi Library | Old City of Jerusalem; manuscripts spanning centuries |
| Intellectual tradition | Islamic jurisprudence, literature, diplomacy |
| 1948 disruption | Family dispersed; library survived under difficult conditions |

This was not a family of peasant farmers. The Khalidis embodied the urban, educated, institutional infrastructure of Palestinian society — the kind of multi-generational human capital that takes centuries to accumulate and moments to disperse.

## The Palestine He Documented

Khalidi's central scholarly project was reconstructing the economic and social reality of Palestine before 1948 — a society that political narratives on multiple sides had incentives to minimize or erase.

### Pre-1948 Palestinian Economy

| Sector | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Agriculture | Citrus (Jaffa oranges were a global export brand), olives, grain, tobacco |
| Urban economy | Merchants, professionals, craftsmen in Jerusalem, Jaffa, Haifa, Acre, Nablus |
| Education | Schools, colleges, newspapers, literary societies |
| Trade | Connected to broader [[Levant Trade]] networks; Mediterranean port cities |
| Finance | Banks, merchant credit networks, property markets |
| Labor | Organized labor movements by the 1930s |

### What *All That Remains* Recorded

*All That Remains: The Palestinian Villages Occupied and Depopulated by Israel in 1948* (1992) was the culmination of decades of research. Khalidi and his team documented 418 villages that were depopulated during and after the 1948 war.

| Category | What was recorded |
|----------|-------------------|
| Population | Village-by-village population figures from Mandate-era census and tax records |
| Economy | Agricultural output, crops, livestock, crafts, markets |
| Infrastructure | Schools, mosques, churches, clinics, roads |
| Architecture | Stone buildings, notable structures, layout |
| Fate | Whether destroyed, repurposed, or left in ruins |
| Current status | Israeli settlements or parks built on former village sites |

This was not polemic — it was archival reconstruction. Khalidi used British Mandate records, Ottoman tax registers, aerial photographs, and field surveys to build a database of a destroyed society.

## Institute for Palestine Studies

Khalidi co-founded the Institute for Palestine Studies (IPS) in Beirut in 1963 — the first independent, non-governmental research institution devoted to the Palestinian question. IPS publishes the *Journal of Palestine Studies* and has produced a substantial scholarly literature.

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Founded | 1963, Beirut |
| Type | Independent, non-profit research institute |
| Publication | *Journal of Palestine Studies* (quarterly, since 1971) |
| Function | Academic research, archival preservation, policy analysis |
| Significance | Created institutional infrastructure for Palestinian scholarship at a time when no Palestinian state existed to support it |

The founding of IPS reflected a fundamental insight: displaced peoples who lose their archives lose their history. Khalidi understood that without institutional scholarship, the Palestinian narrative would exist only as oral memory — vulnerable to erasure.

## The Economic Lens

### Displacement as Economic Destruction

Khalidi's work, read through the lens of economic history, documents one of the 20th century's sharpest disruptions of accumulated capital — not financial capital primarily, but the human, social, and institutional capital that underpins productive economies.

| Type of capital destroyed | What was lost |
|--------------------------|---------------|
| Agricultural | Orchards, terraced hillsides, irrigation systems built over generations |
| Human | Professionals, craftsmen, teachers, merchants dispersed across multiple countries |
| Institutional | Municipal governance, courts, schools, commercial networks |
| Social | Village-level cooperation, extended family economic units, trade relationships |
| Cultural | Libraries, manuscripts, architectural heritage, place-based knowledge |

The 1948 displacement didn't just move people — it destroyed the economic infrastructure they had built. Orchards require decades to mature. Trade networks require trust built over generations. Institutional knowledge — how the courts work, how water is allocated, how disputes are resolved — is not portable.

### The Diaspora Economy

The Palestinian response to dispossession followed a pattern seen in other displaced communities (Armenian, Jewish, overseas Chinese): massive investment in education as portable capital.

| Diaspora pattern | Palestinian experience |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Education emphasis | Among the highest literacy and university enrollment rates in the Arab world by the 1970s |
| Professional class | Engineers, doctors, teachers, academics throughout the Gulf states |
| Remittance networks | Diaspora Palestinians supported families in camps and occupied territories |
| Gulf state role | Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, UAE employed large Palestinian professional classes |
| 1990-91 shock | ~400,000 Palestinians expelled from Kuwait after PLO's Gulf War positioning |

### The Historiography as Economic Argument

Khalidi's scholarship made an implicit economic argument: Palestine before 1948 was not empty, undeveloped, or waiting for modernization. It had a functioning agricultural economy, an urban professional class, and institutional infrastructure. The destruction of this society was not the replacement of nothing with something — it was the destruction of one economy to build another.

This connects to broader [[Decolonization]] patterns: colonial and settler-colonial projects routinely required the erasure of existing economic systems, then narrated that erasure as "development."

## The Deeper Point

Khalidi's life work illuminates a recurring theme in economic history: the relationship between documentation and dispossession. Peoples who control the archive control the narrative, and the narrative shapes who is seen as having legitimate economic claims. The Ottoman land registers that Khalidi used to reconstruct village populations were the same documents that British and later Israeli authorities reinterpreted to classify land as "uncultivated" or "state-owned."

The economic destruction Khalidi documented was not incidental to the conflict — it was structural. Displacement destroys accumulated capital that took generations to build. The 418 villages cataloged in *All That Remains* represent not just lost homes but lost orchards, lost wells, lost trade relationships, lost institutional knowledge. This is the economic content of what Palestinians call the Nakba: not merely a political event but the destruction of an economic civilization.

The deeper parallel is to other cases where institutional and economic continuity was shattered by force — the destruction of [[Ottoman Decline|Ottoman-era]] urban society, the displacement of Armenians, the partition of India. In each case, the economic costs of displacement vastly exceeded what contemporaries acknowledged, because the destroyed capital was largely invisible: social networks, institutional knowledge, place-based expertise. Khalidi spent his career making the invisible visible.
