# Mohammad Mossadegh

Mohammad Mossadegh — aristocrat, Swiss-educated lawyer, weeping nationalist orator — became Iran's prime minister in 1951 and immediately nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company, seizing the most profitable British overseas asset and triggering the first great confrontation between a resource-rich developing nation and international capital. Britain responded with a global boycott of Iranian oil that strangled the economy; when political pressure failed, MI6 and the CIA executed Operation Ajax (August 1953), overthrowing Mossadegh and restoring [[Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi]] to full power. Mossadegh spent three years in prison, then fourteen under house arrest until his death in 1967. The coup preserved Western access to Iranian oil for a generation but poisoned Iranian politics permanently: it gave [[Ayatollah Khomeini]] the founding grievance for the [[Iranian Revolution]] twenty-six years later, turned "1953" into a synonym for Western betrayal across the developing world, and demonstrated that resource sovereignty would be punished by force — a lesson that shaped nationalization movements, OPEC's formation, and the politics of oil from Nasser's Egypt to Chávez's Venezuela.

## Background

### The Qajar Aristocrat

Mossadegh came from Iran's ruling class — his mother was a Qajar princess, his father a finance minister. This was no populist outsider; he was the establishment turning against the concession system that had humiliated Iran since the 1890s.

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Family | Qajar aristocracy — mother was granddaughter of a Shah |
| Education | European legal training (Paris, Neuchâtel) |
| Early career | Provincial governor by age 24; finance minister, foreign minister, governor of Azerbaijan |
| Political identity | Constitutional monarchist, Iranian nationalist, anti-foreign-concession |
| Style | Theatrical, emotional speeches; wept openly in parliament; held meetings from his bed (chronic illness) |

### The Concession Grievance

Iran's relationship with foreign oil companies was the defining political issue of the 20th century. The [[BP|D'Arcy Concession]] (1901) had given Britain control over three-quarters of Iran's territory for oil exploration, on terms that became progressively more insulting as the industry matured.

| Period | Iran's Oil Revenue | BP's Profit |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| 1901–33 | 16% of net profits | 84% |
| 1933 renegotiation | Fixed royalty per ton | Still vastly favorable |
| 1947–50 | ~£7 million/year | £100+ million after tax |
| Comparison | Aramco offered Saudi Arabia 50-50 in 1950 | Anglo-Iranian refused to match |

By 1951, the British government collected more in tax from Anglo-Iranian than Iran received in royalties from its own oil. The Abadan refinery — the world's largest — employed tens of thousands of Iranians in segregated conditions while British managers lived in luxury compounds.

## The Nationalization Crisis (1951-1953)

### The Act

On May 1, 1951, Mossadegh's government nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company. The law was overwhelmingly popular — the Majles (parliament) voted unanimously. Mossadegh framed it as the completion of a struggle that began with the Tobacco Protest of 1891: Iranians controlling Iranian resources.

### Britain's Response

Britain could not accept nationalization. Anglo-Iranian was the British government's single largest overseas investment (51% shareholder since [[BP|Churchill's 1914 purchase]]) and the Royal Navy's fuel supply.

| British action | Effect |
|----------------|--------|
| Global boycott of Iranian oil | No tanker would carry Iranian crude; refineries refused to process it |
| Naval blockade | HMS Mauritius patrolled the Gulf; threatened to seize any tanker loading Iranian oil |
| AIOC staff withdrawal | British technicians left Abadan; Iran lacked trained replacements |
| Frozen assets | Iranian assets in British banks seized |
| UN and ICJ appeals | Iran won at the ICJ (ruled it lacked jurisdiction, effectively siding with Iran's sovereignty) |
| World Bank mediation | Mossadegh rejected compromise — full nationalization was the only acceptable outcome |

### Economic Strangulation

The boycott worked. Iranian oil exports collapsed from ~700,000 bpd to nearly zero. Government revenue cratered. The economy deteriorated sharply.

| Indicator | Before nationalization (1950) | During crisis (1952-53) |
|-----------|------------------------------|------------------------|
| Oil exports | ~700,000 bpd | Near zero |
| Government revenue | Heavily oil-dependent | Collapsed |
| Inflation | Moderate | Rising |
| Political stability | Fragile coalition | Fragmenting |

Yet Mossadegh held on. His popularity was immense. He survived a royalist counter-coup attempt, won emergency powers from parliament, and broke diplomatic relations with Britain.

## Operation Ajax (August 1953)

### Why the CIA Joined

Britain had been trying to overthrow Mossadegh since 1951 but could not do it alone. The Eisenhower administration — more receptive to Cold War framing than Truman had been — was persuaded that Mossadegh's weakness could open the door to communist takeover via Iran's Tudeh Party.

| Actor | Motivation |
|-------|------------|
| MI6 (Operation Boot) | Restore British oil interests; punish nationalization |
| CIA (Operation Ajax) | Prevent Soviet influence; Cold War containment logic |
| Shah | Wanted full power; feared Mossadegh's popularity |
| Iranian military | Conservative officers opposed Mossadegh's leftward drift |
| Clergy (Ayatollah Kashani) | Initially supported nationalization; turned against Mossadegh |
| Tudeh Party | Communist party; growing but never controlled government |

### The Coup

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| August 15, 1953 | First coup attempt fails; Shah flees to Rome |
| August 16-18 | CIA organizes street mobs (paid $100,000+ to gang leaders, clergy, military officers); anti-Mossadegh demonstrations staged |
| August 19, 1953 | Coup succeeds; Mossadegh's house shelled; he surrenders |
| August 22, 1953 | Shah returns from Rome |

Kermit Roosevelt Jr. (Teddy Roosevelt's grandson) ran the CIA operation from Tehran. The total cost was approximately $1 million — perhaps the highest-return covert operation in American history, if measured by decades of subsequent access to Iranian oil.

### Aftermath

| Consequence | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Mossadegh tried | Military tribunal; 3 years solitary confinement, then house arrest until death (1967) |
| Oil settlement (1954) | International consortium replaced AIOC: BP got 40% (down from 100%), American majors got 40%, Shell 14%, CFP 6% |
| Shah's power | Consolidated absolute rule; SAVAK secret police established (1957) with CIA/Mossad assistance |
| Iran's oil revenue | Improved under 50-50 split, then better terms — but under foreign-controlled consortium |
| American influence | Replaced Britain as Iran's primary external patron |

## The Economic Lesson

### Resource Sovereignty vs. International Capital

Mossadegh's nationalization was the first major test of whether a developing country could control its own resources against the wishes of international capital backed by great-power military force. The answer, in 1953, was no.

| Dimension | What Mossadegh Demonstrated |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| Legal right | Iran's sovereignty over its resources was legally defensible (ICJ declined to override it) |
| Economic capacity | Iran lacked the technical expertise and market access to operate independently |
| Power asymmetry | Britain could shut Iran out of global oil markets unilaterally |
| Cold War overlay | Legitimate nationalism was reframed as communist threat to justify intervention |
| Precedent | Showed the limits of nationalization without alternative buyers or technical capacity |

### The Long Shadow

| Downstream consequence | Connection |
|------------------------|------------|
| Shah's dictatorship (1953-79) | Coup removed Iran's most popular democratic leader; Shah ruled by force |
| SAVAK repression | Secret police modeled on CIA/Mossad techniques |
| Anti-American sentiment | Every Iranian schoolchild learned about the coup; most Americans never heard of it |
| [[Iranian Revolution]] (1979) | Khomeini built his movement on the narrative of Western betrayal that began with 1953 |
| [[IRGC]] | The revolution's security apparatus was built to prevent another 1953 |
| OPEC (1960) | Nationalization movements across the developing world drew on Iran's experience |
| Resource nationalism | From Nasser's Suez Canal (1956) to Chávez's PDVSA — the pattern of seizing foreign-controlled assets |

### What If?

The counterfactual is impossible to prove but worth stating: Mossadegh was a constitutional democrat, not a revolutionary. Had nationalization succeeded — or had Britain accepted 50-50 terms as Aramco did in Saudi Arabia — Iran might have followed a very different trajectory: secular, democratic, oil-rich, and Western-aligned. Instead, the coup that preserved Western oil access for 26 years produced the revolution that ended it permanently.

## The Deeper Point

Mossadegh embodies the central tension in 20th-century economic history between resource sovereignty and the international system built to protect foreign capital. His nationalization was legally sound, democratically supported, and economically rational — Iran was receiving a fraction of the value of its own oil. But the international economic order of the 1950s had no mechanism for accommodating resource nationalism without force. The D'Arcy Concession's exploitative terms created the grievance; the boycott demonstrated that formal sovereignty meant nothing without market access; and the coup showed that the ultimate backstop of the concession system was military power.

The economic parallel to later resource conflicts is direct. When Nasser nationalized the Suez Canal (1956), when Libya nationalized its oil (1970), when OPEC imposed the embargo (1973) — all were drawing on the same logic Mossadegh articulated: that the wealth beneath your soil should belong to you. The difference was that by the 1970s, producer states had learned Mossadegh's lesson about the importance of collective action and alternative buyers. OPEC succeeded where Mossadegh failed because it operated as a cartel rather than a single vulnerable nation.

The deepest irony: the coup's architects believed they were defending the free market against state control. What they actually did was demonstrate that the "free market" in oil was underwritten by covert force — and that lesson radicalized a generation across the developing world.
