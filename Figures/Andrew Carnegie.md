# Andrew Carnegie

Andrew Carnegie emigrated from Scotland as a penniless 13-year-old, rose from bobbin boy to telegraph operator to railroad executive, then built Carnegie Steel into the world's most efficient producer through vertical integration and relentless cost-cutting—controlling everything from Mesabi Range ore mines through [[Henry Clay Frick]]'s coke fields to the finished rail. He crushed the Homestead Strike of 1892 with Pinkertons while vacationing in Scotland, articulated the [[Gospel of Wealth]] doctrine that the rich must give away their fortunes during their lifetimes, sold his empire to J.P. Morgan for $480 million in 1901 to create U.S. Steel, and spent his remaining 18 years distributing $350 million to libraries, universities, and peace causes—dying in 1919 with the First World War having shattered his belief that industrial progress would make war obsolete.

| Transport | Own railroad lines, ore boats on Great Lakes |
| Steel mills | Edgar Thomson, Homestead, Duquesne works |
| Finished products | Rails, structural steel, armor plate |

### The Frick Partnership

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Alliance | Carnegie acquired majority of Frick Coke Company (1881) |
| Division | Carnegie: strategy, finance, salesmanship; Frick: operations, labor |
| Value | Frick's coke monopoly gave Carnegie cost advantage no rival could match |
| Tension | Frick was harder, more openly ruthless — Carnegie preferred to seem benign |
| Break | Frick forced out 1899 after personal and business disputes |
| Frick's verdict | "Tell Mr. Carnegie I'll meet him in Hell" |

### Cost Obsession

| Method | Application |
|--------|-------------|
| Thomson's cost sheets | Tracked cost per ton with obsessive detail |
| "Watch the costs and the profits take care of themselves" | Carnegie's operating maxim |
| Technology adoption | Immediately scrapped equipment when better technology appeared |
| Competitive pricing | Undercut rivals during downturns, drove them out |
| Reinvestment | Plowed profits back into plant rather than distributing dividends |

## The Homestead Strike (1892)

| Stage | Events |
|-------|--------|
| Background | Homestead Steel Works, near Pittsburgh — Carnegie's flagship |
| Issue | Frick demanded wage cuts and end to union recognition |
| Carnegie | Left for Scotland — private instructions to Frick: break the union |
| June 29 | Frick locked out 3,800 workers |
| July 6 | 300 Pinkerton agents arrived by barge — workers fought them off |
| Casualties | 10 killed (7 workers, 3 Pinkertons), dozens wounded |
| July 12 | Pennsylvania governor sent 8,500 state militia |
| November | Strike collapsed, union destroyed |
| Aftermath | Steel industry remained non-union until 1930s |

### The Contradiction

Carnegie had written sympathetically about labor's right to organize. His 1886 essay praised unions and condemned the use of strikebreakers. But when his own profits were at stake, he let Frick use Pinkertons and militia. He spent the rest of his life insisting Homestead "hurt me more than any other event"—while never reversing the outcome. The union was destroyed, wages were cut, and Carnegie Steel's profits soared. The [[Gospel of Wealth]] promised that the rich would be benevolent trustees; Homestead showed the limits of that promise when it conflicted with the balance sheet.

## Gospel of Wealth

### The Argument

Carnegie published "Wealth" in the *North American Review* (June 1889), articulating the doctrine that would define his legacy. See [[Gospel of Wealth]] for the full argument.

| Principle | Claim |
|-----------|-------|
| Inequality is natural | Concentration of wealth is the price of civilization |
| The rich are trustees | Surplus wealth belongs to the community |
| Give during your lifetime | Heirs should receive only modest provision |
| Give wisely | Institutions (libraries, universities), not alms |
| "The man who dies thus rich dies disgraced" | The ultimate test of a fortune |

### Practice

Carnegie's philanthropy was the essay enacted:

| Institution | Gift |
|-------------|------|
| Carnegie libraries | 2,509 libraries worldwide ($55 million) |
| Carnegie Hall | New York concert hall (1891) |
| Carnegie Institute of Technology | → Carnegie Mellon University |
| Carnegie Institution for Science | Washington (1902) |
| Carnegie Endowment for International Peace | (1910) |
| Carnegie Hero Fund | Rewarded civilian heroism (1904) |
| Church organs | 7,689 organs donated |
| Total | ~$350 million (~$11 billion in 2024 dollars) |

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Buyer | J.P. Morgan |
| Price | $480 million (Carnegie's personal share) |
| New entity | U.S. Steel Corporation — first billion-dollar company |

### Why He Sold

| Factor | Weight |
|--------|--------|
| Age | 65, wanted to focus on philanthropy |
| Gospel of Wealth | Had to practice what he preached |
| Morgan's threat | Building competing steel capacity through Federal Steel |
| Charles Schwab | Carnegie's protégé brokered the deal |
| Legacy anxiety | Wanted to be remembered as giver, not taker |

## The Deeper Point

Carnegie embodies the central tension of the [[Gilded Age]]: the same man who crushed Homestead built 2,509 libraries. This wasn't hypocrisy so much as the logical endpoint of his philosophy—that wealth must be concentrated to be productive, and then redistributed to be just. The [[Gospel of Wealth]] was simultaneously a defense of inequality (the capable must accumulate) and a critique of inheritance (the heirs didn't earn it). It offered the American elite a bargain: keep your fortunes, but give them away before you die.

The bargain worked—up to a point. Carnegie gave away $350 million, [[John D. Rockefeller]] gave away $540 million, and the philanthropic foundations they created still operate today. But the doctrine assumed benevolence that couldn't be enforced. Carnegie chose to fund libraries; another magnate might choose to fund nothing. The [[Progressive Era]]'s response—the income tax (1913), the estate tax (1916)—was society's answer to the Gospel of Wealth's weakness: if voluntary redistribution isn't reliable, make it mandatory.

Carnegie's vertical integration—ore to rail in one company—was the organizational innovation that made American steel dominant. But it also demonstrated that [[Creative Destruction|creative destruction]] is not gentle: the handloom weavers of Dunfermline, the steelworkers of Homestead, the independent producers crushed by Carnegie's cost advantages were all destroyed by the same process that brought cheap steel to a growing nation. Carnegie understood this better than most—his own father was a casualty—but he could only address it through philanthropy after the fact, never through restraint during the act.

The man who died with $30 million remaining from a $480 million fortune came closer than anyone to living his own doctrine. That he failed to prevent Homestead, failed to prevent the war he funded peace movements to stop, and failed to give away every last dollar suggests not personal failure but the limits of individual virtue as a substitute for [[Institutions and Progress|institutional]] design.
