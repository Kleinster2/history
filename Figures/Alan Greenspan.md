# Alan Greenspan

Alan Greenspan (1926–2026) chaired the US Federal Reserve for nearly two decades (1987–2006) and, more than any other individual, personified the era of central-bank-managed prosperity that ran from the end of Paul Volcker's disinflation to the [[2008 Financial Crisis]]. An Ayn Rand acolyte turned consummate Washington insider, he was appointed by Reagan weeks before the October 1987 crash — which he met with a flood of liquidity, establishing the asymmetric reflex of rescuing falling markets that became known as the "Greenspan put." Through the 1990s he presided over the Great Moderation of low inflation and long expansions, warned of "irrational exuberance" (1996) yet declined to lean against the dot-com bubble, and after 2001 held rates low and championed financial deregulation and unregulated derivatives — conditions that helped inflate the housing bubble. He retired in January 2006 hailed as "the Maestro"; within three years the crisis reframed his legacy, and in October 2008 he told Congress he had found "a flaw" in his free-market model. He died June 22, 2026, aged 100.

## From Ayn Rand to the Eccles Building

Greenspan trained as an economist and ran the consultancy Townsend-Greenspan, and as a young man belonged to Ayn Rand's inner circle — a committed believer in free markets and the gold standard. He chaired the Council of Economic Advisers under Gerald Ford (1974–77) before Reagan named him Fed chair in August 1987, succeeding Paul Volcker, whose 1979–82 rate shock had broken the back of 1970s inflation. Greenspan inherited a disinflated economy and spent two decades managing its expansion under four presidents.

## The 1987 crash and the birth of the "put"

Barely two months into his term, Black Monday (Oct 19, 1987) saw the Dow fall 22.6% in a single day. Greenspan's Fed flooded the system with liquidity and signaled it would backstop financial markets; they recovered quickly. The episode established the reflex investors came to count on — that the Fed would cut rates and supply liquidity to arrest asset-price declines. Repeated in 1998 (the LTCM rescue) and after the dot-com bust, it became the "Greenspan put," the asymmetric reaction function critics argue institutionalized moral hazard and is now a permanent feature of how markets read the central bank.

## The Great Moderation and "irrational exuberance"

The 1990s delivered low inflation, falling unemployment, and the longest US expansion on record — the "Great Moderation," credited at the time to skilled central banking (Greenspan's discretionary "risk-management" approach) as much as to structural luck (globalization, technology, favorable demographics). Greenspan asked publicly whether "irrational exuberance" had inflated asset values (Dec 1996) but declined to raise rates to deflate the dot-com bubble, articulating the doctrine that the Fed should clean up after bubbles rather than lean against them. His reputation peaked: Bob Woodward's *Maestro* (2000) captured the near-oracular authority he held.

## Low rates, deregulation, and the road to 2008

After the dot-com bust and 9/11, Greenspan cut the federal funds rate to 1% (2003–04) and held it low, helping fuel a housing and credit boom. He was a vocal champion of financial deregulation and of leaving over-the-counter derivatives unregulated — resisting Brooksley Born's late-1990s push to bring them under supervision — and praised the spread of subprime and adjustable-rate mortgages as financial innovation. Low rates plus light-touch supervision are central to the "how did we get here" account of the [[2008 Financial Crisis]]. He retired in January 2006, succeeded by Ben Bernanke, just as the housing market peaked.

## The reckoning

When the crisis hit, Greenspan's legacy inverted. In House Oversight Committee testimony on October 23, 2008, he conceded he had "found a flaw" in his belief that self-interested banks would protect their own shareholders — "a flaw in the model that I perceived is the critical functioning structure that defines how the world works." It was an extraordinary public reversal by the era's most celebrated free-market technocrat. How much of the crisis traces to Greenspan-era policy — easy money versus a global savings glut versus regulatory failure — remains a live debate that feeds directly into [[Financial Crisis Theory]] and the [[Post-2008 Economy]].

## Why he matters

Greenspan is the hinge figure of late-20th-century [[Central Banking]]: he connects Volcker's conquest of inflation to the Global Financial Crisis, embodies the rise of the central banker as the economy's chief steward, and is the namesake of the market's enduring belief that the Fed will catch falling asset prices. The lesson cuts both ways — the same market-friendly, discretionary approach that produced two decades of apparent stability also accumulated the fragilities that broke in 2008.
