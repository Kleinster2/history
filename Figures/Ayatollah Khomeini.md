# Ayatollah Khomeini

Ruhollah Musavi Khomeini — Shia cleric, mystic, political theorist, and the architect of the world's first modern theocracy — transformed Iran from a US-allied monarchy into an Islamic republic whose founding mythology required permanent enmity with the West. Born into a clerical family in Khomein (1902), he rose through the seminary in Qom, broke into politics by opposing [[Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi]]'s White Revolution in 1963, was exiled for fifteen years (first to Turkey, then Iraq, then Paris), and returned on February 1, 1979, to claim a revolution he had not started but would monopolize completely. Before assuming power, he promised democratic governance, equal rights for women, and that clerics would not hold political office — then systematically broke every promise, establishing *velayat-e faqih* (guardianship of the Islamic jurist) as the supreme governing principle, purging liberals, leftists, and rival clerics, and executing thousands. He exploited the US embassy hostage crisis (444 days) to destroy the moderate government and consolidate theocratic rule. His indifference to economics — "economics is for donkeys," he reportedly said — shaped a system where revolutionary ideology overrode material development: mass nationalizations created bonyads (revolutionary foundations) that became corrupt conglomerates, autarky replaced trade, and the [[Iran-Iraq War]] he prolonged for six years beyond the point of military necessity forged the [[IRGC]] into the dominant institution of the state. He died on June 3, 1989, leaving behind a system so thoroughly constructed that his successor, Ali Khamenei — whom Khomeini had publicly humiliated and who was not even an ayatollah — could inherit it and accumulate more institutional power than any Iranian ruler in history, including the Shah.

## Background

### The Clerical World

Khomeini came from a family of Shia clerics — his grandfather and father were both religious scholars. His father was murdered when Ruhollah was five months old. He grew up in the seminary system, rising through Qom's hierarchy.

| Stage | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Childhood | Father killed 1903; raised by mother and aunt (both died by 1918) |
| Seminary | Qom — studied jurisprudence, philosophy, and mysticism (Sufism, irfan) |
| Teacher | Became a respected teacher of ethics and philosophy in Qom by the 1930s |
| Political quietism | For decades, followed the dominant Shia tradition that clerics should advise, not rule |
| Break | 1962-63 — entered politics by opposing the Shah's White Revolution |

### The Political Turn

Traditional Shia political theory held that legitimate government could only exist under the Hidden Imam (the Twelfth Imam, in occultation since 874 CE). In practice, this meant clerics stayed out of governance. Khomeini broke decisively with this tradition.

| Factor | What changed |
|--------|-------------|
| White Revolution (1963) | Shah's land reform seized religious endowments (waqf); women's suffrage challenged clerical authority |
| Capitulation law (1964) | Shah granted legal immunity to US military personnel in Iran — Khomeini denounced it as a return to colonial subjugation |
| Exile | Expelled 1964; spent 15 years developing the theory that clerics should not merely advise but *govern* |
| *Velayat-e faqih* | Published in Najaf (1970): argued that in the Imam's absence, a qualified jurist should exercise supreme political authority |

## Exile and Revolution (1964-1979)

### Fifteen Years in Exile

| Location | Period | Activity |
|----------|--------|----------|
| Turkey (Bursa) | 1964-65 | Brief, unhappy stay |
| Iraq (Najaf) | 1965-78 | Taught, wrote, built network of followers; developed *velayat-e faqih* |
| France (Neauphle-le-Château) | Oct 1978 – Feb 1979 | Media access transformed him into a global figure; Western journalists portrayed him as a moderate mystic |

### The Promises

From France, Khomeini told the world what it wanted to hear:

| Promise | Reality after power |
|---------|-------------------|
| "Clerics will not hold political office" | Clerics controlled every branch of government |
| Democratic republic | Theocratic republic with sham elections |
| Equal rights for women | Compulsory hijab; rollback of Family Protection Law |
| Freedom of expression | Censorship, mass arrests, executions |
| Independent judiciary | Revolutionary courts under clerical control |
| Inclusive government | Purge of liberals, leftists, ethnic minorities |

### The Return

On February 1, 1979, Khomeini flew from Paris to Tehran on an Air France charter. Approximately 3 million Iranians lined the route from the airport. Asked on the plane what he felt about returning to Iran after 15 years, he replied: "Nothing." (*Hichi.*) The answer was either Sufi detachment or a warning of what was coming.

## Consolidation of Power (1979-1981)

### The Hostage Crisis as Instrument

The US embassy seizure (November 4, 1979) was not spontaneous — it was the second attempt (the first failed). Khomeini recognized its utility and endorsed it.

| Function | Mechanism |
|----------|-----------|
| Destroyed the moderates | PM Bazargan's provisional government resigned; Khomeini replaced them with loyalists |
| Eliminated the left | Tudeh Party and Mojahedin-e Khalq marginalized as the political field narrowed to Khomeini's faction |
| Anti-American bonding | 444 days of confrontation unified the revolutionary base |
| Humiliated Carter | Hostages released minutes after Reagan's inauguration — a calculated insult |
| Set the template | Enmity with America became a founding principle, not a tactical choice |

### The Purges

| Group targeted | Method |
|----------------|--------|
| Liberals (National Front) | Sidelined, arrested, forced into exile |
| Left (Tudeh, Mojahedin, Fedayeen) | Thousands executed in 1981 and 1988 prison massacres |
| Rival clerics | Ayatollah Shariatmadari (Khomeini's most prominent clerical rival) placed under house arrest, "defrocked" |
| Ethnic minorities | Kurdish, Arab, Baluch uprisings crushed |
| Military | Shah's officer corps purged; many executed |
| Women | Compulsory hijab enforced; women judges removed; marriage age lowered to 9 |

The 1988 prison massacre — in which Khomeini issued a fatwa ordering the execution of political prisoners who remained loyal to opposition groups — killed an estimated 2,800-5,000 people in weeks. It was carried out in secret; Iran has never officially acknowledged the numbers.

## Economic Vision (or Lack of One)

### "Economics Is for Donkeys"

Khomeini's indifference to economics was not incidental — it was ideological. The revolution's purpose was spiritual and political, not material. This shaped every economic decision.

| Policy | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Mass nationalization | Banks, insurance companies, major industries, and private assets of the Shah's circle seized |
| Bonyads (revolutionary foundations) | Confiscated assets organized into foundations — Bonyad-e Mostazafan (Foundation of the Oppressed) became one of Iran's largest economic entities |
| Autarky | Self-sufficiency as ideology; international trade viewed with suspicion |
| Brain drain | Educated middle class fled; an estimated 2-3 million Iranians emigrated in the revolution's first decade |
| Price controls | State-administered prices on basic goods; black markets flourished |
| Oil dependence | Despite rhetoric about independence, the Islamic Republic remained as oil-dependent as the Shah's regime |

### The Bonyad System

The bonyads became one of the revolution's most lasting economic legacies — and one of its most damaging.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Origin | Seized assets of the Shah, royal family, and connected elite |
| Legal status | Report only to the Supreme Leader; exempt from taxation and auditing |
| Scale | Bonyad-e Mostazafan alone controlled ~$12 billion in assets by the 2000s |
| Function | Officially: welfare for war veterans, the poor, martyrs' families |
| Reality | Corrupt conglomerates run by regime insiders; crowded out private sector |
| Pattern | Identical to military-economic empires: Roman legions, Ottoman Janissaries, [[IRGC]] |

## The Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988)

### Khomeini's War

Saddam Hussein invaded in September 1980. Iran's resistance was genuine and heroic. But by 1982, Iran had recaptured all lost territory. Khomeini chose to continue the war for six more years, seeking to overthrow Saddam and "liberate" Karbala.

| Decision point | Khomeini's choice | Consequence |
|----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| 1982: territory recovered | Continue the war | Six more years of slaughter |
| Human wave attacks | Endorsed use of basij (volunteer militias, including teenagers) | Massive casualties; martyrdom mythology |
| Chemical weapons | Iraq used them; the West looked away | Deepened narrative of Western betrayal |
| 1988: ceasefire | Accepted — called it "drinking poison" | War ended at status quo ante; no territorial change |
| Legacy | The war forged the [[IRGC]] | The revolution's permanent enforcer was born |

### The War's Economic Cost

| Impact | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Casualties | ~500,000 Iranian dead; hundreds of thousands wounded |
| Infrastructure | Oil facilities, cities (Khorramshahr, Abadan) devastated |
| Cost | Estimated $500 billion+ combined |
| Lost generation | Millions of young men dead, disabled, or traumatized |
| IRGC empowered | Post-war reconstruction contracts went to the Guards |
| Strategic solitude | Iran fought alone → proxy doctrine, missile program, nuclear hedging |

## Death and Succession (1989)

Khomeini died on June 3, 1989. His funeral was attended by millions of mourners so frenzied that the coffin fell from the bier and had to be recovered. The body was buried, disinterred, and reburied.

### The Succession He Created

| Factor | Consequence |
|--------|-------------|
| Designated successor (Montazeri) | Dismissed in 1989 for criticizing the prison massacres |
| Ali Khamenei | The compromise choice — Rafsanjani made him supreme leader, believing he could be controlled |
| Khamenei's weakness | Not even an ayatollah; had to be elevated overnight; lacked religious credentials |
| Khamenei's solution | Made the IRGC his power base — reversing Khomeini's ban on military involvement in politics |
| Result | By the 2020s, Khamenei held more institutional power than any Iranian ruler, including the Shah |

The irony: Khomeini created a system of clerical rule, but his successor's power came not from religious authority but from military alliance. The [[IRGC]] — which Khomeini had created for the revolution and forged in the war — became the dominant institution precisely because the next supreme leader needed it to compensate for religious credentials he lacked.

## The Economic Lesson

### Revolution vs. Development

Khomeini's Iran illustrates a pattern shared with the French and Russian revolutions: ideological regimes that prioritize purity over prosperity.

| Revolution | Ancien régime replaced | Economic result |
|------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| French (1789) | Corrupt but economically functional monarchy | Terror, war, economic disruption → Napoleon |
| Russian (1917) | Developing empire with growing industry | Civil war, famine, collectivization, purges |
| Iranian (1979) | Modernizing petro-state with growing middle class | Brain drain, war, sanctions, institutional decay |

In each case, a regime with legitimate grievances against the old order sacrificed economic development to ideological consolidation. The difference with Iran: oil revenue provided a rentier cushion that neither France nor Russia had — enough to sustain the regime without reform, but not enough to develop the economy.

### What Khomeini Built (Economically)

| Feature | Character |
|---------|-----------|
| State sector | Massive — bonyads + nationalized industries + IRGC enterprises |
| Private sector | Marginalized — viewed with suspicion as potentially counter-revolutionary |
| Middle class | Shrank from ~65% to far less; educated Iranians fled |
| Oil dependence | Unchanged — renamed AIOC as NIOC, but same structural reliance |
| Corruption | Shifted from the Shah's court to the revolutionary elite — different beneficiaries, same pattern |
| Innovation | Stifled by autarky, sanctions, brain drain, and ideological constraints |

## The Deeper Point

Khomeini is the figure who most clearly demonstrates how revolutionary ideology can lock a country into economic isolation. His grievances against the Shah were legitimate: political repression, foreign domination, cultural imperialism, inequality. But the system he built required permanent enemies to justify permanent emergency, and permanent emergency precluded economic development. The hostage crisis was not a mistake — it was the mechanism that made enmity with America structural rather than contingent. The Iran-Iraq War was not just a defensive necessity — the six years Khomeini prolonged it beyond territorial recovery created the [[IRGC]] as a permanent institution. The bonyads were not just revolutionary redistribution — they became the vehicle for a new elite to capture the economy.

The deepest economic insight is about the relationship between revolutionary legitimacy and institutional development. Khomeini built a system whose legitimacy depended on opposition to the West, resistance to foreign influence, and defense of Islamic purity — none of which required economic performance. The Shah's legitimacy depended on development (and failed because development without freedom was insufficient). Khomeini's legitimacy depended on ideology (and survived because ideology could be sustained by oil rents and external enemies without development). Both systems were built on oil. The Shah spent it on modernization; Khomeini spent it on revolution. Neither built the institutions that might have made Iran's wealth productive rather than merely distributable.
