# Traditionalism Arc

Traditionalism is a counter-history of modernity: instead of seeing the last few centuries as progress from superstition to reason, it sees modernity as a fall from sacred order into materialism, leveling, and spiritual amnesia. The historical arc matters because a current that began as esoteric religious philosophy later supplied vocabulary for parts of the radical and populist right: decline, inversion, hierarchy, cyclical time, civilizational rupture, and renewal through collapse.

This note tracks the arc as history, not endorsement. The key question is how anti-modern metaphysics became political language.

---

## Core Thesis

Capital-T Traditionalism begins as a spiritual-intellectual revolt against modernity and becomes politically relevant when later figures translate its metaphysics into civilizational politics.

The mechanism is not a simple chain of command. It is a repertoire:

| Traditionalist motif | Political translation |
|----------------------|-----------------------|
| Primordial Tradition | Authentic civilization has been buried by modernity |
| Cyclical decline | Present crisis is not repairable progress but late-cycle decay |
| Spiritual hierarchy | Egalitarian liberalism is metaphysical disorder |
| Initiatic elite | Real authority belongs to those who see through modern illusions |
| Anti-materialism | Capitalism, communism, liberalism, and technocracy can be treated as variants of the same leveling force |
| Inversion | Official expertise, journalism, universities, and institutions say the opposite of truth |

## Act I: The Birth of Modern Anti-Modernism

The modern Traditionalist school is usually traced to Rene Guenon in Paris in the 1920s. Its roots reach back through nineteenth-century European encounters with Asian religious texts, occult revival, comparative religion, and reaction against Enlightenment rationalism.

The paradox is important: Traditionalism is anti-modern, but it is also a modern product. It emerges from print culture, colonial-era access to non-Western religious texts, urban intellectual networks, and a crisis of confidence in European Christianity after liberalism, science, and industrial capitalism disrupted older authority structures.

## Act II: Guenon, Perennialism, and Sacred Order

Guenon framed Tradition as a primordial metaphysical truth preserved imperfectly in orthodox religious forms. This was not generic nostalgia. The claim was that modernity had severed the link between outward institutions and inward sacred knowledge.

The historical imagination here is cyclical. The present is not "behind" some future of progress; it is late in a degenerative age. That makes Traditionalism different from ordinary conservatism. A conservative may want to preserve institutions. A Traditionalist may see modern institutions themselves as symptoms of decline.

## Act III: Evola and Political Hardening

Julius Evola pushed Traditionalism toward a more explicitly political and hierarchical register. In the twentieth century, this matters because Evola's version could interact with fascism, postwar neo-fascist currents, and later European New Right milieus while still claiming to stand beyond ordinary modern politics.

This is the dangerous hinge in the arc: spiritual hierarchy becomes social hierarchy; critique of modern leveling becomes attack on liberal democracy; cyclical decline can make catastrophe seem purifying rather than merely destructive.

## Act IV: Postwar Margins and Metapolitics

After World War II, Traditionalism was not a mass party ideology. It persisted through publishers, salons, esoteric circles, religious converts, far-right intellectuals, and academic discussion of religion and modernity.

Its political channel was often metapolitical rather than electoral. The aim was less to win office directly than to change the cultural and intellectual atmosphere: books, schools, media, religious authority, aesthetics, and elite networks. That made the current easy to miss if one only looked for parties and platforms.

## Act V: Contemporary Populist-Right Translation

Benjamin R. Teitelbaum's Traditionalism case, developed in *War for Eternity*, is that figures around Steve Bannon, Aleksandr Dugin, Olavo de Carvalho, Gabor Vona, and others made Traditionalist themes newly relevant to twenty-first-century right-populist politics.

The historical shift is not that masses became Traditionalist. It is that elite political entrepreneurs could use Traditionalist motifs to frame globalization, liberalism, media, secularism, feminism, migration, and technocratic governance as signs of civilizational decay.

## Why It Matters for Economic History

Traditionalism is anti-progress at the level of historical imagination. That is why it belongs in this vault even though it is not mainly about markets or GDP.

The history vault's baseline question is how societies came to produce sustained material progress. Traditionalism attacks the moral authority of that story. It treats modern economic progress, specialization, mass education, institutional expertise, and technological development as possible signs of spiritual collapse rather than achievement.

That inversion can alter political economy:

| Economic-history object | Traditionalist suspicion |
|-------------------------|--------------------------|
| Industrial growth | Material expansion without spiritual order |
| Mass democracy | Leveling of hierarchy and authority |
| Globalization | Homogenization and border erasure |
| Expertise | Modern credential systems hiding deeper ignorance |
| Capitalism and communism | Rival faces of materialist modernity |
| Liberal rights | Emancipation narrative that denies sacred hierarchy |

The key historical move is not just "anti-capitalism" or "anti-liberalism." It is anti-modernism so deep that both capitalism and communism can be interpreted as siblings.

## Cases and Evidence

### Intellectual History

- Mark Sedgwick's *Against the Modern World* is the main scholarly map of Traditionalism as a twentieth-century movement.
- Cambridge's treatment of Guenonian Traditionalism identifies its origin in Guenon's 1920s writings, its capital-T distinction, and its emphasis on perennial wisdom, initiation, anti-modernism, and cyclical decline.
- Teitelbaum's *War for Eternity* is the contemporary political-network source.

### Brazil Link

The Unicamp interview around *Guerra pela Eternidade* is especially useful for the Brazilian line. It places Olavo de Carvalho alongside Bannon and Dugin in Teitelbaum's network analysis while also recording Teitelbaum's caution that metapolitics and Traditionalism are connected more by co-occurrence and shared far-right milieus than by a single explicit doctrine.

### Russia / Eurasia Link

Dugin is the most geopolitically legible case because his civilizational and Eurasian language connects anti-liberal political theology to spatial geopolitics. The caution is that Dugin draws from multiple sources, including Carl Schmitt in the geopolitics vault, so Traditionalism should be treated as one strand rather than the whole explanation.

## Critiques and Limits

- Influence is uneven. A politician can borrow a Traditionalist motif without being a Traditionalist.
- The category is easy to overextend. Not all anti-liberalism, nationalism, religious conservatism, or right populism is Traditionalism.
- Traditionalism is internally diverse: Guenon, Schuon, Evola, Eliade, Dugin, and later political users do not form a single doctrine.
- The political reading can obscure religious and scholarly variants that were not primarily party-political.
- Catastrophe-as-renewal narratives are analytically important because they can make institutional breakdown attractive, but that does not prove operational control over political movements.
