# Pleistocene Epoch

The Pleistocene epoch (2.58 Ma - 11,700 years ago) was defined by dramatic climate oscillations, massive ice sheets, and the evolution and global spread of the genus *Homo*. Often called the "Ice Ages," this epoch shaped modern landscapes, drove megafaunal extinctions, and witnessed the emergence of anatomically modern humans.

## Overview

The name "Pleistocene" derives from Greek *pleistos* (most) and *kainos* (new), reflecting the prevalence of modern species. This epoch encompasses the majority of human evolutionary history.

## Climate

### Glacial-Interglacial Cycles

The Pleistocene is defined by ~50 major glacial-interglacial cycles:

| Cycle Period | Dominant Before | Dominant After | Trigger |
|--------------|-----------------|----------------|---------|
| 41,000 years | 2.58-1.0 Ma | - | Obliquity (tilt) |
| 100,000 years | - | 1.0 Ma-present | Eccentricity |

### Ice Sheet Extent

During glacial maxima:
- 30% of Earth's land covered by ice (vs. 10% today)
- Ice sheets 3-4 km thick over North America, Europe
- Sea level 120-140 m lower than present
- Land bridges exposed (Beringia, Sunda, Sahul)

|-----------|-----|---------|

### Interglacial Periods

Warm periods between glacials:
- Duration: 10,000-30,000 years
- Temperatures similar to or warmer than today
- Higher sea levels
- Forest expansion
- Last interglacial (Eemian, 130-115 ka): Warmer than present

## Effects on Landscapes

### Glacial Features
- U-shaped valleys
- Moraines and drumlins
- Fjords
- Kettle lakes
- Loess deposits (windblown glacial dust)

### Non-Glacial Changes
- Pluvial lakes in deserts (e.g., Lake Bonneville)
- Expanded and contracted forests
- River system reorganization
- Coastal plain exposure and flooding

## Flora and Fauna

### Pleistocene Megafauna

Large mammals that characterized the epoch:

**Proboscideans:**
- Woolly mammoth (*Mammuthus primigenius*)
- Columbian mammoth (*M. columbi*)
- Mastodons
- Straight-tusked elephants

**Carnivores:**
- Sabertooth cats (*Smilodon*, *Homotherium*)
- Cave lions (*Panthera spelaea*)
- Dire wolves (*Aenocyon dirus*)
- Cave bears (*Ursus spelaeus*)
- Cave hyenas

**Herbivores:**
- Woolly rhinoceros
- Giant ground sloths
- Glyptodonts
- Irish elk (*Megaloceros*)
- Aurochs (*Bos primigenius*)
- Steppe bison

### Megafaunal Extinction

Major extinctions occurred at the end of the Pleistocene:

| Region | Extinction Timing | % Large Mammals Lost |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Australia | ~46,000 years ago | 88% |
| Americas | 15,000-10,000 years ago | 72% |
| Europe | 50,000-10,000 years ago | 36% |
| Africa | Gradual | 18% |

**Causes (debated):**
- Human hunting ("overkill hypothesis")
- Climate change
- Combination of both
- Disease
- Habitat change

## End of the Pleistocene

### Transition to Holocene (11,700 BP)

**Climate:**
- Rapid warming
- Ice sheet melting
- Sea level rise (~120 m total)
- Forest expansion

**Human Impact:**
- Megafaunal extinctions
- Population growth
- Spread into new territories
- Development of agriculture (early Holocene)

### Younger Dryas (12,900-11,700 BP)

Brief cold snap before final warming:
- Return to near-glacial conditions
- 1,200 years duration
- May have influenced human subsistence changes
- Coincides with early plant cultivation experiments
