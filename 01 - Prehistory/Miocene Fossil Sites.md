# Miocene Fossil Sites

The Miocene epoch is documented by fossil sites across Africa and Eurasia. These localities have yielded the remains of apes, early hominins, and the ecosystems in which they lived. Understanding these sites is essential for reconstructing human origins.

## Overview

### Geographic Distribution
Miocene ape and hominin fossils are found across three main regions:
- **East Africa**: Kenya, Ethiopia, Uganda
- **Central Africa**: Chad
- **Eurasia**: Spain, France, Germany, Hungary, Greece, Turkey, Pakistan, India, China, Thailand

### Taphonomy and Preservation
Most sites preserve fossils through:
- Volcanic ash and sediments
- Lake and river deposits
- Cave accumulations
- Windblown sediments

## East African Sites

### Rusinga Island, Kenya (18-17 Ma)

**Location:** Lake Victoria, western Kenya

**Key Fossils:**
- *Proconsul* (multiple species)
- *Dendropithecus*
- *Limnopithecus*
- Complete primate skeletons

**Significance:**
- Most complete early Miocene ape material
- *Proconsul heseloni* skeleton (KNM-RU 2036)
- Established *Proconsul* as tailless early ape
- Preserves forest ecosystem snapshot

**Discovery History:**
- Explored since 1909
- Mary Leakey found key *Proconsul* skull (1948)
- Continued excavations to present

**Environment:** Tropical forest with volcanic deposits

---

### Songhor, Kenya (20-19 Ma)

**Location:** Western Kenya

**Key Fossils:**
- *Proconsul* species
- *Rangwapithecus*
- *Dendropithecus*
- *Limnopithecus*

**Significance:**
- One of the oldest Miocene ape sites
- Shows early ape diversity before dispersal to Eurasia

---

### Moroto, Uganda (21-20 Ma)

**Location:** Northeastern Uganda

**Key Fossils:**
- *Morotopithecus bishopi* (partial skeleton)
- *Afropithecus*

**Significance:**
- *Morotopithecus* shows earliest evidence of orthograde (upright-trunk) posture
- Suggests modern ape body plan evolved very early
- Vertebrae indicate stiff lower back for vertical climbing

---

### Kalodirr & Moruorot, Kenya (17-16 Ma)

**Location:** Turkana region, northern Kenya

**Key Fossils:**
- *Afropithecus turkanensis*
- *Turkanapithecus*

**Significance:**
- Documents apes with thick enamel
- Transitional period before Eurasian dispersal

---

### Tugen Hills, Kenya (15-6 Ma)

**Location:** Central Kenya, Rift Valley

**Key Sites Within Region:**

| Site | Age (Ma) | Key Fossils |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Lukeino Formation | 6.1-5.7 | *Orrorin tugenensis* |
| Ngorora Formation | 12-9 | *Samburupithecus* |
| Various | 15-6 | Extensive mammal sequences |

**Significance:**
- Long time sequence spanning mid-to-late Miocene
- *Orrorin* provides earliest definitive bipedal evidence
- Documents transition from apes to hominins

---

### Middle Awash, Ethiopia (5.8-4.3 Ma)

**Location:** Afar region, northeastern Ethiopia

**Key Sites:**

| Site | Age (Ma) | Key Fossils |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Asa Issie | 5.8-5.2 | *Ardipithecus kadabba* |
| Aramis | 4.5-4.3 | *Ardipithecus ramidus* |
| Gona | 4.3-4.5 | *Ar. ramidus* |

**Key Specimen:** "Ardi" skeleton (ARA-VP-6/500)

**Significance:**
- Most complete early hominin fossils
- Detailed paleoenvironmental reconstruction
- Proved early hominins lived in woodland, not savanna
- Changed understanding of human-ape common ancestor

**Research Team:** Tim White, Berhane Asfaw, Gen Suwa

---

### Lothagam, Kenya (8-4 Ma)

**Location:** West side of Lake Turkana

**Key Fossils:**
- Late Miocene fauna
- Possible early hominin (mandible fragment, disputed)
- Transition to Pliocene

**Significance:**
- Documents faunal change across Miocene-Pliocene boundary
- Shows environmental shifts

## Central African Sites

### Toros-Menalla, Chad (7-6 Ma)

**Location:** Djurab Desert, northern Chad

**Key Fossils:**
- *Sahelanthropus tchadensis* ("Toumaï")
- Cranium TM 266-01-060-1
- Associated fauna

**Significance:**
- Oldest proposed hominin
- Far from East African Rift - expands known hominin range
- Associated with lake-margin woodland
- Challenges East African focus of human origins research

**Discovery:** Michel Brunet's team, 2001

**Environment:**
- Ancient Mega-Lake Chad shores
- Gallery forest along waterways
- Savanna and woodland mosaic

---

## European Sites

### Can Llobateres, Spain (9.5 Ma)

**Location:** Catalonia, Spain

**Key Fossils:**
- *Hispanopithecus laietanus* (nearly complete skeleton)
- Multiple individuals

**Significance:**
- Shows highly suspensory European ape
- Long curved fingers for arm-swinging
- Went extinct during late Miocene cooling

---

### Rudabánya, Hungary (10 Ma)

**Location:** Northeastern Hungary

**Key Fossils:**
- *Rudapithecus hungaricus*
- Extremely complete skull
- Postcranial elements

**Significance:**
- One of best-preserved Miocene ape skulls
- Facial structure suggests possible African ape/human ancestor
- Evidence for "out of Eurasia" hypothesis for African ape origins

---

### Els Hostalets de Pierola, Spain (12-11 Ma)

**Location:** Catalonia

**Key Fossils:**
- *Pierolapithecus catalaunicus* (12.5 Ma)
- *Anoiapithecus brevirostris* (11.9 Ma)
- *Dryopithecus fontani*

**Significance:**
- *Pierolapithecus* shows orthograde body plan with primitive hands
- Documents early great ape evolution
- Multiple species show European ape diversity

---

### Saint-Gaudens, France (12-11 Ma)

**Location:** Southwestern France

**Key Fossils:**
- *Dryopithecus fontani* (type specimen)

**Significance:**
- First fossil ape recognized from Europe (1856)
- Type locality for *Dryopithecus* genus
- Demonstrated apes once lived in Europe

---

### Baccinello, Italy (8-7 Ma)

**Location:** Tuscany

**Key Fossils:**
- *Oreopithecus bambolii*
- Complete and partial skeletons

**Environment:** Island setting (Tusco-Sardinian island)

**Significance:**
- Bizarre island ape with unique features
- Shows convergent evolution of bipedalism
- Isolated evolution produced unique morphology

---

### Pikermi, Greece (8-7 Ma)

**Location:** Near Athens

**Key Fossils:**
- *Ouranopithecus macedoniensis*
- *Mesopithecus* (monkey)
- Rich fossil fauna

**Significance:**
- Late Miocene open-habitat ape
- Thick enamel, robust jaws
- Shows apes could adapt to more open environments

---

### Xirochori/Ravin de la Pluie, Greece (9-10 Ma)

**Location:** Northern Greece

**Key Fossils:**
- *Ouranopithecus macedoniensis*

**Significance:**
- Additional material of late Miocene Greek ape
- Demonstrates range of *Ouranopithecus*

## Asian Sites

### Siwalik Hills, Pakistan & India (15-7 Ma)

**Location:** Foothills of Himalayas

**Key Fossils:**
- *Sivapithecus* (multiple species)
- *Gigantopithecus giganteus*
- Rich mammalian fauna

**Significance:**
- Largest collection of Asian Miocene apes
- *Sivapithecus* linked to orangutan ancestry
- Long sequence documenting environmental change
- Himalayan uplift affected local climate

**Key Sites:**
| Site | Age (Ma) | Key Fossils |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Potwar Plateau | 12-7 | *Sivapithecus* |
| Chinji | 14-11 | *Sivapithecus indicus* |
| Nagri | 10-9 | *Sivapithecus sivalensis* |

---

### Lufeng, China (9-6 Ma)

**Location:** Yunnan Province, southern China

**Key Fossils:**
- *Lufengpithecus*
- Multiple species and specimens
- Associated fauna

**Significance:**
- Shows ape presence in Southeast Asian forests
- Multiple species indicate regional diversity
- Possible orangutan relative

---

### Chiang Muan, Thailand (13-10 Ma)

**Location:** Northern Thailand

**Key Fossils:**
- *Khoratpithecus*

**Significance:**
- Extends known ape range into Southeast Asia
- Possible orangutan lineage member

---

### Longgupo, China (2-1.8 Ma, but relevant)

**Location:** Sichuan Province

**Key Fossils:**
- *Gigantopithecus* teeth
- Other Pleistocene fauna

**Significance:**
- Documents *Gigantopithecus* survival into Pleistocene
- Shows giant ape persisted while other large apes went extinct

## Methods and Techniques

### Dating Methods Used
- **Radiometric**: K-Ar, Ar-Ar dating of volcanic layers
- **Paleomagnetic**: Reversals in sediment sequences
- **Biostratigraphic**: Fauna-based age estimates
- **Tephrostratigraphy**: Volcanic ash correlation

### Paleoenvironmental Reconstruction
- Stable isotopes (C, O) from teeth and soils
- Fossil pollen and plant remains
- Associated fauna (indicators of habitat)
- Sedimentology
