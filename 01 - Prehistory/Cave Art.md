# Cave Art

Cave art represents humanity's earliest visual art tradition, with paintings, engravings, and handprints adorning the walls of caves across Europe, Southeast Asia, and Africa. Dating back over 40,000 years, these works demonstrate sophisticated artistic skill and provide windows into the minds of our ancestors.

## Overview

### Definition

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Parietal art | Art on cave walls (paintings, engravings) |
| Mobiliary art | Portable art objects |
| Pictograph | Painted image |
| Petroglyph | Engraved or carved image |

### Distribution

| Region | Oldest Known (ka) | Major Sites |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| Europe | ~45 (disputed), 37 (confirmed) | Chauvet, Lascaux, Altamira |
| Indonesia | 45+ | Sulawesi caves |
| Australia | 17+ (Kimberley) | Various |
| Africa | 27+ | Apollo 11 (Namibia) |

## Techniques

### Painting Methods

| Technique | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Finger painting | Direct application |
| Brush painting | Using plant fibers, hair |
| Spray painting | Blowing pigment (for hand stencils) |
| Engraving | Incising into rock |
| Relief sculpture | Carving rock face |

### Pigments

| Color | Source |
|-------|--------|
| Red | Iron oxide (ochre, hematite) |
| Black | Manganese dioxide, charcoal |
| Yellow | Ochre, limonite |
| White | Kaolin, calcite |
| Brown | Iron oxides |

### Preparation

Evidence of planning:
- Pigment grinding and mixing
- Binder addition (animal fat, water)
- Scaffolding for high locations
- Lamp light for illumination

## Interpretation

### Theories Through History

| Era | Dominant Interpretation |
|-----|------------------------|
| 19th century | Art for art's sake |
| Early 20th century | Hunting magic |
| Mid-20th century | Fertility cult |
| Late 20th century | Shamanism |
| Current | Multiple meanings, context-dependent |

### Hunting Magic Hypothesis

Proposed by Abb√© Breuil:
- Painting animals to ensure hunting success
- Wounded animals common
- Problems: Many painted species not hunted

### Shamanism Hypothesis

Proposed by Lewis-Williams:
- Entoptic phenomena from altered states
- Animals as spirit helpers
- Deep caves as spiritual space
- Problems: Not universally applicable

### Structuralist Interpretation

Proposed by Leroi-Gourhan:
- Animals arranged by sex/opposition
- Horses (male) vs. bison (female)
- Spatial organization meaningful
- Problems: Classification disputed

### Current Understanding

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Multiple functions | Not single explanation |
| Context matters | Each site may differ |
| Social meaning | Identity, memory, place-making |
| Deep knowledge | Artists knew animals intimately |
| Symbolic system | Part of broader cultural practice |

## Hand Stencils

### Types

| Type | Method |
|------|--------|
| Negative | Pigment blown around hand |
| Positive | Hand dipped in pigment, pressed |

### Distribution

Found globally:
- Europe (El Castillo, Cosquer, Gargas)
- Indonesia (Sulawesi)
- Australia
- Americas

### "Mutilated" Hands

At some sites (Gargas, Maltravieso):
- Missing fingers on hand stencils
- Ritual mutilation?
- Folded fingers?
- Disease?
- Debated interpretation

### Handedness

Analysis of hand stencils:
- Left hands most common
- Suggests right-handed painters
- Same pattern as modern humans

## Dating Methods

| Method | Material Dated | Precision |
|--------|---------------|-----------|
| Radiocarbon | Charcoal pigments | Good |
| Uranium-thorium | Calcite over paintings | Good |
| Optically stimulated luminescence | Sediments | Moderate |
| Style comparison | Art itself | Poor |

### Challenges

| Problem | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Contamination | Modern carbon intrusion |
| Minimum age | Flowstone dates post-creation |
| Multiple phases | Same panel, different ages |
| Black pigments | Manganese can't be dated |

| Technique | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|
| Perspective | Overlapping, size variation |
| Animation | Multiple legs suggest movement |

### Learning and Tradition

Evidence suggests:
- Apprenticeship and learning
- Stylistic traditions
- Consistent iconography
- Multigenerational use of sites
