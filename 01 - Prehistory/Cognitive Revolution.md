# Cognitive Revolution

The emergence of fully modern human behavior. Sometime between 100,000 and 50,000 years ago, [[Homo sapiens]] began exhibiting capacities—symbolic thought, complex language, artistic expression, long-distance trade—that set them apart from all other species. This cognitive transformation made possible all subsequent economic development.

## The Deeper Point

The Cognitive Revolution created the mental toolkit that makes economic activity possible—but cognition alone doesn't explain economic development. Humans had modern brains for 100,000+ years before agriculture, 200,000+ years before industry. The hardware was present; the [[Institutions and Progress|institutional]] software took much longer to develop.

This gap is the central puzzle. If cognitive capacity enables investment, trade, specialization, and accumulation, why did these remain limited for so long? The answer must lie in social and institutional arrangements, not individual psychology. Hunter-gatherer societies had sharing norms that prevented accumulation. Only with the [[Neolithic]] did property rights and surplus storage become possible—and even then, sustained growth took millennia more.

The Cognitive Revolution is thus necessary but not sufficient for economic development. It provided the capacity for symbolic thought that makes money meaningful, the language that enables contracts, the future orientation that permits investment. But translating these capacities into sustained progress required institutional innovations—property rights, markets, states—that came much later.

The comparison with Neanderthals is instructive. They had large brains and sophisticated tools but didn't develop the cultural accumulation that characterizes Homo sapiens. The difference may have been social rather than individual—larger networks, more reliable transmission, better collective learning. Economic progress is a social phenomenon; individual genius matters less than institutional arrangements that harness and transmit knowledge.
