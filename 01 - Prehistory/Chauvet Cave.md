# Chauvet Cave

Chauvet Cave (Grotte Chauvet) contains the oldest known cave paintings in Europe—sophisticated artworks dating back 37,000 to 28,000 years. Discovered in 1994, the cave stunned researchers with its artistic mastery, overturning assumptions that prehistoric art evolved gradually from simple to complex.

## Dating

### Two Phases of Occupation

| Phase | Age (BP) | Characteristics |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| Phase 1 | 37,000-33,500 | Older paintings, hand stencils |
| Phase 2 | 31,000-28,000 | Some additional work |
| Closure | ~21,000 | Entrance sealed by rockfall |

### Dating Methods

| Method | Application |
|--------|-------------|
| Radiocarbon | Charcoal pigments |
| Uranium-thorium | Calcite over paintings |
| Thermoluminescence | Heated sediments |
| Paleomagnetic | Deposits |

### Significance of Age

| Impact | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Oldest dated art | Confirmed European earliest |
| Full sophistication | No "primitive" stage |
| Aurignacian period | First modern humans in Europe |
| Revises evolution | Art didn't develop gradually |
