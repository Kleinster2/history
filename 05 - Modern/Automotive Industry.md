# Automotive Industry

The automobile industry was mass production's proving ground and remains creative destruction's ongoing test case. From Ford's assembly line to Toyota's lean production to Tesla's electric disruption, automaking has been the arena where manufacturing paradigms are invented, perfected, and overthrown. The industry shaped 20th-century life—suburbs, highways, oil dependency, middle-class prosperity—and now faces its greatest transformation since the Model T: electrification, autonomy, and the challenge of decarbonization.

## The Ford Revolution (1908-1930)

### Henry Ford

| Achievement | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| Vision | "A car for the great multitude" |
| Model T | 1908—simple, rugged, affordable |
| Highland Park | Assembly line factory (1913) |
| Price reduction | $825 (1908) → $260 (1925) |

### The Assembly Line

| Innovation | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| Moving line | Work comes to worker |
| Specialization | Each worker does one task |
| Interchangeable parts | Precision manufacturing |
| Speed | Model T build time: 93 min vs. 12 hours |
| Scale | 15 million Model Ts produced |

| Policy | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Doubled wages (1914) | From $2.34 to $5 |
| Reduced turnover | Workers stayed |
| Created consumers | Workers could buy cars |
| Productivity | Higher than wage increase |
| Social control | Ford Sociological Department |

## The Deeper Point

The auto industry is where [[Institutions and Progress|institutional]] capitalism continually reinvents itself. Ford's assembly line created [[Mass Production|mass production]] and mass consumption; Toyota's lean system showed another way; Tesla is attempting yet another transformation. Each revolution restructured not just production but labor relations, supplier networks, and geographic concentration. [[Entrepreneurship|Entrepreneurial]] energy in autos has flowed to different activities in different eras: production engineering (Ford), marketing (Sloan), quality systems (Toyota), software and batteries (Tesla). The industry demonstrates that competitive advantage is always temporary—[[Creative Destruction]] eventually overthrows even the most dominant players. GM's 50% market share seemed unassailable until it wasn't; Japanese dominance was challenged by Koreans and now Chinese. The current electric transition will produce new winners and losers, determined not just by technology but by [[Business Regulation|regulatory]] choices (emissions standards, EV mandates), industrial policy (battery supply chains, charging infrastructure), and labor settlements (UAW's demands for EV-era protections). The auto industry remains the test case for whether advanced economies can manufacture things—and for whether the next transformation will be captured by legacy players, new entrants, or entirely different nations.
