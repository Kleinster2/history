# Iranian Revolution

A broad coalition — liberals, leftists, feminists, nationalists, Islamists — united by opposition to Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi's autocratic rule toppled one of the Middle East's most powerful governments in early 1979. Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, initially seen as a transitional figure who promised equal rights for women and barred clerics from politics, exploited his unchallenged popularity to monopolize power and establish a theocracy. The US embassy hostage crisis (444 days) ruptured relations with America, the loss of ~5 million bpd of oil exports triggered the [[Oil Crisis|second oil shock]], and Saddam Hussein's invasion launched the [[Iran-Iraq War]] that forged the [[IRGC]] into the revolution's permanent enforcer. The regime survived war, sanctions, and reformist challenges by institutionalizing enmity with the West — a posture rooted in the [[BP|1953 coup]] but hardened by decades of mutual betrayal, producing a feedback loop in which Iranian hardline actions consistently empowered hawks in opposing countries, whose responses then empowered Iranian hardliners.

## Pre-Revolutionary Iran

Iran under the Shah was economically prosperous with strong foreign relations — good ties with both the United States and the Soviet Union, the strongest military in the Middle East, and a modernizing society. What it lacked was political freedom. Power was concentrated in the Shah and a corrupt political elite, and a widespread (if inaccurate) perception held that the Shah was an American puppet.

This perception had deep roots in the 1953 CIA/MI6 coup that overthrew Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh after he nationalized Iranian oil — an event covered in detail in [[BP]] and [[Persian Economic History]]. Iran's nationalism runs deep: one of the only countries in the 18th and 19th centuries to avoid becoming a Western colony, it maintained a fierce sense of independence comparable to China's "Middle Kingdom" self-conception. Every Iranian schoolchild learned about the 1953 coup; most Americans had never heard of it.

## Khomeini's Consolidation

Khomeini said all the right things before assuming power. Once 3 million Iranians welcomed him in Tehran, he recognized his power was unchallenged and moved to establish theocratic rule. The US embassy hostage crisis served this consolidation directly:

| Dimension | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| **Domestic politics** | Forced the moderate government to resign; Khomeini replaced them with loyalists |
| **Revolutionary symbolism** | Demonstrated Iran would no longer be "subjugated" to the United States |
| **Precedent** | Revolutionaries had earlier stormed *both* the US and Soviet embassies — ejected from both. Only the second US embassy takeover succeeded because it served Khomeini's agenda |
| **Release terms** | Hostages freed minutes after Reagan's inauguration — Khomeini deliberately humiliating Carter |

The US did not deliver on Algiers Accord promises of non-interference or return of frozen assets — establishing a pattern of mutual betrayal that would repeat for decades.

## The Hardliner Feedback Loop

The revolution established a pattern that the crisis analyst Ali Vaez calls "what comes around goes around": hardline actions on each side consistently empowered the other's hawks.

| Iranian action | Western response | Hardliner benefit |
|---------------|------------------|-------------------|
| Hostage crisis (1979) | Reagan elected (more hawkish than Carter) | Regime consolidation |
| Funding Hamas/Hezbollah (1990s) | Netanyahu empowered, Oslo sabotaged | Palestinian cause maintained as leverage |
| Rejected engagement with Khatami (1997–2005) | Reformists discredited | Deep state preserved |
| [[JCPOA]] compliance (2015–18) | Trump withdrew, [[Iran Sanctions\|maximum pressure]] | Hardliners vindicated: "deals don't work" |
| October 7th (2023) | Israeli escalation directly to Iran | Soleimani's absence left proxies freelancing; Iran pulled into war (see [geopolitics vault](obsidian://open?vault=geopolitics&file=Events%2F2026%20Iran%20conflict%20--%20analyst%20perspectives)) |

This institutionalized enmity always benefited hawks on all sides more than moderates who tried to change course.

## Succession: Khamenei's Rise

Khomeini died in 1989. His successor, Ali Khamenei, was an absolute underdog — the symbolic president whom Khomeini publicly chastised. Akbar Hashemi Rafsanjani, the powerful parliamentary speaker, made Khamenei supreme leader believing he could be controlled. Khamenei was not even an ayatollah; they had to make him one overnight.

| Factor | Consequence |
|--------|-------------|
| Lacked religious credentials | Sought alternative power base → turned to IRGC |
| Militarized politics | Reversed Khomeini's ban on IRGC in politics |
| Watched Soviet collapse (1989–91) | Concluded reform = unraveling of ideological systems |
| Outmaneuvered Rafsanjani | Over decades, sidelined every rival |
| Result | More institutional power than any Iranian ruler, including the Shah |

Khamenei's calculation — that opening the door to reforms would unravel the system — became the regime's operating principle and the reason every reformist president (Khatami in 1997, Rouhani in 2013) was ultimately sabotaged by the deep state.

## State and Society

The rupture between state and society deepened over decades. The 1997 reformist election of Khatami was the last moment of genuine hope that the system could reform from within — and its failure was the beginning of the population losing faith. By the 2020s, the regime relied on perhaps 5–10% of Iranian society as its core constituency, but could not reform without unraveling.

Two factors kept it in place despite mass unpopularity:

1. **No exit ramp** — The revolutionary elite had nowhere to go. Unlike the Shah's circle (villas in Côte d'Azur, Swiss Alps, Southern California), these people had no plan B.
2. **No organized alternative** — No opposition had the ground game or organizational capacity to replace the regime. Air power could degrade military capabilities but could not manufacture a viable political alternative.

## The Deeper Point

The Iranian Revolution illustrates a recurring pattern: revolutions born from legitimate grievances (political freedom, anti-corruption, self-determination) can produce regimes whose founding mythology locks them into economic isolation. Iran had "a lot going for it" — prosperity, external relations, a modernizing society — and lost most of it not because the grievances were wrong but because the revolutionary consolidation required permanent enemies. The 1953 coup gave Khomeini his founding narrative; the hostage crisis gave him his instrument of consolidation; the Iran-Iraq War gave him his permanent security apparatus. Each crisis strengthened the regime's hard men and weakened its moderates — a ratchet that, once set, proved nearly impossible to reverse.

The economic parallel to the French and Russian revolutions is instructive: all three replaced a corrupt but economically functional ancien régime with a system that prioritized ideological purity over material development, exported the revolutionary model (alarming neighbors), and relied on external threats to justify internal repression. The difference is that Iran's oil wealth provided a rentier cushion that neither France nor Russia had — enough to sustain the regime without reform, but not enough to develop the economy.
