# Platform Monopolies

Big Tech's dominance tests antitrust's ability to permit creative destruction while preventing extraction. Google, Apple, Meta, Amazon, and Microsoft (plus Chinese counterparts) have achieved market positions that dwarf the trusts of the Gilded Age. Network effects, data advantages, and ecosystem lock-in create winner-take-all dynamics that traditional competition policy struggles to address. The 2020s regulatory surge—in the EU, US, and China—represents the most significant antitrust moment since the breakup of Standard Oil. Whether it will reshape the digital economy or merely constrain its edges remains to be seen.

## The Deeper Point

Platform monopolies test whether [[Institutions and Progress|institutional]] responses to market power can adapt to new forms of dominance. Traditional [[Antitrust History|antitrust]] focused on price effects—but Google is free, and Amazon often has low prices. The harm is different: control over access to markets, power to pick winners, ability to copy and crush competitors, influence over public discourse. [[Entrepreneurship|Entrepreneurial]] energy in platforms has produced genuine innovation (search, social, e-commerce, cloud), but also created gatekeepers who extract rents from the innovation of others (app store fees, marketplace commissions). The regulatory response is fragmented: Europe leads on rules, the US on lawsuits, China on political control. Whether any approach can actually restrain platform power—or whether it just shapes how that power is exercised—remains uncertain. The [[Creative Destruction]] that created these giants has not (yet) been applied to them: Facebook is 20 years old, Google 25, Amazon nearly 30. Whether that's because they've genuinely won, or because they've foreclosed competition, is the central question. The answer will shape whether digital economies remain dynamic or ossify into administered markets controlled by a handful of global platforms.
