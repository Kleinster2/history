# Asian Financial Crisis 1997

The emerging market crisis that revealed capital flow dangers. The Asian crisis showed how [[Institutions and Progress|institutions]] designed for one context (capital mobility) could devastate economies lacking supporting institutions (bank regulation, flexible rates).

## The Miracle Before the Fall

|---------|----------------------|
| South Korea | 8.6% |
| Thailand | 7.5% |
| Indonesia | 6.0% |
| Malaysia | 6.8% |
| Philippines | 3.5% |

| Achievement | Mechanism |
|-------------|-----------|

| Education | Human capital investment |
| Stability | Long political continuity |

## Build-Up to Crisis

### Capital Inflows

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| High interest rates | Attracted foreign capital |
| Fixed exchange rates | Reduced perceived currency risk |
| Short-term borrowing | Cheaper than long-term |
| Dollar pegs | Implicit guarantee |

### Vulnerabilities

| Weakness | Manifestation |
|----------|---------------|
| Current account deficits | Thailand 8% of GDP |
| Short-term foreign debt | Often exceeded reserves |
| Bank lending booms | Real estate, speculation |
| Crony capitalism | Connected lending |
| Currency mismatch | Borrowed in dollars, earned in baht |

### Warning Signs Ignored

| Signal | Response |
|--------|----------|
| Property bubbles | "Different here" |
| Bad loans | Hidden, rolled over |
| Overvaluation | Defended with reserves |
| Mexico 1994 | "We're not Mexico" |

## Mechanisms of Crisis

### The Doom Loop

| Stage | Process |
|-------|---------|
| 1 | Foreign investors flee |
| 2 | Currency falls |
| 3 | Foreign debt burden rises |
| 4 | Banks insolvent |
| 5 | Credit contracts |
| 6 | Economy shrinks |
| 7 | More investors flee |

### Bank Runs and Panic

| Dynamic | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| Liquidity crisis | Couldn't roll over short-term debt |
| Fire sales | Forced to sell at any price |
| Credit crunch | Banks stopped lending |
| Contagion | One country's crisis triggered others |

### Why Contagion?

| Channel | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Trade links | Export competition |
| Common creditors | Banks pulled out of region |
| Wake-up call | Reassessed all emerging markets |
| Herding | Funds followed each other out |

### The Rescue Packages

### IMF Conditions

| Requirement | Rationale |
|-------------|-----------|
| Fiscal austerity | Restore confidence |
| High interest rates | Defend currency |
| Bank closures | Remove insolvent banks |
| Structural reforms | Corporate governance, transparency |

### The Controversy

| Critique | IMF Defense |
|----------|-------------|
| Austerity worsened recession | Standard medicine for balance of payments |
| High rates killed economy | Had to stop capital flight |

## Recovery Paths

### Divergent Responses

| Country | Strategy | Outcome |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Korea | IMF program, reforms | Fast recovery |
| Malaysia | Capital controls, rejected IMF | Recovered anyway |
| Thailand | IMF program | Slow recovery |
| Indonesia | IMF program, political collapse | Prolonged crisis |

### Malaysia's Alternative

| Policy | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Fixed exchange rate (3.8/1) | Stabilized expectations |
| Capital controls | Stopped outflows |
| Lower interest rates | Supported growth |
| Bank restructuring | Nationalized, recapitalized |
| Outcome | Recovered without IMF austerity |

### Lessons from Divergence

| Observation | Interpretation |
|-------------|----------------|
| Malaysia did okay | Maybe capital controls work |
| Korea recovered fast | Reforms mattered |
| Indonesia worst | Political collapse compounded |
| No one-size-fits-all | Context matters |

## Lessons Learned

### For Countries

| Lesson | Application |
|--------|-------------|
| Watch short-term debt | Maturity mismatch dangerous |
| Build reserves | Self-insurance |
| Flexible exchange rates | Absorb shocks |
| Bank regulation | Supervise lending |
| Capital flow management | Maybe controls have a place |

### For International System

| Lesson | Change |
|--------|--------|
| Lender of last resort needed | But conditionality controversial |
| Early warning | IMF surveillance |
| Orderly debt restructuring | Collective action clauses |
| Private sector involvement | Bail-in, not just bailout |

### What Was Missing

| Gap | Consequence |
|-----|-------------|

| Corporate governance | Connected borrowing |

| Flexible rates | Couldn't adjust smoothly |

|--------|---------|

## The Deeper Point

The Asian crisis showed how [[Institutions and Progress|institutions]] designed for one context could devastate economies lacking supporting institutions. Capital account liberalization worked well in countries with strong bank supervision, transparent corporate governance, and flexible exchange rates. When applied to countries without these supports, it created catastrophic vulnerabilities.

The pattern reveals a general principle: institutional reforms work as packages. Capital mobility without bank supervision is dangerous. Fixed exchange rates without reserves are fragile. Rapid lending without governance creates crony capitalism. The "Washington Consensus" assumed institutions could be transplanted one at a time; the Asian crisis showed they come as interconnected systems.

The aftermath also revealed institutional capacity for learning. Countries built massive reserves. Regional cooperation emerged. Bank supervision improved. Institutions failed in 1997, but the failure itself became a source of institutional learning.
