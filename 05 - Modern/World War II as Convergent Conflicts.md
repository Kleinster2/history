# World War II as Convergent Conflicts

"World War II" is a retrospective label imposed on what began as several independent wars with distinct origins, belligerents, and logic. They converged into a single global conflict between 1940 and 1941—fused by resource interdependence, political opportunism, and the cascading consequences of France's fall. The standard narrative (Hitler started a war, others joined) obscures a more structural reality: industrial economies competing for resources in an already-integrated world could not fight regional wars without triggering global ones.

## The Separate Wars

### 1. The Sino-Japanese War (1937-1945)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Origins | Japanese expansion into Manchuria (1931), full invasion of China (1937) |
| Drivers | Resource scarcity, military factionalism, pan-Asian ideology |
| Scale | 15-20 million Chinese dead, largest Asian land war |
| Connection to Europe | Initially none—separate conflict with separate causation |

Japan's imperial project grew from [[Meiji Restoration]] logic: industrialize, arm, expand. The seizure of Manchuria (1931) and the Marco Polo Bridge incident (1937) had nothing to do with European politics. Japan needed iron, coal, and food that China's northeast provided. The war bogged down into a grinding occupation that consumed resources faster than it secured them—creating the pressure for southward expansion that eventually linked this war to the others.

### 2. The European War (1939-1941)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Origins | Versailles revisionism, Lebensraum, failure of collective security |
| Trigger | German invasion of Poland (September 1, 1939) |
| Initial scope | Western Europe—contained conflict |
| Character | Fast conquest (Poland, France), then stalemate (Britain) |

The [[Interwar Economy]]'s failures—reparations, depression, autarky—created the political conditions. German rearmament absorbed unemployment and generated growth, but the economic model was unsustainable without conquest (Tooze's *Wages of Destruction* argument). Hitler's war was a resource war disguised as an ideological one: grain from Ukraine, oil from Romania and the Caucasus, labor from occupied territories.

### 3. The Nazi-Soviet War (1941-1945)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Origins | Ideological enmity masked by [[Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact]] (Aug 1939) |
| Trigger | Operation Barbarossa (June 22, 1941) |
| Scale | 27 million Soviet dead, 80% of German military casualties |
| Character | Exterminatory—not a conventional war between states |

Stalin and Hitler were *partners* from August 1939 to June 1941, dividing Poland and the Baltics between them. Barbarossa created a fundamentally different war—ideological, racial, and fought at a scale that dwarfed every other theater. The Eastern Front was where the German army was destroyed. Economically, Germany needed Soviet resources (grain, oil, manganese) and couldn't obtain them through trade once the war economy consumed everything.

### 4. The Pacific War (1941-1945)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Origins | Japanese resource dependence, US/British oil embargo (July 1941) |
| Trigger | Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941) |
| Logic | Seize Dutch East Indies oil, neutralize US fleet |
| Character | Naval-air war across vast distances |

Japan imported 80% of its oil from the United States. When Japan occupied French Indochina (July 1941)—possible only because France had fallen to Germany—the US froze Japanese assets and imposed a full oil embargo. Japan's strategic oil reserves would last roughly 18 months. The clock was now ticking.

Japan's target was not the United States—it was the **Dutch East Indies** (modern Indonesia), whose oil fields could replace American supply, along with British Malaya's rubber and tin. But the US Pacific Fleet at Pearl Harbor could intercept the southward advance. Pearl Harbor's purpose was to neutralize that fleet—an enabling strike to protect the real objective, not the main attack.

The bitter irony: by attacking Pearl Harbor, Japan guaranteed the one outcome it couldn't survive—a full industrial war against the world's largest oil producer. Japan attacked its supplier not to get oil, but because the oil was already cut off and only American naval power stood between Japan and an alternative source.

The DEI fields were operated by Royal Dutch Shell (~75% of output), Stanvac (Exxon/Mobil joint venture), and Caltex (Chevron/Texaco) — the same Western oil majors that had been selling Japan its fuel. The Dutch destroyed the installations as Japan invaded; Japan never restored more than half of prewar output; US submarines then sank the tankers. Japan started the war over oil and lost it because of oil. See [[Oil Economy#WWII and the Dutch East Indies]] for the full corporate and operational detail.

This war had its own logic entirely, yet it was triggered by the European war's consequences.

## The Fusion Points

The separate wars merged through a specific chain of events, each link dependent on the last:

| Date | Event | What It Connected |
|------|-------|-------------------|
| Aug 1939 | Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact | Temporarily *separated* what might have merged (Germany + USSR vs. Western allies) |
| June 1940 | Fall of France | Left French Indochina undefended, Dutch East Indies unprotected |
| Sept 1940 | Tripartite Pact | Formally linked Germany, Italy, Japan—but coordination remained minimal |
| July 1941 | Japan occupies Indochina | Direct consequence of France's fall; triggered US oil embargo |
| June 1941 | Barbarossa | Opened Eastern Front; created the two-front war Germany couldn't win |
| July 1941 | US oil embargo on Japan | Set 18-month clock before Japanese oil reserves ran out |
| Dec 7, 1941 | Pearl Harbor | Fused Pacific and European theaters politically |
| Dec 11, 1941 | Hitler declares war on US | The gratuitous act that made it one war—no treaty required this |

Hitler's declaration of war on the United States (December 11) is the single most important fusion event. Japan's attack on Pearl Harbor did not obligate Germany to declare war on America. Hitler chose to, likely expecting Japan would reciprocate against the USSR (Japan didn't). This decision united the European and Pacific conflicts under one Allied command structure and made "Germany First" possible.

## The Resource Logic

What connected all these "separate" wars was **resource competition in an industrialized world**:

| Belligerent | Critical Resource Need | War's Purpose |
|-------------|----------------------|---------------|
| Japan | Oil (80% from US), rubber, tin, rice | Seize Southeast Asia |
| Germany | Oil (Romania, Caucasus), grain (Ukraine), iron (Sweden) | Conquer east |
| Italy | Oil (Libya), markets (Mediterranean) | Control mare nostrum |
| Britain | Oil (Middle East), shipping lanes, imperial supply | Defend empire |
| USSR | Survival—industrial base threatened | Defend and then push west |
| USA | Already resource-rich—fought to preserve the open system | Prevent Axis resource autarky |

The [[Oil Economy]] was the thread connecting the Pacific and European theaters. Japan needed oil that the US controlled. Germany needed oil that the Caucasus held. Both launched wars of conquest to secure what they couldn't buy. The Allies' fundamental advantage was resource access: the US produced 60% of the world's oil in 1940.

## The Historiographical Debate

| Position | Historian | Argument |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| Multiple wars that merged | Evan Mawdsley, *World War II: A New History* | Until late 1941, no single war existed; Allied strategy *imposed* unity |
| Structurally one war | Gerhard Weinberg, *A World at Arms* | Hitler's global ambitions and resource interconnection made it one conflict from the start |
| Resource war | Adam Tooze, *Wages of Destruction* | Germany's economic model required conquest; the wars were economically determined |
| Thirty Years' Crisis | E.H. Carr, Arno Mayer | 1914-1945 as one continuous conflict with an intermission |
| Asian war as separate | Rana Mitter, *Forgotten Ally* | China's war (1937-45) has been written out of the Western narrative |

The strongest synthesis: **independent origins, convergent resource logic, politically constructed unity.** The wars started separately but the industrial economy made isolation impossible. Once the global commodity system was disrupted (shipping lanes, oil flows, rubber supply), every regional war pulled in distant powers.

---

## The Economic Throughline

The "convergent conflicts" framing matters for economic history because it reveals that WW2 was not primarily an ideological war that happened to have economic effects. It was a **resource war among industrial economies** that used ideology to mobilize populations:

- Japan didn't invade China for fascism—it invaded for coal, iron, and agricultural land
- Germany didn't invade the USSR for anti-communism alone—it invaded for grain, oil, and Lebensraum
- The US didn't enter for democracy—it entered because the Axis threatened the open trading system on which American prosperity depended
- Britain didn't fight for freedom—it fought to preserve an empire whose resources it needed

The [[Interwar Economy]]'s collapse into autarkic trade blocs (Sterling area, Yen bloc, German bilateral clearing) meant that resource access could no longer be secured through trade. When markets fail, states fight. The war was the ultimate market failure.
