# Iran-Iraq War

Saddam Hussein invaded revolutionary Iran in September 1980, expecting a quick victory against a regime busy purging its own US-trained military — a calculation strikingly similar to Putin's in Ukraine (2022). Instead, the war lasted eight years, killed over a million people, featured trench warfare and chemical weapons reminiscent of World War I, and ended with no territorial change — the first war in 250 years where Iran didn't lose territory. The conflict forged the [[IRGC]] from a revolutionary militia into a hardened fighting force, birthed Iran's ballistic missile program, and created the concept of "strategic solitude" that would drive Iran's entire subsequent security architecture: proxy networks from Lebanon to Yemen, designed to project power beyond borders as a Shia Persian nation surrounded by Sunni Arabs and Turks. The Arab Gulf monarchies funded Iraq as a shield; the United States backed Saddam to contain Iran and degrade American weapons now in revolutionary hands. Paradoxically, the war consolidated the very regime it was meant to destroy.

## Origins

### Saddam's Calculation

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Revolutionary chaos | Iran purging US-trained officers and pilots |
| Shia threat | Iran's revolution threatened Iraq's Sunni-minority rule |
| Opportunity | Weakened military, new regime |
| Arab backing | Gulf monarchies feared revolutionary Islam, funded Iraq as a shield |
| US interest | Containing Iran, degrading US weapons in revolutionary hands |
| Expected outcome | Quick victory |

### Iran's Vulnerability

The [[Iranian Revolution]] had left Iran with the largest American arsenal in the region but without the trained personnel to use it. Revolutionary purges and executions had decimated the officer corps. Saddam — like Putin 42 years later — concluded this meant a quick campaign. He was wrong.

| Dimension | Description |

### Strategic Solitude

Iran fought almost entirely alone. The regional and global lineup:

| Backer | Supported |
|--------|-----------|
| Arab Gulf states (Saudi, Kuwait, UAE) | Iraq — financed as a shield against revolutionary Iran |
| United States | Iraq — intelligence, dual-use technology, diplomatic cover |
| Soviet Union | Iraq — weapons supplier |
| France | Iraq — Mirage jets, Exocet missiles |
| Israel | Iran (covertly) — preferred to weaken Saddam; part of Iran-Contra |
| Syria | Iran — the exception among Arab states |
| North Korea, China | Iran — limited weapons supplies |

This experience of *strategic solitude* — facing the world almost entirely alone — became the foundational trauma of Iranian strategic thinking. Every subsequent security decision was shaped by the lesson: Iran cannot rely on anyone else for its defense.

## The Iran-Contra Dimension

The war intersected with American domestic politics through the Iran-Contra affair: the Reagan administration secretly sold weapons to Iran (via Israel) and used the proceeds to fund Nicaraguan rebels. This episode — in which the US simultaneously backed Iraq publicly and armed Iran covertly — illustrates the tangled, contradictory nature of great power involvement in the conflict.

## Endgame

Iran's acceptance of the ceasefire in 1988 was, in [[Ayatollah Khomeini|Khomeini]]'s words, like "drinking poison." The war ended at the status quo ante — no territory changed hands. But Iran's narrative framed survival itself as victory: the first war in 250 years where Iran didn't lose territory, fought against a world that wanted it to lose.

[[Ayatollah Khomeini|Khomeini]] died in 1989, one year after the war ended. The IRGC he had created and the strategic doctrine the war had forged would outlive him by decades.

## The Deeper Point

The Iran-Iraq War is the hidden foundation of everything that followed in Iranian geopolitics: the IRGC's dominance, the proxy networks, the missile program, the nuclear hedging, the institutional paranoia about Western intentions. All of it traces back to eight years of fighting alone against a chemically-armed enemy backed by both superpowers and the region.

The economic lesson is about how wars shape institutions. The IRGC emerged from the war not just as a military force but as an economic one — controlling reconstruction, then expanding into bonyads, construction, telecoms, and oil contracts. This is a pattern visible from the Roman legions (who settled and farmed) to the post-Soviet military-industrial complex: military organizations created in wartime develop economic interests that make them permanent political actors. The Iran-Iraq War created an institution that, by 2025, was more powerful inside Iran than the government it was supposed to protect.
