# Lebanese Banking Crisis

Lebanon built the Middle East's most sophisticated banking sector on a 1956 secrecy law that attracted capital fleeing Nasser's nationalizations, then watched it destroy the country. For decades, Beirut was the "Switzerland of the Middle East" — 85 banks by 1960, deposits multiplying 38-fold between 1950 and 1975. The 1975-90 civil war devastated the economy, but Prime Minister Rafik Hariri rebuilt through massive borrowing, pushing public debt past 100% of GDP by 1997. Central bank governor Riad Salameh — a former Merrill Lynch banker appointed in 1993 — fixed the lira at LL 1,507.50 per dollar and maintained it for 25 years by paying commercial banks extraordinary interest rates on dollar deposits, which were funded not by productive investment but by new deposit inflows: classic [[Ponzi Schemes|Ponzi finance]]. By 2019, banking assets reached $217 billion (4x GDP), 71% of deposits were dollarized, and government debt hit 178% of GDP. The system collapsed in October 2019 when a proposed WhatsApp tax triggered mass protests, banks imposed informal capital controls without legal authority, and Lebanon defaulted on its sovereign debt in March 2020 — its first default in history. The Beirut port explosion (August 2020, 2,750 tonnes of ammonium nitrate, 218 dead) accelerated the spiral. The lira lost 98% of its value. GDP contracted 58% — the World Bank called it one of the worst crises episodes since the 1850s. Dollar deposits became "lollars," worth a fraction of face value. By 2021, poverty reached 85-90%. The banking sector that had made Lebanon exceptional among oil-poor [[Middle Eastern Business|Middle Eastern economies]] consumed it.

## Origins: The "Switzerland of the Middle East" (1943-1975)

### The Banking Secrecy Advantage

Lebanon gained independence from France in 1943 with a confessional political system that divided power among Maronite Christians, Sunni Muslims, and Shia Muslims. In 1956, Finance Minister Raymond Edde championed the Banking Secrecy Law (Law No. 1/1956), modeled on Switzerland's system, prohibiting banks from disclosing client information to any private or public authority.

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Nasser's Egypt (1952-56) | Socialist nationalizations drove capital to Beirut |
| Syria's Ba'athist turn | More capital flight to Lebanon |
| Gulf oil boom (1950s-70s) | [[Petrodollar System|Petrodollars]] sought safe haven |
| Banking secrecy (1956) | Legal protection attracted regional deposits |
| Free-market orientation | Laissez-faire when neighbors nationalized |

### Scale of Success

| Metric | Figure |
|--------|--------|
| Banks (1945) | 9 |
| Banks (1960) | 85 |
| Deposit growth (1950-75) | ~38-fold increase |
| Expatriate remittances (1970) | $250M (18% of national income) |
| Expatriate remittances (1975) | $910M (rising to 35% by 1980) |

The "merchants' republic" label captured Lebanon's model: a trading and financial hub connecting Gulf oil wealth to Western markets, with no domestic production base. The vulnerability was structural — Lebanon intermediated other people's money without generating its own.

## Civil War and Reconstruction (1975-1997)

### Destruction

The Lebanese Civil War (1975-1990) devastated the banking sector. Militias looted banks, the pound hyperinflated, and capital fled. The war killed an estimated 120,000 people and displaced nearly a million.

### The Taif Agreement (1990)

The Taif Agreement ended the war by formalizing confessional power-sharing:

| Position | Sect |
|----------|------|
| President | Maronite Christian |
| Prime Minister | Sunni Muslim |
| Speaker of Parliament | Shia Muslim |

This created a system designed to divide spoils, not govern effectively. Every major decision required consensus among sectarian leaders, each with veto power and patronage networks to feed.

### Hariri's Reconstruction Gamble

Rafik Hariri — a construction billionaire who had made his fortune in Saudi Arabia — became Prime Minister in 1992 and financed reconstruction primarily through borrowing.

| Metric | Figure |
|--------|--------|
| Fiscal deficit (1992-97 average) | 20.7% of GDP |
| Public debt (1992) | $2.9B |
| Public debt (1997) | $16.5B (exceeding 100% of GDP) |
| Solidere expenditure | ~$75B total, only $6B to public projects |
| Hariri net worth (1996 → 2004) | $2.5B → $4.3B |

Solidere, the joint-stock company Hariri founded (1994) to reconstruct downtown Beirut, exemplified the conflation of public purpose and private enrichment. Property owners in the destroyed district were compensated with shares worth as little as 15% of their property's value. Hariri was the major shareholder.

### The Dollar Peg

In December 1997, BdL fixed the lira at LL 1,507.50 per dollar — a rate that would hold for 25 years. Maintaining it required constant inflows of real dollars.

## The Ponzi Machine (1997-2019)

### The Circular Flow

The system that sustained Lebanon's dollar peg and funded its government was, in [[Financial Crisis Theory|Minsky's]] terms, Ponzi finance — income covered neither interest nor principal; the system depended entirely on new capital inflows.

```
Lebanese diaspora + Gulf depositors place USD in banks
        ↓
Banks deposit dollars at BdL at high interest rates
        ↓
BdL uses dollars to finance government (T-bills, Eurobonds) and defend peg
        ↓
Government pays interest on debt from budget (funded by more borrowing)
        ↓
Interest payments attract more deposits
        ↓
Cycle repeats — with growing obligations
```

### Riad Salameh's BdL

Riad Salameh, appointed BdL governor in 1993, ran this system for three decades. A former Merrill Lynch private banker, he was initially celebrated for stabilizing the lira and later investigated across six European countries for embezzlement.

| Period | BdL Strategy |
|--------|-------------|
| 1993-2005 | Post-war stabilization; high rates attract diaspora deposits |
| 2005-2015 | Hariri assassination shock absorbed; rates keep climbing |
| 2016-2019 | "Financial engineering" — desperate last-stage operations |

### The 2016 "Financial Engineering"

When deposit inflows slowed, Salameh launched extraordinary operations:

| Operation | Mechanism | Cost |
|-----------|-----------|------|
| Phase 1 (June-Oct 2016): Swaps with commissions | BdL exchanged LL treasury bills for USD Eurobonds at premiums equal to full coupons to maturity | Banks earned ~$5B; BdL cost ~$13B in foreign currency |
| Phase 2 (2017+): Loans under leverage | Bank deposits $100 at BdL → borrows LL 125 at 2% → redeposits at BdL at ~13% | 11-point spread = pure profit |

The IMF estimated participating banks made a ~40% return on Phase 1 operations alone. These were not investments — they were transfers of public resources to private banks to keep the deposit machine running.

### The Political-Banking Nexus

| Metric | Figure |
|--------|--------|
| Top 20 banks with politically connected shareholders | 18 of 20 |
| Share of banking assets controlled by political elites | 43% |
| Sectarian distribution | Every major faction had banking interests |

The system was self-reinforcing: politicians profited from bank ownership, banks profited from government bonds, government bonds were funded by deposits attracted by high rates, and the sectarian system ensured no faction would dismantle an arrangement that enriched them all.

### The Numbers at Peak (2019)

| Metric | Figure |
|--------|--------|
| Banking assets | $217B (~4x GDP of ~$52B) |
| Total deposits | ~$155B |
| Dollar deposits | ~$117B (71% of total) |
| Lira deposits | ~$38B |
| Government debt/GDP | 178% |
| BdL interest rates to banks (USD) | 5.5-8%+ (some operations: 40%) |

A banking sector four times GDP, dollarized without dollar earnings, paying above-market rates funded by new deposits. Every Ponzi scheme has the same obituary: it worked until it didn't.

## The Collapse (2019-2023)

### October 2019: The WhatsApp Tax

On October 17, 2019, the government proposed a $0.20/day tax on WhatsApp calls. The tax was trivial; the rage was not. Hundreds of thousands took to the streets in unprecedented cross-sectarian protests — *kellon yaane kellon* ("all of them means all of them"). The movement targeted the entire political class, not a single faction.

### Bank Closures and Informal Capital Controls

Banks closed for two weeks — the longest closure in Lebanese history. When they reopened November 1, they imposed capital controls: banned overseas transfers from pre-October 2019 deposits, limited cash withdrawals, and restricted dollar access. No formal capital control law was ever passed. The controls were imposed by private banks, without legal authority, varying from branch to branch.

### Sovereign Default (March 2020)

On March 23, 2020, Lebanon defaulted on a $1.2 billion Eurobond — its first sovereign default in history. The government acknowledged what everyone already knew: the money was gone.

### Beirut Port Explosion (August 4, 2020)

2,750 tonnes of ammonium nitrate — confiscated from the cargo ship MV Rhosus in 2014 and stored unsafely in a port warehouse for six years despite repeated warnings — detonated with the force of 1.1 kilotons of TNT. The explosion killed 218 people, injured 7,000, displaced 300,000, and caused an estimated $15 billion in property damage. The port handled 80% of Lebanon's maritime shipping. No senior official has been held accountable.

### "Lollarization"

Dollar deposits in Lebanese banks became "lollars" — a term coined to describe bank credits denominated in dollars but not convertible to actual dollars. By January 2024, the cash value of a $1,000 bank check had fallen to $160 — 16 cents on the dollar. Depositors who had saved in dollars for decades discovered their savings existed only as entries in insolvent banks' ledgers.

## Historical Parallels

| Crisis | Mechanism | Parallel to Lebanon |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| Argentina 2001 (Corralito) | Dollar deposits forcibly converted to pesos at deflated rate | Depositor expropriation through currency manipulation; "lollarization" parallels pesification |
| Cyprus 2013 (Bail-in) | Uninsured deposits seized (~48% haircut); bank closed | Over-banked economy; depositor losses to recapitalize |
| Iceland 2008 | Banking assets 10x GDP; entire system collapsed | Oversized banking sector relative to economy |
| Greece 2010-15 | Sovereign debt crisis, capital controls, troika austerity | Euro-area constraints, political paralysis |

The critical distinction: Argentina, Cyprus, Iceland, and Greece all had formal legal frameworks — however painful — for imposing losses. Lebanon imposed losses informally, without legislation, without honored deposit insurance, without international bailout, and without any resolution framework until 2025 — six years after the collapse. The absence of legal process was not accidental; it reflected the same sectarian paralysis that built the Ponzi in the first place.

## The Deeper Point

The Lebanese banking crisis is the purest modern example of sovereign [[Ponzi Schemes|Ponzi finance]] — not fraud in the Madoff sense, but Ponzi finance in Minsky's precise definition: a system where income covers neither interest nor principal, sustained only by the expectation of continued capital inflows. The BdL was not a criminal enterprise; it was a central bank operating a legal scheme whose mathematics guaranteed eventual failure. Everyone involved — depositors earning 8%, bankers earning 40% on swaps, politicians collecting bond interest — had rational incentives to continue. The scheme required only one thing to persist: more dollars arriving than leaving. When confidence broke, the arithmetic was merciless.

What made Lebanon's version uniquely destructive was the confessional political system. In a normal state, a crisis of this magnitude produces a resolution: an IMF program (Argentina), a bail-in law (Cyprus), capital controls legislation (Iceland), even a military intervention (Greece's troika). Lebanon's sectarian power-sharing meant no faction could impose losses on another's depositors, banks, or patronage networks. The very system designed to prevent civil war — consensus among sects — made economic governance impossible. Reform required unanimity among the people who profited from the status quo.

The historical irony runs deep. Beirut's role as a financial hub traces back to the [[Levant Trade|Levant's]] ancient function as a commercial junction — the place where goods and capital changed hands between East and West. The 1956 banking secrecy law was a modern version of the same positional logic: Lebanon offered a service (discretion, access, intermediation) rather than producing anything itself. But the Levant ports extracted rents from real goods flowing through real chokepoints. Lebanon's modern banking sector extracted rents from financial flows with no underlying production — and when the flows stopped, there was nothing beneath them.

The [[Petrodollar System]] provided the dollars; confessional democracy ensured no one would stop the game; and the absence of oil (which would have given the state independent revenue) meant the banking sector was the state's only economic asset. Lebanon consumed its only asset to sustain a political equilibrium, then had neither.
