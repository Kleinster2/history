# BP

British Petroleum — born as the Anglo-Persian Oil Company — is the most politically entangled of the [[Oil Economy#The Seven Sisters Era (1920s-1970s)|Seven Sisters]]. No other oil company's history so directly shaped the geopolitics of the 20th century: Churchill's decision to convert the Royal Navy from coal to oil (1911-14) made the British government BP's majority shareholder. BP's Iranian concession provoked the 1953 CIA-MI6 coup that overthrew Mossadegh. BP's North Sea discoveries rescued Britain in the 1970s. And BP's Deepwater Horizon disaster (2010) became the largest accidental marine oil spill in history. Through it all, BP has been the company where state interest and corporate interest are most impossible to separate.

## Overview

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Origin | Anglo-Persian Oil Company (1909) |
| Name changes | Anglo-Persian → Anglo-Iranian (1935) → British Petroleum (1954) → BP (2001) |
| Distinction | British government was majority shareholder from 1914 to 1987 |
| Character | The most geopolitical of the Sisters — state instrument as much as corporation |

## Origins: D'Arcy and Persia (1901-1914)

### The Concession

| Date | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1901 | William Knox D'Arcy, Australian mining magnate, buys oil concession from the Shah of Persia |
| 1901-08 | Seven years of drilling, near-bankruptcy, mounting costs |
| 1908 | Oil struck at Masjid-i-Suleiman — first major discovery in the Middle East |
| 1909 | Anglo-Persian Oil Company incorporated in London |

D'Arcy's concession covered 480,000 square miles — three-quarters of Persia — for 60 years, in exchange for £20,000 cash, £20,000 in shares, and 16% of net profits. The terms would poison British-Iranian relations for half a century.

### Churchill and the Navy (1911-1914)

| Decision | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Context | Winston Churchill, as First Lord of the Admiralty, decided to convert the Royal Navy from coal to oil — faster ships, greater range |
| Problem | Britain had coal; it had no oil |
| Solution | British government bought 51% of Anglo-Persian (1914) for £2.2 million |
| Effect | The British state became an oil company owner — unique among the Seven Sisters |
| Strategic logic | Secure fuel supply for the navy that defended the Empire |

This was the moment oil became a strategic commodity. Churchill's decision meant that British foreign policy in the Middle East would be inseparable from BP's corporate interests for the next seven decades.

## The Iranian Era (1914-1953)

### Building the Abadan Refinery

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Location | Abadan, on the Shatt al-Arab waterway |
| Scale | Largest refinery in the world by the 1930s |
| Workforce | Tens of thousands of Iranian workers |
| Conditions | Segregated housing, poor pay, British management lived in luxury |
| Resentment | Deep and growing — Iranians saw their oil enriching foreigners |

### Profit Distribution

| Period | Iran's Share | Company's Share |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| 1901-1933 | 16% of net profits | 84% |
| 1933 renegotiation | Fixed royalty per ton | Still vastly favorable to BP |
| 1947-50 | Iran received £7 million/year | BP's after-tax profit: £100 million+ |

The profit imbalance was extreme. When [[Chevron|Aramco]] offered Saudi Arabia 50-50 in 1950, Iran demanded the same. Anglo-Iranian refused.

### The 1953 Coup

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1951 | Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh nationalizes Anglo-Iranian's operations |
| 1951-53 | Britain organizes global boycott of Iranian oil; Iran's economy suffers |
| August 1953 | CIA (Operation Ajax) + MI6 (Operation Boot) overthrow Mossadegh; Shah restored |
| 1954 | Oil consortium formed — BP gets 40% (down from 100%), other majors share the rest |
| Legacy | Iranian resentment simmered for 26 years — fueling the 1979 Islamic Revolution |

The coup preserved Western access to Iranian oil for a generation. But the blowback — the 1979 revolution, the hostage crisis, four decades of US-Iran hostility — suggests it was the most consequential corporate-political act of the 20th century.

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Forties field | BP's discovery (1970) — largest in the North Sea at the time |
| Timing | Just before the 1973 [[Oil Crisis]] |
| Effect | Gave Britain domestic oil just as OPEC flexed its power |
| GDP impact | North Sea revenue transformed UK public finances in the 1980s |

## Privatization and Transformation (1979-2000)

### Thatcher Privatizes BP

| Date | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1979 | Thatcher government begins selling government stake |
| 1987 | Full privatization — government exits after 73 years of ownership |
| Effect | BP becomes a normal publicly traded company for the first time |

### The Mergers

| Acquisition | Date | Value |
|-------------|------|-------|
| Amoco (Standard Oil of Indiana) | 1998 | $48 billion |
| ARCO (Atlantic Richfield) | 2000 | $27 billion |
| Burmah Castrol | 2000 | $4.7 billion |

BP absorbed two major American companies — including Amoco, itself a Standard Oil fragment. The British company now operated more in America than in Britain.

### "Beyond Petroleum"

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| CEO | John Browne (1995-2007) |
| Brand | Rebranded as "bp" with green sunflower logo (2000) |
| Claim | Positioning as a forward-looking energy company, not just oil |
| Investments | Solar, wind, hydrogen — modest relative to oil spending |
| Critics | Greenwashing — oil production continued to grow |

## Deepwater Horizon (2010)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Date | April 20, 2010 |
| Location | Macondo well, Gulf of Mexico, 5,000 feet deep |
| Explosion | 11 workers killed; rig sank |
| Spill | 4.9 million barrels over 87 days — largest accidental marine oil spill in history |
| Cause | Multiple failures: cement job, blowout preventer, cost-cutting on safety tests |

### Consequences

| Dimension | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| Financial | $65 billion in total costs (cleanup, fines, settlements) |
| Market cap | Fell from $189 billion to $95 billion |
| Legal | Gross negligence finding; largest environmental fine in US history ($5.5 billion) |
| CEO | Tony Hayward resigned after saying "I'd like my life back" |
| Environmental | Gulf ecosystems damaged for years; full recovery debated |

## The Deeper Point

BP is the case study for **state-corporate entanglement**. When Churchill bought 51% in 1914, he created something that was neither purely a company nor purely a government agency — an entity that pursued profit while serving imperial strategy. This hybrid status produced the Iranian coup, shaped Middle Eastern borders, and embedded corporate interests in British foreign policy. The 1953 coup's blowback — the 1979 revolution, the hostage crisis, decades of instability — is a reminder that corporate decisions made for commercial reasons can have civilizational consequences. Deepwater Horizon was a different kind of failure — operational, not political — but it too reflected institutional DNA: a company that had always relied on the state to backstop its risks (nationalization insurance, coup assistance, military protection of assets) developed a culture that underpriced risk. When the state wasn't there to absorb the consequences, the consequences were $65 billion.
