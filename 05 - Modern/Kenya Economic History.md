# Kenya Economic History

East African emergence. Kenya shows how [[Institutions and Progress|institutional innovation]] (M-Pesa) can leapfrog—the question is whether one sector can transform the whole economy.

## Colonial Economy (1895-1963)

### White Highlands

| Feature | Characteristic |
|---------|----------------|
| Land alienation | Best agricultural land to settlers |
| Cash crops | Coffee, tea, sisal, pyrethrum |
| African exclusion | Couldn't grow coffee initially |
| Labor | Squatter system, pass laws |

### Dual Economy

| Sector | Character |
|--------|-----------|
| European commercial | Export crops, modern |
| African peasant | Subsistence, crowded reserves |
| Asian commercial | Trade, manufacturing, excluded from land |

### Colonial Infrastructure

| Investment | Purpose |
|------------|---------|
| Railway | Mombasa to Lake Victoria (export corridor) |
| Ports | Mombasa as regional hub |

## The Deeper Point

Kenya shows how [[Institutions and Progress|institutional innovation]] can leapfrog—M-Pesa allowed Kenya to skip a generation of traditional banking infrastructure. But it also shows the limits of sectoral success: fintech flourishes while roads crumble, agriculture stagnates, and manufacturing never emerged.

The M-Pesa story is genuinely important: it demonstrates that African countries can innovate, that regulatory flexibility enables rather than blocks progress, and that mobile technology can transform economic life. Kenya's fintech ecosystem is now globally recognized.

But Kenya's broader development challenges—inequality, youth unemployment, infrastructure deficits, corruption—remain. M-Pesa reduced transaction costs but didn't create productive employment. Tech startups flourish in Nairobi while much of the country remains in subsistence agriculture.

The [[Institutional Economics|institutional question]] is whether Kenya can build on M-Pesa's success: use the financial infrastructure it created, the institutional capacity its regulation demonstrated, and the entrepreneurial energy its tech sector revealed, to address broader development challenges. The pieces are there—a regional hub role, an educated population, demonstrated innovation capacity. But translating sectoral success into economy-wide transformation requires the kind of sustained institutional development that Kenya's patronage politics has historically undermined.
