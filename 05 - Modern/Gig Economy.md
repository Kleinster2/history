# Gig Economy

The gig economy is institutional arbitrage—platforms escaping labor law's channeling of business ambition by classifying workers as independent contractors. Uber, DoorDash, TaskRabbit, and their competitors have created new forms of work: flexible, technology-enabled, and often precarious. For some workers, gig work is freedom; for others, it's exploitation dressed in app aesthetics. The legal and political battles over worker classification are fundamentally about what rules should govern work in the 21st century—and who bears the risks of economic life.

## Overview

### What Is the Gig Economy?

| Definition | Elements |
|------------|----------|
| Platform-mediated work | App connects workers and customers |
| Independent contractor | Worker not employee |
| On-demand | Work as needed |
| Algorithmic management | Software directs, monitors |
| Task-based | Discrete jobs, not ongoing employment |

### Scale

| Metric | Estimate |
|--------|----------|
| US gig workers | 10-15% of workforce (some gig work) |
| Primary gig workers | 1-2% of workforce |
| Global market | $500B+ (2023) |
| Growth | 15%+ annually |

### Major Platforms

| Sector | Platforms |
|--------|-----------|
| Ride-sharing | Uber, Lyft, Didi, Grab, Ola |
| Delivery | DoorDash, Uber Eats, Grubhub, Deliveroo |
| Task work | TaskRabbit, Handy |
| Freelancing | Fiverr, Upwork |
| Care | Care.com |

## How It Works

### The Business Model

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Platform | App matching workers to tasks |
| Commission | 20-30% of transaction |
| Surge pricing | Prices rise with demand |
| Rating systems | Workers rated by customers |
| Deactivation | Low-rated workers removed |

### Algorithmic Management

| Function | How It Works |
|----------|--------------|
| Task allocation | Algorithm assigns rides/deliveries |
| Pricing | Dynamic based on demand |
| Routing | GPS directs workers |
| Monitoring | Location, speed, behavior tracked |
| Incentives | Bonuses for completing X tasks |
| Nudging | "One more ride to reach bonus" |

### Worker Experience

| Upside | Downside |
|--------|----------|
| Flexibility | Unpredictable income |
| Low barriers | No benefits |
| Immediate pay | No sick leave |
| Multiple platforms | Deactivation risk |
| No boss (sort of) | Algorithmic control |

## Worker Classification

### The Core Legal Question

| Employee | Independent Contractor |
|----------|----------------------|
| Employer controls work | Worker controls work |
| Benefits, protections | No benefits required |
| Workers' comp, unemployment | None |
| Minimum wage applies | No floor |
| Right to organize | Difficult |

### Platform Arguments

| Claim | Response |
|-------|----------|
| "Workers are independent" | But platforms control everything |
| "Flexibility is valued" | But flexibility can be offered to employees |
| "Workers choose hours" | But not conditions or pay |
| "Technology company" | "Transportation company using technology" |

### Legal Battles

| Jurisdiction | Outcome |
|--------------|---------|
| California AB5 | Workers presumed employees |
| Prop 22 | Reversed AB5 for gig platforms |
| UK Supreme Court | Uber drivers are workers |
| Spain | Delivery riders are employees |
| EU Platform Work Directive | Classification presumption |

## California as Battleground

### AB5 (2019)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| ABC test | Strict independent contractor test |
| Presumption | Worker is employee unless proven otherwise |
| Effect | Would make most gig workers employees |
| Industry response | Massive campaign against |

### Proposition 22 (2020)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Spending | $200M by platforms (most expensive ballot measure) |
| Outcome | Passed 58-42% |
| Effect | Exempted app-based drivers from AB5 |
| Compromise | Some benefits (health subsidy, accident insurance) |
| Legal challenge | Partially struck down, appealed |

### Significance

| Lesson | Implication |
|--------|-------------|
| Platform power | Can outspend opposition |
| Worker ambivalence | Many voted for Prop 22 |
| Regulatory difficulty | Hard to close loopholes |
| Model | Other states watching |

## The Deeper Point

The gig economy is [[Institutions and Progress|institutional]] arbitrage in real time—platforms exploiting gaps in [[Labor Law and Business|labor law]] that channeled 20th-century employment toward worker protections. The same [[Entrepreneurship|entrepreneurial]] energy that created innovative matching platforms also found ways to avoid minimum wages, benefits, and collective bargaining. Whether this represents legitimate innovation or exploitation depends on what you think labor law is for. If it's obsolete bureaucracy, gig economy is liberation. If it's protection against power imbalance, gig economy is regression. The practical question is whether workers have genuine alternatives: when Uber is better than unemployment, drivers may rationally choose it; when it's worse than employment they can't access, the choice is constrained. The [[Platform Monopolies|platform monopoly]] structure matters too: when one platform dominates, workers lose the flexibility argument (they need that platform, whatever its terms). The gig economy reveals that employment law only works if it covers the forms of work that actually exist—and technology enables new forms faster than law adapts. The deeper institutional question: should the rules of work follow the platform's definition of the relationship, or should they follow the economic reality of who controls what?
