# AI and Labor

Automation's latest wave—artificial intelligence—tests whether institutions can channel disruption productively. Every previous automation wave (textile machinery, assembly lines, computers) created more jobs than it destroyed, though the transition was painful for those displaced. Generative AI and large language models may be different: they affect cognitive work, not just manual labor, and develop faster than workers can adapt. Whether AI follows the historical pattern (net job creation) or breaks it (technological unemployment) depends on institutional choices about education, social insurance, and the distribution of AI's gains.

|------|--------|--------|

| Finding | Evidence |
|---------|----------|
| Net job creation | More jobs after automation than before |

| Institutional response | Education, unions, social insurance |
| Time scale | Decades for adjustment |

## The Deeper Point

AI and labor is where [[Institutions and Progress|institutional]] choices will determine whether transformative technology benefits broadly or concentrates gains. The technology itself is neutral—it can augment workers or replace them, depending on how it's deployed. [[Entrepreneurship|Entrepreneurial]] energy in AI can flow toward productivity enhancement (tools that make workers more effective) or labor substitution (systems that eliminate jobs). The [[Business Regulation|institutional]] framework—education systems, social insurance, labor law, antitrust—will channel that energy. Previous automation waves ultimately created more jobs than they destroyed, but the transition periods were brutal (artisans became factory workers; factory workers became service workers). Whether AI follows this pattern or breaks it depends on: (1) whether new tasks emerge fast enough; (2) whether workers can access them; and (3) whether gains are shared. The historical pattern required institutions—unions, public education, social security—that took decades to build. AI's speed may not allow that time. The deepest question isn't "will AI take jobs?" but "who will own AI, and will they share the gains?" If AI follows the pattern of [[Financialization]] and [[Platform Monopolies]]—concentrated ownership, captured rents—inequality will widen regardless of net employment effects. If AI tools become widely accessible and competition diffuses the benefits, the optimistic scenario is possible. [[Baumol's thesis|Baumol]] applies: the same technology can be channeled toward productive or extractive ends.
