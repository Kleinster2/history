# Norwegian Economic History

From Europe's poorest country to its richest. Norway's transformation—fishing and shipping nation to oil superpower—and its sovereign wealth fund model show how institutional design can escape the [[Resource Curse|resource curse]] that trapped others.

## Avoiding the Resource Curse

### The Norwegian Paradox

Other oil-rich nations experienced:
- Dutch disease (manufacturing decline)
- Corruption and rent-seeking
- Boom-bust volatility
- Institutional degradation

Norway experienced:
- Continued diversification
- Low corruption
- Stability
- Institutional strengthening

### Institutional Design

| Institution | Function |
|-------------|----------|
| State ownership (Statoil/Equinor) | Public capture of rents |
| Tax regime | High government take |
| Sovereign wealth fund | Intergenerational savings |
| Fiscal rules | Limited annual spending |
| Transparency | Public accountability |

### Comparison

| Factor | Norway | Other Oil States |
|--------|--------|------------------|
| State capacity (pre-oil) | Strong | Often weak |
| Democracy | Established | Often absent |
| Diversification | Maintained | Often lost |
| Corruption | Minimal | Often endemic |

## The Deeper Point

Norway's resource wealth management is a triumph of [[Institutions and Progress|institutional design]]. The sovereign wealth fund embodies intergenerational thinking—saving wealth for future citizens rather than spending it today. The 3% rule prevents the boom-bust cycles that plague other resource economies. State ownership of Equinor ensures that oil rents flow to the public rather than private shareholders.

But the deeper point is that Norway could design these institutions because it already had strong institutions. Democratic accountability, bureaucratic competence, social trust, and the Nordic cooperative tradition preceded oil discovery. Countries with weak institutions that discover oil often see the resource amplify dysfunction rather than create prosperity.

The contrast with the [[Resource Curse|resource curse]] cases is stark: Norway had what economists call "good institutions" before oil, and oil strengthened them. Nigeria, Venezuela, and others had weak institutions, and oil weakened them further. The resource itself is neutral—what matters is the institutional framework that determines who captures the rents and how they're used.

Norway's challenge now is managing decline. The fund provides a cushion, but transitioning workers and regions away from oil requires the same institutional competence that managed the boom. The real test of Norwegian institutions comes when the oil runs out.
