# Suez Crisis

In July 1956, Egyptian President [[Gamal Abdel Nasser]] nationalized the Franco-British [[Suez Canal Company]] one week after the United States and Britain withdrew funding for the Aswan High Dam; by November, Britain, France, and Israel had launched a secretly coordinated invasion of Egypt under the [[Sèvres Protocol]], and by January Britain's prime minister [[Anthony Eden]] had resigned, the pound had been rescued by an emergency [[IMF]] drawing on American terms, and the United Kingdom's place among the postwar superpowers was over. The plot — Israeli forces into Sinai (October 29), an Anglo-French ultimatum demanding both sides withdraw from a canal they were nowhere near (October 30), and Anglo-French air strikes followed by airborne landings at Port Said (October 31–November 6) — collapsed within eight days not on the battlefield but in foreign-exchange markets: the [[Bank of England]] hemorrhaged reserves at a rate that, sustained, would have forced sterling devaluation, and US Treasury Secretary [[George Humphrey]] conditioned access to a $561.5 million IMF drawing and a $738.5 million stand-by on a ceasefire. Egypt had already sunk roughly 40 ships to block the canal and Syrian saboteurs had destroyed three pumping stations on the Iraq-Baniyas pipeline, cutting two-thirds of Europe's Middle East oil flow and forcing petrol rationing in Britain from December 1956 through May 1957. The crisis closed the [[BP|Anglo-Iranian]]-era British primacy over Middle East oil that the [[1953 Iranian Coup]] had tried to extend, demonstrated that postwar sterling was a US-discretionary asset, brought down Eden in favor of his chancellor [[Harold Macmillan]] (the same man who had told him to fold), and produced the historical moment after which "superpower" applied only to the United States and the Soviet Union. Suez was, in Denis Healey's phrase, "the only successful use of sanctions in history" — and the template for every subsequent American use of the dollar system as an instrument of statecraft, from oil-importer financing in the [[Petrodollar System|1974 petrodollar architecture]] through the [[Iran Sanctions|SWIFT cutoff of 2012]].

## Strategic Context: Britain After 1945

### The Sterling Position

By 1956 Britain was an empire whose finances had been on US life support since 1941. The pound was overvalued at $2.80, sterling balances held abroad were roughly £4 billion (much of it owed to Commonwealth holders who had financed Britain's wartime imports), and the country could not run a current-account deficit for more than a few weeks without losing reserves it could not replenish from US sources. The [[Bretton Woods]] system formalized this dependency: sterling was a reserve currency in name but a US-discretionary asset in practice, since any run could only be stabilized through IMF drawings or Federal Reserve swap lines that required Washington's consent.

### The Canal as Imperial Artery

The Suez Canal, opened in 1869 by [[Ferdinand de Lesseps]], had become indispensable to British imperial economics by the time Disraeli bought the Egyptian government's 44% stake in 1875. By 1956 it carried roughly two-thirds of Europe's Middle East oil — about 1.2 million barrels per day by tanker, complementing 800,000 b/d that moved via the [[Trans-Arabian Pipeline]] from Saudi Arabia to Lebanon and the Kirkuk-Baniyas line from Iraq to Syria. Britain had withdrawn its last troops from the Canal Zone in June 1956 under the 1954 Anglo-Egyptian Agreement, ending a 74-year military occupation. The Suez Canal Company itself remained a private joint Franco-British enterprise, though Egyptian sovereignty over the surrounding territory was now uncontested.

## Path to Nationalization

### The Aswan Funding Withdrawal

In December 1955 the United States, Britain, and the World Bank had pledged roughly $70 million in initial financing for the [[Aswan High Dam]], conceived as a counterweight to Soviet aid offers. Through the first half of 1956 Nasser drifted toward Moscow on arms procurement (the Czech-Egyptian arms deal of September 1955), recognized the People's Republic of China, and rejected the [[Baghdad Pact]]. Secretary of State [[John Foster Dulles]] concluded the project should be killed. On July 19, 1956, with Eisenhower's assent in a ten-minute Oval Office meeting, Dulles publicly withdrew the US offer, citing financial uncertainty but motivated by Nasser's nonalignment. Britain followed the same day; the World Bank, whose participation was contingent on bilateral aid, withdrew shortly after.

### Nasser's Counter

On July 26, 1956, the third anniversary of King Farouk's overthrow, Nasser delivered a speech in Alexandria announcing the nationalization of the [[Suez Canal Company]]. He calculated — correctly — that revenue from the canal could finance Aswan within a decade. The decree placed the canal under an autonomous Egyptian agency under the Ministry of Commerce, promised compensation to shareholders at the previous day's Paris Bourse closing price, and guaranteed continued operation under the existing 1888 [[Convention of Constantinople]] terms governing free passage. Nasser had given Egyptian engineers years of preparation; canal pilots were on station within hours.

## The Sèvres Conspiracy

The plan was developed at a villa in Sèvres outside Paris between October 22 and October 24, 1956. Israel would attack Egypt across the Sinai Peninsula. Britain and France would then issue an ultimatum demanding both sides withdraw to ten miles from the canal — a stipulation designed to require Egyptian withdrawal from territory Israeli forces had not yet reached. When Nasser refused, Britain and France would intervene "to separate the combatants" and seize the canal zone. The intended outcome was the overthrow of Nasser, though no successor government had been organized. Eden, recovering from biliary surgery and reportedly using Benzedrine to manage exhaustion, insisted the British role remain secret; the Protocol of Sèvres was deliberately constructed to allow plausible deniability that did not survive the first week of operations.

## The Financial War on Britain

The decisive front was not Sinai or Port Said. It was the foreign exchange market, where the United States used its control of sterling support and IMF lending to force a British climbdown within a week.

### The Run on Sterling

Sterling came under sustained selling pressure from the morning of October 30. The Bank of England's reserves, which had stood at about $2.4 billion at the start of October, fell by roughly $50 million in the four trading days from October 30 to November 2, with losses accelerating as it became clear the United States would not provide support. At that rate sustained losses would have forced devaluation within weeks. Holders of sterling balances around the Commonwealth and the Middle East began converting to dollars.

| Date (1956) | Sterling action |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Oct 30 | Anglo-French ultimatum triggers selling |

| Nov 6 (morning) | Eden polls Macmillan; Macmillan reports the reserve position is untenable |

### The American Lever

US Treasury Secretary George Humphrey, acting under Eisenhower's instructions, refused to authorize support operations and prepared to sell part of the US government's sterling-denominated bond holdings if Britain did not accept a ceasefire. The mechanism was deliberately simple: the United States would not block an IMF drawing outright, but US opposition meant the Executive Board would not approve one. Without an IMF facility, Britain could not borrow against its reserves to defend the parity. Macmillan, who had been a hawk during the planning phase, became the cabinet voice insisting that the financial position required immediate capitulation — a reversal so complete that one Treasury official described him as having traveled "first in, first out."

### The IMF Drawing (December 1956)

Once Britain had accepted the ceasefire and Israeli, French, and British forces began withdrawal under the cover of the [[UN Emergency Force]] organized by Canadian Foreign Minister [[Lester B. Pearson]], the United States permitted financing on conditional terms:

| Component | Amount | Date | Terms |
|-----------|--------|------|-------|
| IMF first credit tranche drawing | $561.5 million | December 1956 | Approved without conditionality |
| IMF stand-by arrangement | $738.5 million | December 1956 | Available over 12 months, beyond first tranche |
| Export-Import Bank line | $500 million | February 1957 | Required congressional release |

The package totaled roughly $1.8 billion — comparable in real terms to a sovereign rescue. Humphrey then warned Macmillan at a Paris meeting that Britain "could not afford to draw a single penny of the stand-by" without further damaging confidence. The lesson was unmistakable: sterling's defense depended on US permission, and US permission required policy alignment.

## Oil Supply Collapse

### The Choke Points

| Route | Pre-crisis flow | Status during crisis |
|-------|----------------|----------------------|
| Suez Canal (tankers) | 1.2 million b/d to Europe | Closed by 40 blockships sunk by Egypt (22 in Port Said) |
| Trans-Arabian Pipeline (Saudi Arabia → Sidon, Lebanon) | ~400,000 b/d | Saudi Arabia embargoed UK and France |
| Kirkuk → Baniyas (Iraq → Syria) | ~400,000 b/d | Syrian saboteurs destroyed three IPC pumping stations |
| Trans-Mediterranean tanker | residual | Routed around Cape of Good Hope, adding ~15 days |

Two-thirds of Western Europe's oil supply was disrupted simultaneously by Egyptian, Saudi, and Syrian action. The canal remained closed until April 1957, when a UN-organized salvage operation cleared the blockships. Petrol rationing was introduced in Britain on December 17, 1956, and continued until May 14, 1957. The crisis exposed Western Europe's dependence on Middle Eastern hydrocarbon transit and accelerated investment in alternatives: very large crude carriers capable of profitable Cape routing, Libyan and Algerian crude development, North Sea exploration that would eventually yield commercial discoveries in 1969–70, and Soviet pipeline imports through the [[Druzhba Pipeline]] (operational 1964).

### The Saudi Embargo

Saudi Arabia's embargo of Britain and France was the first deliberate use of Arab oil as a coercive instrument against Western consumers — the precedent for the [[1973 Oil Crisis]] seventeen years later. Crucially, Saudi Arabia continued shipments to the United States and to other neutral parties. The embargo demonstrated that Arab producers could discriminate by customer when politically motivated, a capability that became permanent in the [[OPEC]]-era price regime.

## The Soviet Nuclear Threat

On November 5, 1956, Soviet Premier [[Nikolai Bulganin]] sent letters to Eden, French Prime Minister Guy Mollet, and Israeli Prime Minister Ben-Gurion threatening Soviet "rocket weapons" against the three capitals if hostilities did not cease. The threat was almost certainly not credible — Soviet ICBM capability was years away — but its timing maximized political pressure on cabinets already deliberating over the sterling crisis. Eisenhower used the moment to demand restraint from all parties while reinforcing his own message to London: US financial support was conditional on immediate ceasefire. The Soviet intervention was a rhetorical accelerant; the American financial coercion was the operative cause of British withdrawal.

## Aftermath

### Eden's Fall

Anthony Eden left for Jamaica on November 23, 1956, ostensibly to recover from exhaustion. During his absence Macmillan and Rab Butler — both of whom had supported the operation — coordinated to ensure he would not return to office. Eden's resignation letter of January 9, 1957, cited declining health from "bad abdominal operations" and acknowledged increased reliance on stimulants while denying these had affected his Suez judgment. Macmillan succeeded him as Prime Minister on January 10. The man who had told Eden the reserves could not be defended now inherited Britain's relations with Washington — and immediately set about repairing them.

### The Eisenhower Doctrine (January 1957)

Eisenhower's January 5, 1957 address to Congress announced what became known as the [[Eisenhower Doctrine]]: US economic and military assistance, including the use of US armed forces, would be made available to any Middle Eastern state requesting protection against communist aggression. Congress authorized $200 million in aid. The doctrine formalized what Suez had revealed empirically: the United States was now the primary external security guarantor of the Middle East, replacing the British-French condominium that had governed the region since the 1919 [[Sykes-Picot Agreement|San Remo Conference]]. US Marines would land in Lebanon eighteen months later (July 1958) under doctrine authority.

### End of Three-Power "Superpower"

Before Suez, contemporary usage applied the word "superpower" to three states — the United States, the Soviet Union, and the British Empire. After Suez, only the first two. The shift was diagnostic rather than terminological: Suez had demonstrated that Britain could not conduct an independent foreign policy involving the use of force against US opposition. France drew an opposite conclusion — that European autonomy required nuclear weapons and freedom from Anglo-American institutions — and accelerated its [[Force de Frappe]] program; the Treaty of Rome was signed five months after the ceasefire (March 25, 1957), with French support partly motivated by Suez.

|------|-------|

The pace of British decolonization tripled in the decade after Suez relative to the decade before. The decisive change was not anti-colonial pressure, which had been mounting since 1945, but the recognition in Whitehall and the Treasury that empire's economic logic no longer functioned: the metropole could not finance imperial commitments without US tolerance, and US tolerance was withdrawing in favor of bilateral relationships with newly independent successor states.

### The Long Aswan Postscript

Egypt completed the Aswan High Dam with Soviet financing and engineering, with construction beginning in 1960 and the dam fully operational in 1970. The Soviet Union supplied roughly $1.1 billion in credits and 1,800 Soviet technicians and engineers worked on the project. The dam became a centerpiece of Egyptian state-building and a permanent fixture of Soviet-Egyptian relations that survived until Sadat's expulsion of Soviet advisors in 1972. From an institutional perspective, the project demonstrated that the developing world had acquired an alternative patron capable of financing large infrastructure outside the World Bank framework — a competitive dynamic that shaped Cold War development economics for the next three decades.

## The Economic-History Lens

### Currency Hegemony as Discipline

Suez is the cleanest case in modern economic history of reserve-currency status functioning as an instrument of strategic discipline. The United States did not impose tariffs, freeze assets, or use military force against Britain. It signaled willingness to sell sterling and to withhold IMF support. Within four trading days that signal alone collapsed the British cabinet's resolve. The mechanism is identical in structure to the [[Iran Sanctions|SWIFT cutoff of Iranian banks in 2012]], the [[Russian Central Bank reserve freeze]] of 2022, and the secondary-sanctions architecture that the US Treasury has built around the dollar over the subsequent decades. Suez is the prototype.

The implication, observed at the time by Healey and developed later by [[Susan Strange]] in *Sterling and British Policy* (1971), is that monetary hegemony is itself a form of power that operates without conventional military instruments and yet produces the same submission outcomes. Britain in 1956 was militarily uncontested in the eastern Mediterranean. The British defeat was administered by the New York Federal Reserve and the IMF Executive Board.

### Hegemony Transition Without War

Most accounts of [[Hegemonic Transition]] focus on cases — Athens-Sparta, Spain-Netherlands, France-Britain, Britain-Germany — where rising and falling powers fought directly. Suez is the canonical case of a transition completed without combat between principals. The senior power (Britain) initiated the operation; the dominant power (the United States) used financial pressure to stop it; the senior power complied within a week and accepted a permanently subordinated international position. No shots were fired between the United States and Britain. This pattern would be repeated, in a different form, in the US containment of Japan's financial position during the [[Plaza Accord]] of 1985 and in US pressure on European banks over Iran sanctions enforcement from 2006 onward.

### The Sanctions-Effectiveness Question

Sanctions are usually judged by their failure rate. The Hufbauer-Schott-Elliott literature finds modest behavioral change in roughly one-third of cases. Healey's claim — that Suez is "the only successful use of sanctions in history" — overstates the count but captures the analytical point: the conditions under which financial sanctions actually work are narrow and well-defined. The target must depend on the dominant currency for transaction settlement, reserve composition, or sovereign borrowing. The dominant power must control the gatekeeping infrastructure (in 1956, the IMF; in 2012, SWIFT). The target's political class must be vulnerable to short-horizon costs (sterling reserves measured in weeks of imports, not years). When all three conditions hold, sanctions can produce capitulation in days, not years. Britain in 1956 satisfied all three. Iran under [[JCPOA]] negotiations in 2013–15 partially satisfied them. Russia in 2022 satisfied none.

### Suez and the Petrodollar

The petrodollar architecture of 1974 was the inverse of the Suez settlement. In 1956 the United States used dollar leverage to dismantle British primacy over Middle East oil flows. In 1974 the United States used military protection (replacing the British role) to anchor Arab oil revenues in the dollar system. The intermediate step was the 1957 [[Eisenhower Doctrine]], the 1971 [[Bretton Woods]] collapse, the 1973 oil embargo, and the Kissinger-Saudi understanding. Each step was a refinement of the lesson Suez had taught: that primary control over the Middle East oil-finance-security nexus was now an American function, and that the dollar was the discipline mechanism rather than the gold link.
