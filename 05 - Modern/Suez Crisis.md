# Suez Crisis

In July 1956 Egypt's Gamal Abdel Nasser nationalized the Anglo-French Suez Canal Company after John Foster Dulles abruptly withdrew American funding for the Aswan High Dam. Britain and France—fearing the loss of their imperial jugular and the precedent it set for [[Resource Nationalism|resource nationalism]]—secretly colluded with Israel at Sèvres (October 1956) to stage a pretext for invasion: Israel would attack Sinai, the two European powers would intervene "to separate the combatants," and in the confusion they would retake the canal and topple Nasser. The military operation worked; the economic operation failed spectacularly. As British and French paratroopers landed in early November, the [[Pound Sterling|pound sterling]] hemorrhaged reserves, and President [[Dwight Eisenhower]] refused to let the [[IMF|International Monetary Fund]] extend a lifeline until British forces withdrew. Chancellor Harold Macmillan—the crisis's original hawk—flipped within days and told the Cabinet the run on sterling was unstoppable. On November 6, with troops still advancing, Britain accepted a humiliating ceasefire. Prime Minister [[Anthony Eden]] resigned two months later. Nasser kept the canal, the Soviet Union gained a foothold in the Arab world, and the [[United States]] emerged as the indispensable outside power in the Middle East. For the [[British Empire Economy|British Empire]], Suez was the moment the imperial balance sheet was posted on the front page: Britain could no longer finance independent action abroad without American consent. It was less a battle lost than an audit failed.

## The Setting

### A Canal, An Empire, An Economy

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Canal opened | 1869 ([[Ferdinand de Lesseps]]) |
| Length | 164 km, sea-level, no locks |
| Strategic value | Britain–India route cut by 6,000 km |
| Ownership | Suez Canal Company (French-controlled, 44% British after Disraeli's 1875 share purchase) |
| Traffic by 1955 | ~15,000 ships/year, ⅔ oil tankers |
| British lifeline | ~⅔ of Western Europe's oil passed through |

The canal was not just a shortcut—it was the physical embodiment of the British-Indian-Gulf economic system. When Britain lost India in 1947, the canal became the last tangible asset tying Britain to its Eastern commerce.

### Britain's Postwar Position

| Metric | State in 1956 |
|--------|---------------|
| War debt | Still working off WWII lend-lease obligations |
| Dollar reserves | ~$2.2 billion — enough for a few weeks of trade |
| Sterling balances | ~£3.5 billion held abroad (claims on Britain) |
| Convertibility | Partial; full convertibility a standing ambition |
| Defense spending | 8% of GDP; three carrier groups, global commitments |
| Currency status | Sterling still second reserve currency, but vulnerable |

Britain looked powerful from the outside. The books said otherwise. Any real run on the pound would force either devaluation or an American bailout—and everyone in the Treasury knew it.

### The Aswan Trigger

| Step | Date | Actor |
|------|------|-------|
| Egypt requests Aswan Dam financing | Late 1955 | Nasser |
| US/UK/World Bank tentatively agree | December 1955 | Dulles / Eden |
| Nasser recognizes PRC; buys Czech arms | 1955-1956 | Nasser |
| Dulles abruptly withdraws offer | 19 July 1956 | US State Department |
| Nasser nationalizes the canal | 26 July 1956 | Egypt |
| Justification | Canal tolls to finance the dam | — |

Dulles's withdrawal was meant as a snub. Nasser turned it into a rallying cry. The speech announcing nationalization—delivered in a cotton merchant's Arabic rather than the formal classical—was a piece of political theater that set the Arab world alight.

## The Collusion

### The Protocol of Sèvres

| Clause | Commitment |
|--------|------------|
| Israel attacks Sinai | 29 October 1956 |
| Britain and France issue ultimatum | 30 October — "both sides" withdraw 10 miles from canal |
| Egypt refuses (as expected) | 30 October |
| Anglo-French bombing | 31 October |
| Landings at Port Said | 5 November |
| Stated goal | "Separate the combatants, protect the canal" |
| Actual goal | Topple Nasser, retake the canal |

The secret meeting at Sèvres (24 October 1956) was attended by Selwyn Lloyd (UK), Christian Pineau (France), and David Ben-Gurion (Israel). Eden later had the British copy of the protocol destroyed. It survived because the French and Israelis kept theirs.

### Motivations

| Country | Objective |
|---------|-----------|
| Britain | Restore canal control, preserve empire, deter nationalization wave |
| France | Punish Nasser for supporting Algerian FLN |
| Israel | Break Egyptian blockade of Tiran; preemptive strike on growing Egyptian military |

Three different wars bundled into one operation—a structure that made the political coalition fragile.

## The Economic Weapon

### The Run on Sterling

| Period | Sterling movement |
|--------|-------------------|
| August-October 1956 | Steady outflow; ~$100M/month |
| Early November | Outflow accelerates sharply |
| Week of 5 November | ~$280M reserves lost in one week |
| 6 November | Macmillan reports reserves at dangerous level |
| Cumulative | ~$450M of a $2.2B cushion gone |

The outflow had three components: speculators shorting the pound, sterling-area central banks moving reserves to dollars, and American political signaling. The US Treasury dumped sterling on the open market—an unmistakable message rather than a policy decision.

### Eisenhower's Veto

| Action | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| US refuses emergency oil shipments | UK/France face oil rationing within weeks |
| US blocks IMF drawing | Britain's standard backstop closed |
| US signals opposition to Export-Import Bank loan | No alternate dollar source |
| US sponsors UN ceasefire resolution | International legitimacy denied |

Eisenhower's move was not a threat; it was an accounting fact made operational. The dollar was the plumbing of the postwar trading system, and the United States could shut the valves.

### Macmillan's Flip

| Date | Macmillan position |
|------|---------------------|
| July-October | Prime hawk; "I know Nasser; he's another Mussolini" |
| 1-5 November | Treasury warnings grow urgent |
| 6 November | Reports reserves collapsing; urges immediate ceasefire |
| Cabinet account | "First in, first out" |

Macmillan—who had started the crisis promising financial support for military action—became the Cabinet member most insistent that Britain must stop. The man who had been Eden's closest ally emerged from the crisis as his replacement. Eden resigned 9 January 1957 citing ill health. Macmillan became Prime Minister on 10 January. The ambition was not concealed.

## The Aftermath

### Immediate Consequences

| Event | Date | Effect |
|-------|------|--------|
| Ceasefire | 6 November 1956 | Operations halted mid-advance |
| Canal closure | November 1956 – April 1957 | Egypt blocked canal with sunken ships |
| UN Emergency Force deployed | November 1956 | First UN peacekeeping mission |
| Oil rationing in UK/France | Winter 1956-57 | Tankers rerouted around Cape |
| US fills canal dues from Egypt | 1957 | Nasser retains ownership |
| Eden resigns | January 1957 | Macmillan succeeds |

### The Strategic Realignment

| Shift | Result |
|-------|--------|
| Eisenhower Doctrine | January 1957 — US fills the Middle East vacuum |
| Arab nationalism | Nasser canonized across the region |
| Soviet entry | USSR finances Aswan Dam, arms Egypt |
| Sterling area | Dissolution accelerates; members begin shifting reserves |
| Decolonization | Accelerates in Africa (Ghana independent March 1957) |
| UK defense policy | 1957 Sandys White Paper: cuts carriers, nukes replace empire |
| UK and Europe | Britain's first EEC application begins incubating; lodged 1961 |

### The Shipping Response

| Innovation | Cause | Effect |
|------------|-------|--------|
| Very Large Crude Carriers | Canal closures of 1956-57 and 1967-75 | Ships too large for Suez but economical around Cape |
| Supertanker era | Onassis, Niarchos, Ludwig build 200,000+ DWT ships | Oil transport costs collapse |
| Pipeline alternatives | Trans-Arabian, Iraq-Turkey | Bypass canal risk |
| Route diversification | Cape, Panama, Hormuz | Chokepoint risk priced in |

The Suez closures were the direct cause of the supertanker revolution. The oil majors and Greek shipowners concluded that a canal that could be closed by political decision was not a reliable piece of infrastructure. They responded not by politicking but by building ships big enough to make the canal optional. It is one of the purest examples in the 20th century of engineering routing around politics. See [[Shipping and Maritime Trade]] and [[Oil Economy]].

## The Economic Meaning

### Currency as Weapon

| Mechanism | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Reserve currency dominance | Whoever controls the clearing network can veto policy |
| Convertibility pressure | A run that can't be stopped forces capitulation |
| IMF gatekeeping | Political discretion over emergency liquidity |
| Oil denomination | Oil imports priced in dollars; no dollars, no oil |

Suez was the first peacetime demonstration that the United States could stop an allied military operation without firing a shot, simply by withholding dollar liquidity. Every subsequent use of financial sanctions traces its logic to November 1956.

### The Lag Finally Closes

| Marker | Date |
|--------|------|
| US economy surpasses Britain | ~1872 |
| US financial net creditor | 1914 |
| Bretton Woods (dollar officially supreme) | 1944 |
| Suez (dollar politically supreme) | 1956 |
| Lag from economic to currency dominance | ~84 years |

Bretton Woods had written the dollar's supremacy into treaty. Suez enforced it. Between July and November 1956, the gap between the paper status and the political reality closed completely.

### Hegemonic Transition

| Previous transitions | Pattern |
|----------------------|---------|
| Venice → Portugal (1498) | Slow, via route substitution |
| Dutch → British (1713) | Slow, via succession of wars |
| British → American (1945-1956) | Slow, then sudden |

The strange feature of the British-American transition was that the outgoing and incoming hegemons were allies. There was no war between them—only the gradual accumulation of dollar claims against sterling ones, followed by the moment when the ledger was called in. Suez was the call.

### The Nationalization Wave

Suez normalized nationalization of foreign-owned strategic assets. If Nasser could nationalize the Suez Canal Company and get away with it—indeed, be rewarded for it—the precedent was unmistakable.

| Successor nationalization | Year |
|---------------------------|------|
| Cuba nationalizes US oil | 1960 |
| Algeria nationalizes French oil | 1971 |
| Libya (Qaddafi) nationalizes BP | 1971 |
| OPEC participation agreements | 1972-1973 |
| Full Saudi nationalization of Aramco | 1980 |

[[Mohammad Mossadegh]] had tried this in 1951 and been overthrown. Nasser succeeded because the American veto over Britain was now greater than the American appetite for another covert operation—and because oil was still fungible enough in 1956 that Egypt's act did not directly threaten Western supply the way Iran's had.

## The Deeper Point

Suez is usually remembered as a military fiasco. It was not. The military operation worked: Anglo-French forces reached their objectives on schedule with limited casualties. What collapsed was the financial scaffolding that made military operations meaningful. Britain could still project power; Britain could no longer afford to project power without American permission.

This is the distinction that matters for [[Why Empires Fall|imperial decline]]. Empires rarely collapse because their armies fail. They collapse because the economic base that paid the armies has eroded beneath them, and the collapse is revealed the moment an external shock demands that the base be drawn down. For Rome that moment came in a series of third-century currency debasements. For Spain it came when the Dutch revolt outlasted the silver flow from Potosí. For Britain it came in eight days in November 1956, when the Bank of England watched reserves drain and Macmillan realized that the American Treasury had become the effective policymaker for British foreign affairs.

The term "Suez moment" has entered the vocabulary of international affairs to describe exactly this: the point at which a declining power's budget meets its ambitions and the budget wins. It is a diagnostic term, not a predictive one—a Suez moment can only be recognized in retrospect. But the diagnosis always points to the same structure: a hegemon whose currency is no longer enough, whose creditor is no longer friendly, whose allies are no longer following, and whose adversaries correctly read that the old rules no longer bind.

Britain's saving grace was that it read its own diagnosis quickly. Within eighteen months the Sandys White Paper had rebuilt British defense policy around the nuclear deterrent and the abandonment of empire east of Suez. Within five years Britain had applied to the European Economic Community. Within a decade sterling had devalued twice more and the sterling area was dissolving. Each step represented accepting a smaller Britain in exchange for a solvent one. It is not a bad template for hegemonic decline, and the alternative—continuing to pretend—would almost certainly have been worse.

The deeper irony is that the canal itself mattered far less than the crisis did. Suez was closed twice more (1967-1975), supertankers made the Cape route competitive, and global oil shipping adapted within a decade. What could not be adapted was the political reality the crisis exposed. You can reroute a ship around Africa. You cannot reroute a currency around its issuer.
