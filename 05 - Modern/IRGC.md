# IRGC

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps was created because the revolutionaries who seized power in 1979 did not trust the Shah's US-trained army — its name contains no reference to "Iran" because it exists to safeguard the *revolution*, not the nation. Forged in the crucible of the [[Iran-Iraq War]]'s eight-year trench warfare, where chemical weapons and human wave attacks produced "hard men with very fixed views," the IRGC evolved from a parallel militia into the dominant institution of the Islamic Republic. Ayatollah Khamenei — who lacked his predecessor's religious credentials — made the IRGC his power base, reversing Khomeini's ban on military involvement in politics and militarizing the system. By the 2020s, the IRGC controlled a vast economic empire through bonyads (revolutionary foundations), construction conglomerates, telecoms, and oil contracts, while its Quds Force — under [[Qasem Soleimani]] until his assassination in 2020 — ran proxy networks from Lebanon to Yemen. Maximum pressure sanctions strengthened the IRGC at the expense of Iran's middle class and moderates, concentrating economic power in the one institution best positioned to evade them.

## Origins

### Why a Parallel Army?

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Shah's army | US-trained, US-modeled, US-equipped |
| Revolution's victory | Came when the regular army declared neutrality (US helped persuade this) |
| Revolutionary distrust | Feared the army was too aligned with American interests |
| Solution | Create a parallel force loyal to the revolution, not the state |
| Name | "Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps" — no "Iran" in the title |

### The Iran-Iraq War Crucible

The [[Iran-Iraq War]] transformed the IRGC from a militia into a hardened military force:

| Dimension | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Trench warfare (1980–88) | Created "hard men with very fixed views" |
| Chemical weapons exposure | Deepened hatred of the West, which looked away |
| Strategic solitude | Iran fought alone → IRGC became the survival instrument |
| Ballistic missiles | IRGC drove the missile program as desperate countermeasure |
| Post-war reconstruction | IRGC secured contracts → economic empire begins |

## Khamenei and the IRGC

When Khamenei became supreme leader in 1989, he lacked [[Ayatollah Khomeini|Khomeini]]'s religious authority and popular legitimacy. He needed an alternative power base.

| Khomeini's policy | Khamenei's reversal |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Banned IRGC from politics | Made IRGC his primary political instrument |
| Religious authority legitimized rule | Military-security establishment legitimized rule |
| Multiple power centers | IRGC as dominant institution |

Khamenei's decision was reinforced by watching the Soviet Union collapse through reform (1989–91). His conclusion: ideological systems that open the door to reform unravel. The IRGC was both the instrument of preventing reform and the institution that benefited from preventing it.

## The Proxy Network

The IRGC's Quds Force, under [[Qasem Soleimani|Soleimani]], built a regional network of non-state actors — what Iran called the "axis of resistance":

| Proxy | Location | Function |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Hezbollah | Lebanon | Created after Israel's 1982 invasion; Iran's most capable proxy |
| Hamas | Gaza | Palestinian cause as power projection beyond Shia/Persian identity |
| Houthis | Yemen | Strait chokepoint leverage |
| Iraqi Shia militias | Iraq | Post-2003 influence network |
| Various | Syria | Regime support for Assad |

### Strategic Logic

Iran adopted the Palestinian cause not primarily from ideology but as a geopolitical instrument — the one cause that could transcend its inherent limitations as a Shia Persian nation surrounded by Sunni Arabs and Turks. The [[Iranian Revolution\|Reagan-era Beirut barracks bombing]] (1983, 241 Marines killed) was an early proof of concept: "someone as hawkish as President Reagan packed his bags and left the region."

### Soleimani's Assassination (January 2020)

Trump's killing of [[Qasem Soleimani|Soleimani]] removed the one person who had personal relationships with proxy leaders and the authority to direct them.

### Soleimani's Assassination and the Proxy Coordination Gap

Without [[Qasem Soleimani|Soleimani]], the proxy network lost its coordinating hand. No successor had the personal relationships or authority to direct timing across the "axis of resistance." The consequences — including the chain of miscalculations from October 7th through the 2026 war — belong to the geopolitical present rather than economic history.

## Economic Empire

The IRGC is not just a military organization — it controls a significant share of Iran's economy:

| Sector | IRGC Role |
|--------|-----------|
| Bonyads (revolutionary foundations) | Seized private assets post-revolution; operate as conglomerates |
| Construction | Major government contracts (post-war reconstruction → permanent role) |
| Telecommunications | Controlling interests |
| Oil and gas | Contracts and operations |
| Import/export | Sanctions evasion creates profit opportunities |
| Finance | Linked banking networks |

### The Sanctions Paradox

[[Iran Sanctions|Maximum pressure]] was meant to weaken the regime. Instead, it enriched the IRGC:

| Mechanism | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Formal economy contracts | IRGC better positioned than private sector to navigate sanctions |
| Smuggling networks | Sanctions evasion = profitable enterprise |
| Middle class weakened | Private sector squeezed; IRGC alternatives grow |
| Foreign exchange controls | IRGC controls access to hard currency |
| Result | "Weakened our best allies and strengthened our worst adversaries" (Vaez) |

## The Deeper Point

The IRGC illustrates a pattern visible across military history: organizations created for one purpose develop economic interests that transform their political role. The Roman legions settled land and became political kingmakers. The Ottoman Janissaries became a commercial class that resisted reform. The Soviet military-industrial complex became an economic interest group that outlived communism. The IRGC followed the same trajectory: born from revolutionary distrust of the regular army, forged in an eight-year war, enriched by reconstruction contracts, and entrenched by sanctions that eliminated private-sector competition.

The economic lesson is that [[Iran Sanctions|sanctions]] can have precisely the opposite of their intended effect when they differentially impact civilian and military economies. Sanctions squeezed Iran's middle class — historically pro-Western and the natural constituency for reform — while the IRGC's control of smuggling networks, government contracts, and hard currency access meant it *profited* from isolation. Maximum pressure was, inadvertently, maximum empowerment of the institution it was meant to weaken.
