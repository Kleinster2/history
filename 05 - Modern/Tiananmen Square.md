# Tiananmen Square

The Tiananmen Square protests (April 15 – June 4, 1989) were the largest popular mobilization in modern Chinese history — and far more than a demand for liberal rights against a monolithic state. They were social democratic protests *about* ongoing economic reforms: a demand that if China was going to undergo massive socioeconomic redistribution through marketization, then press freedom, human rights, and democratic participation were essential to give that process an acceptable direction. The military crackdown killed hundreds and led to tens of thousands of arrests. But the deeper consequence was structural: Tiananmen resolved the question of whether the CCP's monopoly on power would be challenged, and set the conditions for the accelerated neoliberal growth of the 1990s. In the reading of [[Wang Hui]] and the Chinese New Left, neoliberalism came to China not despite Tiananmen but *because* of it.

## Background: The Overheating Economy

### Reform and Its Discontents (1978-1988)

[[Deng Xiaoping]]'s reform program — launched after his return to power in 1978 — delivered extraordinary growth but generated distributional crises that made the question of political participation urgent. By the mid-1980s, [[China's Rise|China's economy]] was growing at ~11% per annum. The problems were those of an overheating economy:

| Indicator | Status by 1988 |
|-----------|----------------|
| GDP growth | ~11%/year |
| Inflation (official) | ~20%, heading higher |
| Savings behavior | Bank runs as citizens anticipated price shock |
| Corruption perception | Acute — arbitrage between planned and market prices |
| Urban discontent | Widespread across workers, intellectuals, civil servants |

### The Dual-Track Price System

China operated a complex multi-tiered price system that stabilized parts of society through fixed prices while allowing liberalized prices in other sectors. The attractive features masked a structural flaw: resources, services, and goods were siphoned from the stable-price sector to the liberalized sector where profits could be made. This created endemic corruption and a distributional struggle over who got access to credit, who could raise prices, and who was gouged in the process.

When market reformers within the party proposed comprehensive price liberalization in the summer of 1988, the response was predictable: citizens began pulling money from savings accounts to prepare for the inflationary shock they knew was coming, since it was clear that prices were not equilibrated. This created the conditions for a financial crisis on top of the existing social tensions.

### Who Was Hurt

The inflationary squeeze hit certain groups hardest:

| Group | Position |
|-------|----------|
| Public servants | Fixed incomes eroded by inflation |
| Academics and intellectuals | Distinguished class, wrong end of the inflationary bargain |
| University students | Facing uncertain futures in a chaotic economy |
| Urban workers | Real wages declining amid price instability |

These were not marginal groups. The competitive civil service exam system had been reinstated in the late 1970s and early 1980s, drawing China's most talented into government careers that were now being devalued by inflation.

## The Protests

### Scope and Character

The demonstrations in Tiananmen Square — Beijing's political heart — were only part of a much broader ferment of social protest across urban China. The protests drew in not just students but large parts of the working class and white-collar workforce. This breadth is what made them genuinely threatening to the party.

The comparison to [[Solidarity]] in Poland is instructive: like Solidarność, which was the great trade union movement that unhinged communism in Poland, the Tiananmen protests were about the consequences for urban society of an increasingly disorderly, chaotic, and politically indecisive process of reform. But where Solidarność protested stagnation, the Chinese protesters were protesting the distributional consequences of rapid growth.

The demands for rights were tied to a diagnosis of the massively lopsided nature of socioeconomic change in China in the 1980s. This wasn't a fossilized Stalinist system — it was a rapidly growing, increasingly marketized system, and that made democratic participation *more* essential, not less. The protesters were arguing, in effect, for a social democratic framing of China's market transition.

### The Crackdown

On June 3-4, 1989, the PLA enforced martial law. Hundreds were killed; tens of thousands were arrested or imprisoned. The PLA had played this role before — it was the decisive factor in stabilizing the Cultural Revolution — but calling in the army was a sign of existential crisis for a party that claimed to represent Chinese society.

## Aftermath: Austerity and Sanctions

### Domestic Contraction

The more conservative forces that took power in Beijing after the crackdown imposed a deflationary austerity regime in 1989-1990. This was not purely punitive — it was the macroeconomic answer to the overheating that had fueled the protests. The combination was devastating:

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Austerity policy | Domestic demand deliberately shrunk |
| World Bank funding cut | International agencies withdrew |
| FDI collapse | Corporate investment dried up (1989-90) |
| Sudden stop | External finance halted — classic emerging market crisis |

China experienced what any developing market faces under a sudden stop of external finance: forced contraction. Growth slowed abruptly in the early 1990s as Beijing worked through the internal macroeconomic logic that had produced the protests in the first place. Inflation was finally brought under control by the mid-1990s.

### US and International Response

The international reaction was severe but ultimately short-lived:

| Measure | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| World Bank lending | Suspended — China was a significant recipient |
| Military contacts | Frozen — ending ties built since Kissinger's 1971 visit |
| Trade sanctions | Limited initially; focused on finance and governance |
| FDI | Sharp private-sector pullback (1989-90) |
| Back-channel | Bush administration privately signaled continued interest |

By 1988, over 15,000 foreign ventures operated in China with notional commitments of $28 billion (actual paid-in capital ~$12 billion). There were real pressure points — and the balance was hugely asymmetric: Chinese exports to the US could account for as much as a fifth of all Chinese exports, while US trade with China was a couple of percentage points of US trade.

## Deng's Southern Tour (January 18 – February 21, 1992)

### The Internal Debate

The open question after Tiananmen was whether China's reform program could continue. Conservatives within the CCP argued it should not — that the 1980s reforms had led to unhinged growth, chaos, protests, and crackdown. China should maintain a repressive conservative position and slow growth to make it manageable.

### The Decision

Deng Xiaoping resolved this by boarding his personal train and processing from Beijing to the southern and coastal centers of Chinese economic growth — Shenzhen, Zhuhai, Shanghai. His message: "Development, comrades, is the hard truth." The reforms would not only continue but accelerate:

| Element | Direction |
|---------|-----------|
| Special economic zones | Not retreating — doubling down |
| Foreign investment | Surged dramatically in early 1990s |
| Marketization | Accelerated |
| Political opening | None — the party's monopoly was settled |
| Implicit bargain | Growth continues, repression available if needed |

This was a double message. The only way out was through — China would push on with economic development even at the risk of further destabilization and, if necessary, further repression. The question of whether the party's monopoly would be fundamentally challenged had been answered in June 1989. From that point on, every subsequent phase of Chinese development was about how the party maintained its autonomy over an increasingly complex and affluent society.

## The Clinton Pivot (1993-1994)

### The Human Rights Coalition

The US political response to Tiananmen echoed for years. Key Democratic figures — [[Nancy Pelosi]] and [[Joe Biden]] — led a coalition to condition Most Favored Nation (MFN) trade status on political and human rights criteria. Pelosi, representing a California constituency with a large, motivated Chinese community, pushed for concrete measures like protecting Chinese students from forced return after their studies. Biden focused on nuclear proliferation and missile transfers.

Clinton seized on this in his 1992 campaign against Bush, attacking Republicans as too China-friendly. Taking office in 1993, he extended MFN status conditionally, with a rolling review tied to human rights — hoping to bully Beijing into conformity.

### The Delink

Beijing refused to comply. When American delegations — notably Secretary of State Warren Christopher — visited China, they were treated "incredibly roughly." Beijing was in no mood to compromise.

Meanwhile, the American business community recognized it had been steamrollered by the liberal-national security coalition into destabilizing conditionality on what it saw as a fundamental long-term economic relationship. Within the Clinton administration itself, Treasury Secretary Lloyd Bentsen was pushing hard on the economic opportunity. This was the era of NAFTA and WTO — the administration was in the business of large-scale trade liberalization.

In what seemed at the time like a humiliating U-turn, Clinton in spring 1994 **delinked** US-China economic relations from human rights. The Tiananmen issue faded as a policy driver. American relations with China were restructured around two realities:

1. China was an autonomous actor that would not be bullied
2. Powerful American business and consumer interests were invested in Deng's opening

The two sides — Deng's southern strategy and American commercial interests — clicked into place, setting the course toward [[WTO]] accession in 2001. A notable irony: many of the same actors (Biden, Pelosi) who shaped this 1994 compromise were still making China policy three decades later.

## Four Phases of Chinese Growth

Tooze's framework for understanding post-Mao China, with Tiananmen as the hinge between phases one and two:

| Phase | Period | Character | Key Dynamic |
|-------|--------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | 1978-1989 | Reform and overheating | Deng's opening, rapid growth, inflation crisis, political explosion |
| 2 | 1992-2003 | Doubling down | Ultra-rapid growth, SOE restructuring ("smashing the [[iron rice bowl]]"), mass rural-to-urban migration. Inequality and outright poverty actually *increased* |
| 3 | 2003-~2012 | Social democratic stabilization | Explicit program to keep the wheels on: social spending, private property formalized (late 1990s), mortgage market opened, housing boom as growth driver |
| 4 | ~2012/2020-present | Xi's new normal | Party autonomy reasserted, anti-corruption as instrument against party capture by bourgeois society, explicit reference to 1989 lessons, end of construction boom |

### The Phase 2 Social Cost

The most radical market reform came at the end of the 1990s: comprehensive stripping down of the Maoist heavy industrial sector. An entire generation of Mao-era industrial workers — people who had become workers in the 1950s, 1960s, and 1970s — were thrown out of their jobs. Meanwhile, the new growth in Chinese cities was driven by peasant migrants from the countryside. This produced a huge and largely unreported wave of social protest in the late 1990s and early 2000s.

### The Private Property Pivot

Private property rights were only fully formalized in the late 1990s, ending large-scale provision of housing in kind by state employers. The opening of private mortgages became the big driver of growth — and of Chinese bourgeois accumulation — from that point forward. This is the structural foundation of the housing boom that defined Phase 3 and whose end marks the transition to Phase 4.

## The Deeper Point

Tiananmen is conventionally read as a heroic liberal failure — students demanding rights, crushed by tanks. The economic reading is more unsettling. The protesters had a *diagnosis*: that rapid marketization without democratic participation would produce lopsided, corrupt, socially destructive outcomes. They were right. But the regime's answer — accelerated market reform *under* authoritarian control, with the demonstrated willingness to use force — produced the most dramatic economic transformation in modern history. The Chinese New Left's argument is that the depoliticization of economic growth was the essence of Chinese neoliberalism: stripping distributional questions out of public debate, pushing them into the party apparatus, and ultimately paralyzing the party's political brain even as its technocratic capacity grew. Whether this represents a superior form of managing complex societies or a deferred reckoning is the central question of 21st-century political economy.
