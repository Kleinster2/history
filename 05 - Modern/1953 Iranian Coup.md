# 1953 Iranian Coup

In August 1953, the CIA and MI6 overthrew Iran's democratically elected Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh — the first covert regime change operation in American history — after Mossadegh nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (later [[BP]]) in 1951, threatening British control over Iran's petroleum. Britain organized a devastating global embargo that crippled Iranian oil exports and its economy, but the Truman administration refused to intervene, viewing Mossadegh as a legitimate nationalist. When Eisenhower entered office in January 1953, the Dulles brothers — John Foster at State, Allen at CIA — reframed the crisis as Cold War containment, arguing that economic chaos would deliver Iran to communism. Operation Ajax (CIA) and Operation Boot (MI6) launched in August: the first attempt failed and the Shah fled to Rome, but a second attempt using paid mobs, bribed military officers, and planted propaganda succeeded within days. The Shah returned to rule for 26 more years, and a new oil consortium replaced BP's monopoly — 40% British, 40% American, 20% others — marking the entry of US oil companies into Iranian production and the end of Britain's exclusive control. The coup's immediate success masked its long-term catastrophe: twenty-six years of accumulated grievance over foreign interference in Iranian sovereignty became the founding narrative of the [[Iranian Revolution]], making the 1953 coup arguably the most consequential covert operation of the 20th century.

## The Nationalization Crisis (1951-1953)

### Anglo-Iranian's Profit Imbalance

The fundamental grievance was economic. Anglo-Iranian (BP) had operated Iran's oil under terms grossly favorable to the company since the [[Persian Economic History#The D'Arcy Concession|D'Arcy concession of 1901]].

| Period | Iran's Revenue | Company's Profit | Context |
|--------|---------------|------------------|---------|
| 1901-1933 | 16% of net profits | 84% | Original D'Arcy terms |
| 1933 renegotiation | Fixed royalty per ton | Still vastly lopsided | Reza Shah renegotiated but couldn't break the structure |
| 1947-1950 | ~$7 million/year | $100+ million after tax | British government earned more in tax on BP than Iran earned in royalties |
| 1950 (Aramco deal) | — | — | [[Chevron|Aramco]] offered Saudi Arabia 50-50; Iran demanded the same |

When Anglo-Iranian refused a 50-50 split — a deal that Aramco had already offered Saudi Arabia — Mossadegh's nationalization movement became unstoppable.

### Mossadegh and Nationalization

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| March 1951 | Iranian parliament (Majlis) votes unanimously to nationalize Anglo-Iranian |
| April 1951 | Mossadegh becomes Prime Minister on the nationalization platform |
| May 1951 | National Iranian Oil Company (NIOC) formally takes control |
| June 1951 | British technicians begin leaving; Abadan refinery crippled |
| October 1951 | Last British staff expelled from Abadan |

### Britain's Economic Warfare

Unable to intervene militarily (Truman opposed it), Britain organized a comprehensive economic campaign to strangle Iranian oil exports.

| Measure | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| Global boycott | British navy intercepted tankers; buyers threatened with legal action |
| Refinery expertise | British technicians' departure shut down Abadan |
| Frozen assets | Iranian sterling balances frozen in London |
| Diplomatic pressure | British lobbied allies against purchasing Iranian oil |
| Iranian oil exports | Collapsed from 660,000 bpd (1950) to near zero (1952-53) |
| Economic impact | Government revenue plummeted; Mossadegh relied on austerity and foreign exchange reserves |

The embargo demonstrated a lesson that would echo through OPEC's later history: controlling the resource is necessary but not sufficient. Without the refining expertise, shipping networks, and market access controlled by the majors, nationalization alone could not deliver economic benefits.

## The Road to the Coup

### Truman Refuses

| Factor | Truman's Position |
|--------|-------------------|
| Cold War context | Feared pushing Iran toward Soviet Union |
| Mossadegh's legitimacy | Recognized him as a nationalist, not a communist |
| Anti-colonialism | Sympathetic to decolonization impulse |
| Assessment | Mossadegh was the best available leader |
| Decision | Refused British requests for joint intervention |

### Eisenhower/Dulles Reframe

| Factor | New Framing |
|--------|-------------|
| Cold War lens | Economic crisis → communist takeover |
| Tudeh Party | Iranian communist party growing (used as justification, though never close to power) |
| Dulles brothers | John Foster (State) and Allen (CIA) ideologically anti-communist and close to oil industry |
| British lobbying | MI6's "Monty" Woodhouse sold the coup as anti-communist, not pro-oil |
| Decision | Eisenhower authorized Operation Ajax (early 1953) |

The reframing was decisive. Britain wanted the coup to protect its oil concession. The US agreed to participate because it was presented as Cold War containment. The actual communist threat in Iran was minimal — the Tudeh Party had some support but no path to power — but the framing allowed Eisenhower to authorize what Truman had refused.

## Operation Ajax

### Planning

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| CIA lead | Kermit Roosevelt Jr. (Theodore Roosevelt's grandson) |
| MI6 lead | "Monty" Woodhouse |
| Budget | ~$1 million (CIA funds), additional British contributions |
| Assets | Bribed military officers, paid street mobs, clergy allies, newspaper editors |
| Propaganda | Planted stories portraying Mossadegh as communist sympathizer |
| Shah's role | Required to sign royal decrees dismissing Mossadegh |

### First Attempt (August 15-16)

| Event | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Shah signs decrees | Dismissing Mossadegh, appointing General Zahedi |
| Colonel delivers decree | Mossadegh warned in advance; colonel arrested |
| Mossadegh acts | Announces coup attempt on radio |
| Shah flees | To Baghdad, then Rome |
| CIA assessment | Roosevelt was ordered to leave; he refused |

### Second Attempt (August 19)

| Event | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Paid mobs | Pro-Shah demonstrations organized in Tehran's bazaar |
| Military units | Bribed officers move tanks to key positions |
| Street fighting | Pro- and anti-Mossadegh forces clash |
| Mossadegh's house | Shelled by tanks; Mossadegh escapes, then surrenders |
| Zahedi takes power | Announces new government on radio |
| Shah returns | August 22, from Rome |

### The Aftermath

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Mossadegh | Tried, sentenced to 3 years in prison, then house arrest until death (1967) |
| Tudeh Party | Crushed; used as post-hoc justification |
| Shah's power | Dramatically expanded; never again tolerated a strong PM |
| SAVAK | Secret police established (1957) with CIA assistance |
| Political freedom | Destroyed for 26 years |

## The Oil Settlement (1954)

The coup did not restore BP's monopoly. The United States, having provided the decisive intelligence and operational support, demanded a share.

| Company Group | Share | Members |
|---------------|-------|---------|
| British (BP) | 40% | Anglo-Iranian (renamed British Petroleum) |
| American | 40% | Exxon, Mobil, Chevron, Texaco, Gulf (8% each) |
| French | 6% | CFP (later Total) |
| Dutch (Shell) | 14% | Royal Dutch Shell |

| Feature | Old Terms | New Consortium |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| Iran's share | 16% of net profits | 50-50 profit split |
| Control | BP monopoly | International consortium |
| Duration | 60 years | 25 years (with renewal options) |
| National sovereignty | Nominal | NIOC owned the oil; consortium operated |

The irony: Mossadegh had demanded 50-50 terms that Anglo-Iranian refused. After a coup, the destruction of Iranian democracy, and three years of economic crisis, Iran got the 50-50 deal it had originally sought — minus its democracy.

## Template for Covert Intervention

The 1953 coup established a template the CIA would replicate:

| Operation | Date | Target | Outcome |
|-----------|------|--------|---------|
| Iran (Ajax) | 1953 | Mossadegh | Shah restored; 26-year grievance |
| Guatemala (PBSUCCESS) | 1954 | Arbenz | Overthrown; decades of civil war |
| Congo | 1960 | Lumumba | Assassinated; Mobutu installed |
| Chile | 1973 | Allende | Overthrown; Pinochet dictatorship |

In each case, the pattern was similar: a nationalist leader threatening Western economic interests was reframed as a Cold War threat, covertly removed, and replaced with an authoritarian ally. In each case, the long-term consequences undermined the interests the intervention was meant to protect.

## The 26-Year Fuse

### How the Coup Fueled the Revolution

| Mechanism | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| National memory | Every Iranian schoolchild learned about the coup; most Americans never heard of it |
| Sovereignty wound | Confirmed Iran's historical grievance about foreign interference |
| Shah's legitimacy | Permanently tainted — seen as American/British puppet |
| Political closure | By destroying secular-nationalist opposition, the coup left only religious opposition |
| Khomeini's narrative | "Great Satan" thesis drew directly on 1953 |

The coup eliminated the middle ground in Iranian politics. Mossadegh represented secular, democratic nationalism — the tradition that could have modernized Iran without theocracy. By destroying that alternative, the coup ensured that when revolution came in 1979, it would be led by the one institution the Shah couldn't fully suppress: the clergy.

### The [[White Revolution]] Connection

The Shah's authoritarian modernization program (1963) was itself a response to the post-coup political landscape. Having eliminated democratic participation, the Shah attempted top-down reform — land redistribution, literacy campaigns, women's suffrage — without political institutions. The program's economic successes and social dislocations both traced back to the concentration of power that the coup enabled.

## The Deeper Point

The 1953 Iranian coup is the foundational case study in resource sovereignty versus international capital. Mossadegh's nationalization posed a simple question: who controls the oil? The coup's answer — international companies, backed by Western states — set the template that [[OPEC]] would later overturn collectively through the nationalization wave of the 1970s. What one country could not achieve alone (Iran in 1951), a coordinated cartel accomplished (OPEC in 1973-76).

The economic lesson is about the difference between formal sovereignty and effective control. Iran nominally owned its oil after 1951, but without refining capacity, market access, or diplomatic support, ownership was meaningless. The coup restored effective foreign control under slightly better terms. Only the combination of nationalization plus OPEC coordination plus the 1973 price revolution gave producer countries both formal ownership and effective market power.

The human cost was a 26-year experiment in authoritarian modernization that ended in revolution, theocracy, and four decades of isolation. The coup's architects achieved their immediate objective — restoring Western access to Iranian oil — at the price of creating the anti-Western regime they most feared. In the long arc of [[Persian Economic History]], the coup is the hinge: everything before it explains why it happened; everything after it flows from its consequences.
