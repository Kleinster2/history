# Nuclear Power History

Nuclear power is the most expensive bet civilization ever placed on a single technology — and the most instructive failure of cost control in industrial history. The physics worked. The economics didn't — except in France, where standardization and state control produced the opposite outcome from the same technology. The story is not about atoms but about institutions: who builds, who regulates, who bears cost overruns, and who decides when to stop. After four decades of decline, AI's insatiable demand for 24/7 baseload power has reopened the question.

| Era | Character |
|-----|-----------|

| 1953–1965 | Atoms for Peace — civilian dreams |

## Atoms for Peace (1953–1965)

### The Speech

President Eisenhower addressed the UN General Assembly on **December 8, 1953**, proposing that nations contribute fissionable material from weapons stockpiles to a new international agency for peaceful uses — agriculture, medicine, and power generation. The speech created the institutional framework: the **IAEA**, established July 29, 1957 in Vienna.

### "Too Cheap to Meter"

On **September 16, 1954**, AEC Chairman Lewis Strauss told the National Association of Science Writers:

> "It is not too much to expect that our children will enjoy in their homes electrical energy too cheap to meter."

The quote became nuclear power's original sin — a promise the technology could never keep. Strauss almost certainly meant fission, not fusion; his speech focused entirely on fission applications. When asked by reporters, he confirmed: electricity "too cheap to be metered, just as we have water today."

### Early Reactors

| Reactor | Date | Significance |
|---------|------|--------------|
| EBR-I (Idaho) | Dec 1951 | First electricity from nuclear (lit 4 bulbs) |
| Obninsk (USSR) | Jun 1954 | First grid-connected nuclear plant (5 MWe) |
| Calder Hall (UK) | Oct 1956 | First commercial-scale (50 MWe) |
| Shippingport (US) | Dec 1957 | First full-scale US commercial reactor (60 MWe) |

## The Great Bandwagon (1965–1973)

### The Ordering Boom

General Electric and Westinghouse offered "turnkey" contracts — fixed-price plants delivered ready to operate — as loss leaders to establish the market. Utilities rushed in.

| Period | US reactor orders |
|--------|-------------------|
| By 1967 | 50+ orders placed |
| 1967–1974 | 196 additional orders |
| **1973 (peak)** | **50 orders in one year** |
| NRC permits 1967–1978 | 12+/year average |

### Why Utilities Ordered

| Factor | Logic |
|--------|-------|
| Turnkey pricing | GE/Westinghouse absorbed risk |
| Scale economies | Bigger plants = cheaper per kW (in theory) |
| Baseload demand | Postwar electricity growth ~7%/year |
| [[Oil Crisis]] fear | After 1973, energy independence mattered |
| Regulatory ease | AEC both promoted and regulated (conflict of interest) |

### The Cost Problem Emerges

Original 1960s estimates: **$100–300 million per GW**. By the time plants were completed in the 1980s: **$2–4 billion per GW** — a roughly **10x real-cost increase**.

| Cost driver | Mechanism |
|-------------|-----------|
| Regulatory changes mid-construction | NRC (replacing AEC in 1975) imposed new safety requirements on partially built plants |
| No standardization | Each plant a one-off engineering project |
| Construction delays | 5-year projects became 10–15 year projects |
| Interest during construction | At 1970s inflation (10%+), borrowing costs compounded |
| One-way ratchet | Every safety addition raised costs; no mechanism to reduce them |

## The Catastrophe of Cost Overruns

### Shoreham (Long Island, New York)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Developer | Long Island Lighting Company (LILCO) |
| Construction | 1973–1984 |
| Original estimate | **$65–75 million** |
| Final cost | **$5.5 billion** (~85x original) |
| Interest share | Over one-third of final cost |
| Outcome | Shut down 1989 after a single low-power test — never delivered commercial power |
| Cause of death | Suffolk County declared evacuation impossible post-TMI and Chernobyl |

First large US commercial reactor decommissioned. $5.5 billion for zero kilowatt-hours.

### WPPSS — "Whoops" (Washington State)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Plants ordered | 5 |
| Original estimate (all 5) | **$4.1 billion** |
| Revised estimate (1981) | **$23.8 billion** (~6x) |
| Completed | 1 (Plant 2, now Columbia Generating Station, 1984) |
| Canceled | 4 |
| Default (July 1983) | **$2.25 billion** — largest municipal bond default in US history at the time |

### Marble Hill (Indiana)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Developer | Public Service Indiana |
| 1973 concept | **$700 million** |
| 1977 construction start | **$1.4 billion** estimate |
| 1983 revised estimate | **$7.1 billion** (~10x original) |
| Outcome | Canceled January 1984 after spending **$2.5 billion** — two half-built units, never generating |
| Demolished | 2005 |

### The Pattern

| Plant | Original estimate | Final cost/spend | Multiple |
|-------|-------------------|------------------|----------|
| Shoreham | $70M | $5.5B | 85x |
| WPPSS (5 plants) | $4.1B | $23.8B (est.) | 6x |
| Marble Hill | $700M | $2.5B (canceled) | 10x (of concept) |
| Vogtle 3&4 (2020s) | $14.3B | $34–35B | 2.4x |

Every generation of US nuclear plants cost more than projected. The root cause was institutional, not technical: fragmented regulation, non-standardized designs, construction-period financing in an inflationary environment, and no mechanism to capture learning-curve savings across projects.

## Three Mile Island (1979)

### The Accident

**March 28, 1979** — a loss-of-coolant accident caused a partial fuel melt in Unit 2 of the Three Mile Island plant near Middletown, Pennsylvania. No deaths directly attributed. ~140,000 people evacuated voluntarily. Cleanup cost approximately **$1 billion** (~$2B in 2025 dollars), lasting from 1979 to 1993.

### The Economic Consequences

| Metric | Before TMI | After TMI |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Annual reactor orders | 20–50 | **Zero** |
| Construction permits issued | 12+/year | Effectively none |
| Reactors canceled 1980–1984 | — | **52** |
| Total cancellations 1970s–80s | — | **100+** |
| New US site construction starts after 1979 | — | **None until Vogtle, 2009** |

The last US reactor completed before the 2020s revival was **Watts Bar Unit 2** (TVA) — construction begun 1973, suspended 1985, finally commissioned **October 2016**. A 43-year construction odyssey.

## Chernobyl (1986)

**April 26, 1986** — reactor explosion at Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant, Ukrainian SSR. The worst nuclear disaster in history.

### Economic Impact

| Measure | Value |
|---------|-------|
| Estimated total economic damage | **$235 billion** |
| Agricultural land removed from service | 784,320 hectares |
| Forest production halted | 694,200 hectares |
| Contribution to Soviet fiscal crisis | Significant — accelerated USSR's collapse |

### The Global Retreat

| Country | Response |
|---------|----------|
| Italy | Referendum Nov 8–9, 1987 — **80% voted** to abolish nuclear statutes; all plants closed by 1990 |
| Germany | Anti-nuclear movement intensified; 2000 nuclear consensus set closure by 2022 |
| Sweden | Voted to phase out (later partially reversed) |
| Switzerland | Restrictive referendums |
| Netherlands, Finland | Postponed planned plants |
| France | Continued building — completed program undisturbed |

## The French Exception

### The Messmer Plan (1974)

Prime Minister Pierre Messmer announced France's nuclear commitment on **March 6, 1974** — a direct response to the [[Oil Crisis]]. The plan called for 13 reactors immediately, with an ambitious long-term target.

### What Was Actually Built

| Period | Achievement |
|--------|-------------|
| 1977–1999 | 58 reactors commissioned |
| Build rate | 56 reactors in 15 years |
| Current fleet (2025) | 56 operable reactors, 61.4 GWe |
| Nuclear electricity share | Rose from ~7% (1975) to **over 75%** (1990) |
| France today | Highest nuclear share of any large economy |

### Why France Succeeded

| Factor | How it worked |
|--------|---------------|
| **Standardized design** | All 58 reactors are PWRs based on Westinghouse technology, replicated with minimal variation — learning-curve cost reductions |
| **Single builder** | EDF (state monopoly) executed the entire program under central direction |
| **Vertical supply chain** | Framatome built specialized heavy-component manufacturing (vessels, steam generators) with guaranteed order books |
| **Political insulation** | Centralized state bypassed local veto points — no referendums, no state utility commissions, no multi-decade court battles |
| **Speed** | First three plants started construction the same year as the Messmer announcement |

### The Contrast

The same underlying technology — pressurized water reactors licensed from Westinghouse — produced opposite economic outcomes in France and the United States. The difference was entirely institutional:

| Dimension | France | United States |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Designs | 1 standardized | 100+ custom |
| Builder | 1 (EDF) | Dozens of utilities |
| Regulator | Cooperative | Adversarial, changing rules mid-build |
| Financing | State-backed | Private, exposed to interest rates |
| Local opposition | Overridden | Empowered (courts, referendums, elections) |
| Cost trajectory | Declining through program | Rising with every plant |

France's nuclear program is the strongest evidence that nuclear power's cost problem is institutional, not inherent. Build the same design repeatedly, with a single competent builder, under stable regulation, and costs fall. Build each plant as a unique prototype, with fragmented oversight and mid-construction rule changes, and costs explode.

## Fukushima (2011)

**March 11, 2011** — magnitude 9.0 earthquake and tsunami caused three reactor meltdowns at Fukushima Daiichi (TEPCO), northeast Japan.

### Japan's Response

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| May 2012 | All 54 commercial reactors shut down for inspection |
| 2015 | First restarts (Sendai Units 1 and 2) |
| 2025 | 14 reactors restarted out of 33 operable |
| Dec 2025 | Local consent for Kashiwazaki-Kariwa restart (TEPCO's first since Fukushima) |
| Dec 2024 | Japan's 7th energy plan: nuclear target of 20% of electricity by 2040 |

### Germany's Final Exit

Fukushima triggered Angela Merkel's reversal of a 2010 life-extension plan. The Bundestag voted in June 2011 to shut all plants by 2022 (extended to April 2023 due to the 2022 energy crisis). Germany's last three reactors — Isar 2, Emsland, Neckarwestheim 2 — shut simultaneously on **April 15, 2023**.

## The Nuclear Drought (1990s–2010s)

Cheap natural gas from the [[Shale Revolution]] killed nuclear plants that TMI and Chernobyl hadn't. The US fleet peaked at **112 operating reactors**. Between 1990 and 2024, 18 reactors were permanently decommissioned — almost all because they couldn't compete with $2–3/MMBtu gas.

### Westinghouse Bankruptcy (2017)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Filed Chapter 11 | March 29, 2017 |
| Cause | Massive overruns at Vogtle (GA) and V.C. Summer (SC) — both AP1000 design |
| Toshiba (owner) losses | ~**$9 billion** |
| Emerged from Chapter 11 | August 2018 (sold to Brookfield) |

### V.C. Summer Abandonment (2017)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Units | 2 AP1000 reactors, South Carolina |
| Original estimate (2008) | **$9.8 billion** |
| Revised estimate (2017) | **$25 billion** |
| Abandoned | July 2017 after spending ~**$9 billion** — nothing to show |
| Aftermath | "Nukegate" scandal — executives convicted of fraud for misleading regulators |

### Vogtle Units 3 & 4 — The Survivors

The only US new-build to reach completion in decades:

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Licensed | August 2009 |
| Original estimate | **$14.3 billion** |
| Original schedule | Unit 3 online 2016, Unit 4 online 2017 |
| Westinghouse bankruptcy | March 2017 (mid-construction) |
| Final cost | **$34–35 billion** (~2.4x original) |
| Unit 3 commercial operation | July 31, 2023 |
| Unit 4 commercial operation | April 29, 2024 |
| Total construction time | ~15 years |

### NuScale SMR Cancellation (2023)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Project | Carbon-Free Power Project, Idaho |
| Design | 12 small modular reactors, ~462 MWe total |
| Original estimate | **$5.3 billion** |
| Revised estimate | **$9.3 billion** (+75%) |
| Cost per kW | ~$20,100/kW — comparable to Vogtle |
| Target power price | Rose from $55/MWh to $89/MWh |
| Canceled | November 8, 2023 |

The NuScale cancellation showed that SMRs — nuclear's great hope — faced the same cost disease as large reactors. Factory production was supposed to solve the problem. It hadn't yet.

## The AI Renaissance (2024–present)

After ~45 years of decline, a new buyer class emerged: hyperscalers needing 24/7, carbon-free baseload power for AI data centers. They offered what nuclear had always lacked — creditworthy counterparties willing to sign 20-year power purchase agreements.

### The Deals

| Date | Deal | Capacity | Structure |
|------|------|----------|-----------|
| Sep 2024 | Microsoft + [[Constellation Energy]]: TMI Unit 1 restart | 835 MWe | 20-year PPA; $1.6B refurbishment; renamed Crane Clean Energy Center |
| Oct 2024 | Google + Kairos Power: SMR development | 500 MWe | First reactor by 2030 |
| 2024 | Amazon + Talen Energy: Susquehanna co-location | 960 MWe | $650M campus purchase + 1.9 GW PPA through 2042 |
| Jan 2026 | Meta: deals with Vistra, Oklo, TerraPower, Constellation | up to 6.6 GWe | Powering Prometheus AI supercluster, Ohio |

**TMI restart** is the symbolic turning point: the site of America's worst nuclear accident becoming the power source for its AI future. Constellation stock rose 30% on announcement.

### Supply Chain

| Investment | Detail |
|------------|--------|
| DOE $2.7B uranium enrichment | Awards to three companies for domestic HALEU and LEU capacity |
| HALEU bottleneck | Advanced SMRs need High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium; only Centrus produces it in the US; Russia was the main supplier — now sanctioned |
| ADVANCE Act (2024) | Streamlines NRC advanced reactor licensing |

### China's Lead

**Linglong One** (CNNC) — the world's first commercial land-based SMR (125 MWe), located in Hainan Province, on track for commercial operation **H1 2026**. China is building nuclear at a pace no other country matches: 57 operating reactors with more under construction.

| Country | Reactors | Net capacity |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| United States | 94 | 97 GW |
| France | 57 | 63 GW |
| China | 57 | 55 GW |
| Russia | 36 | 27 GW |
| South Korea | 26 | 26 GW |
| Japan | 14 operating (33 operable) | ~13 GW |
| **Global total** | **416 reactors, 31 countries** | **376 GW** |

2024 set a global generation record: **2,667 TWh** — surpassing the previous record of 2,660 TWh (2006). Average capacity factor: **83%** — higher than any other electricity source.

**56 of 68 reactors** commissioned in the past decade were in Asia. The center of gravity has shifted decisively east.

## The Deeper Point

Nuclear power's history is the definitive case study in how institutional design determines technological outcomes. The physics never changed — a pressurized water reactor in France uses the same fission process as one in Long Island. What changed was everything around it: who designed, who built, who regulated, who financed, who could veto. France proved that nuclear power could be cheap, fast, and reliable when built by a single competent organization under stable rules with standardized designs. The United States proved that the same technology becomes ruinously expensive when every plant is a unique prototype, regulation changes mid-construction, and dozens of parties hold veto power.

The AI renaissance doesn't resolve this institutional question — it just adds a new variable. Hyperscalers bring something nuclear never had: deep-pocketed, creditworthy buyers willing to commit for decades. Microsoft's 20-year PPA for TMI provides the demand-side certainty that private capital requires. But the supply side — permitting, construction, cost control — remains the same institutional challenge that produced Shoreham's $5.5 billion monument to zero kilowatt-hours. Whether the new demand translates into actual megawatts depends on whether America can build things again. France answered that question 50 years ago. The US is still trying.
