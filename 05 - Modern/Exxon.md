# Exxon

Standard Oil of New Jersey — the largest fragment of the [[Foundational Firms#Standard Oil|1911 breakup]] — became the most powerful private oil company in history. Jersey Standard inherited Rockefeller's international operations and institutional DNA: relentless efficiency, vertical integration, indifference to public opinion. It co-founded the [[Exxon#Achnacarry Agreement (1928)|Achnacarry cartel]] with Shell and BP (1928), became the senior partner in [[Exxon#Aramco and Saudi Arabia (1948-1980)|Aramco]] (1948), and dominated the postwar oil order as the largest of the [[Oil Economy#The Seven Sisters Era (1920s-1970s)|Seven Sisters]]. Renamed Esso, then Exxon (1972), the company survived the [[Oil Crisis]], the Exxon Valdez disaster (1989), and OPEC nationalization — then merged with [[Mobil]] to form ExxonMobil (1999), the world's largest publicly traded company. Its most consequential legacy may be the climate controversy: Exxon's own scientists confirmed CO2-driven warming in the late 1970s, while the company spent decades funding denial. The same institutional discipline that made it operationally excellent made it the most effective obstacle to climate action.

## Overview

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Origin | Standard Oil of New Jersey (1882, as holding company for Standard Oil Trust) |
| Breakup inheritance (1911) | International operations, largest refining capacity, the "Standard" brand in most markets |
| Name changes | Jersey Standard → Esso (S.O.) → Exxon (1972) → ExxonMobil (1999) |
| Peak status | Largest publicly traded oil company in the world for most of the 20th century |

## From Standard Oil to Jersey Standard (1882-1911)

| Phase | Development |
|-------|-------------|
| 1882 | Standard Oil Trust organized under New Jersey holding company |
| 1899 | Reconstituted as Standard Oil Company (New Jersey) |
| 1911 | Supreme Court orders breakup into 34 companies |
| Inheritance | Jersey Standard got the crown jewels: international operations, largest refineries, 50% of the trust's net value |

The breakup was supposed to create competition. Instead, it created a collection of regional companies that rarely competed directly — and Jersey Standard, holding the international franchise, was positioned to dominate the century of oil.

## Building the Global Empire (1911-1945)

### The Interwar Expansion

| Region | Strategy |
|--------|----------|
| Latin America | Venezuelan oil (Creole Petroleum subsidiary) — became world's largest producer by 1928 |
| Middle East | Joined the Iraq Petroleum Company (1928) alongside BP, Shell, and CFP |
| Dutch East Indies | Stanvac joint venture with [[Mobil|Socony]] (1933) — the fields Japan would later invade |
| Europe | Refining and distribution under the Esso brand |

### The Red Line Agreement (1928)

Jersey Standard and the other majors carved up the former Ottoman Empire's oil — agreeing not to compete independently within the "red line" drawn around the Middle East. This cartel arrangement held for two decades and shaped the region's development.

### The Achnacarry Agreement (1928)

| What | Secret meeting at Achnacarry Castle, Scotland |
|------|----------------------------------------------|
| Who | Jersey Standard, Shell, BP |
| Agreement | "As-Is" — freeze market shares, avoid price wars |
| Effect | The three largest oil companies became a de facto cartel |
| Duration | Informally governed the industry until the 1950s |

## Aramco and Saudi Arabia (1948-1980)

| Date | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1948 | Jersey Standard buys 30% stake in Aramco (founded by [[Chevron]] and [[Texaco]]) |
| 1950 | 50-50 profit-sharing deal with Saudi Arabia — set the template globally |
| 1950s-60s | Aramco becomes the world's most productive oil operation |
| 1973 | Saudi Arabia begins nationalizing Aramco |
| 1980 | Full Saudi control; Exxon exits as operator |

The 50-50 deal was a pivotal innovation: rather than fight nationalization, Jersey Standard offered to split profits equally with the Saudi government. This became the global standard — and it was structured as a tax credit, so the US Treasury effectively subsidized it. American taxpayers funded the arrangement that kept Middle Eastern oil in American corporate hands.

## The Exxon Era (1972-1999)

### Why the Name Changed

"Esso" (phonetic "S.O.") was blocked by other Standard Oil successors in their territories. Jersey Standard needed one global brand. After spending $100 million on naming research, they chose "Exxon" (1972) — meaningless in every language, ownable everywhere.

### Key Developments

| Period | Event |
|--------|-------|
| 1973-74 | [[Oil Crisis]] — Exxon profits soared while public fury grew |
| 1975 | Briefly the world's most valuable company |
| 1980s | Diversification attempts (Reliance Electric, office equipment) — mostly failed |
| 1989 | Exxon Valdez oil spill — 11 million gallons in Prince William Sound, Alaska |
| 1990s | Refocused on core oil/gas; became the industry's most disciplined operator |

### The Valdez Legacy

| Dimension | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| Environmental | Worst US oil spill at the time; ecosystem damage lasting decades |
| Legal | $5 billion punitive damages (reduced to $507 million on appeal) |
| Reputational | Defined corporate environmental negligence |
| Operational | Industry adopted double-hull tankers |

## ExxonMobil (1999-present)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Merger | Exxon + [[Mobil]] (1999) — $81 billion, the largest merger in history at the time |
| Logic | Reunited two Standard Oil fragments; scale for megaprojects |
| Result | World's largest publicly traded oil company |
| Revenue | $400+ billion annually at peak |

| Date | Development |
|------|-------------|

| Legacy | The gap between private knowledge and public advocacy became a defining corporate ethics case |

## The Deeper Point

Exxon is the purest expression of Rockefeller's organizational DNA: vertical integration, cost discipline, long-term planning, and indifference to public opinion. It survived antitrust breakup, two world wars, nationalization, oil crises, and environmental disasters — not through innovation or charisma, but through institutional competence. The company's internal culture — engineering-driven, hierarchical, deeply conservative — produced both its greatest strength (operational excellence) and its greatest failure (climate denial). Exxon is the case study for how organizational DNA persists across a century: the same institutional instincts that made Standard Oil the world's most efficient company made ExxonMobil the world's most effective obstacle to climate action. Institutions optimize for what they were built to do.
