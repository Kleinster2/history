# Chevron

Standard Oil of California — SoCal — inherited the West Coast franchise from the [[Foundational Firms#Standard Oil|1911 breakup]] and turned it into something Rockefeller never had: access to the greatest oil discovery in history. While the other majors dismissed the Arabian Peninsula, SoCal's geologists found oil in Bahrain (1932) and won the Saudi concession from King Ibn Saud for $50,000 in gold (1933) — perhaps the greatest single business deal of the 20th century. The discovery at Dammam No. 7 (1938) led to [[Chevron#Building Aramco|Aramco]], the consortium that reshaped global power. But everything SoCal built in Saudi Arabia was eventually nationalized (completed 1980), proving that in extractive industries access is always temporary. The company adapted by acquiring its fellow Sisters: [[Gulf Oil]] (1984, after T. Boone Pickens' hostile raid) and [[Texaco]] (2001), consolidating three of the Seven Sisters into one and renaming itself Chevron.

## From Standard Oil to SoCal (1911-1930s)

| Inheritance | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| Territory | California, Pacific Northwest, Hawaii |
| Assets | Production (California crude), refineries, the Chevron brand |
| Advantage | Unlike [[Mobil]], SoCal had upstream production |
| Limitation | Entirely regional — no international presence |

SoCal was the quiet sibling: profitable but provincial. That changed when its geologists went looking for oil where no other major would bother.

## The Arabian Discovery (1932-1944)

### Bahrain (1932)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Context | Major oil companies dismissed the Arabian Peninsula |
| Opportunity | SoCal acquired a concession from Gulf Oil (blocked by the Red Line Agreement) |
| Discovery | Oil struck in Bahrain, June 1932 |
| Significance | Proved the Arabian geological formation held oil |

### Saudi Arabia (1933-1938)

| Date | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1933 | SoCal wins Saudi concession from King Ibn Saud — for $50,000 in gold |
| 1936 | Texaco buys 50% (forming Caltex) — SoCal needed capital and marketing reach |
| 1938 | Oil discovered at Dammam No. 7 — after years of dry holes |
| 1944 | Company renamed Arabian American Oil Company (Aramco) |

The Saudi concession may be the greatest single business deal of the 20th century. SoCal secured access to what proved to be the world's largest conventional oil reserves — for less than the cost of a single well today.

### Building Aramco

| Phase | Development |
|-------|-------------|
| 1944-48 | [[Exxon|Jersey Standard]] (30%) and [[Mobil|Socony]] (10%) brought in as partners |
| 1950 | 50-50 profit-sharing agreement with Saudi Arabia |
| 1950s-60s | Saudi production scaled massively; Aramco became the world's most productive oil operation |
| 1973-80 | Gradual Saudi nationalization |
| 1988 | Aramco becomes fully state-owned Saudi Aramco |

## Caltex: The Asian Joint Venture

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Formed | 1936 (SoCal + Texaco, 50-50) |
| Territory | Eastern Hemisphere — Asia, Africa, Australasia |
| Purpose | SoCal had the crude (Bahrain, Saudi); Texaco had the distribution network |
| Scale | Became one of the largest oil operations in Asia |
| Dissolved | 2001 (when Chevron acquired Texaco) |

Caltex operated in the Dutch East Indies alongside [[Royal Dutch Shell|Shell's]] BPM and [[Exxon|Exxon/Mobil's]] Stanvac — putting all three groups at the center of Japan's [[World War II as Convergent Conflicts|resource war]].

## The Deeper Point

Chevron's history is the story of **discovery as destiny**. One geological bet — sending geologists to a peninsula the other majors ignored — gave a regional California refiner access to the largest oil reserves on earth. That single decision (and $50,000 in gold) generated more wealth than any other business transaction in the 20th century. But it also illustrates the fragility of concession-based empires: everything Chevron built in Saudi Arabia was eventually nationalized. The company adapted by acquiring other Sisters (Gulf, Texaco) — consolidating the fragments back toward the scale Rockefeller had before the breakup. The lesson: in extractive industries, access is everything, but access is always temporary. What endures is institutional capability — the ability to find, develop, and operate the next concession when the current one is taken away.
