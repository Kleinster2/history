# Long-Term Capital Management

Long-Term Capital Management was the hedge fund that proved genius could fail — spectacularly, systemically, and in ways that foreshadowed every financial catastrophe to come. Founded in 1994 by John Meriwether, the legendary Salomon Brothers bond trader forced out in the 1991 Treasury auction scandal, LTCM assembled the most intellectually formidable team Wall Street had ever seen: two Nobel laureates (Myron Scholes and Robert Merton), a former Federal Reserve Vice Chairman (David Mullins), and a core of Salomon's best fixed-income arbitrageurs. The pitch was seductive — apply academic finance models to exploit tiny price discrepancies across global bond markets, leveraged to extraordinary scale. For four years it worked brilliantly, returning 21%, 43%, 41%, and 17% to investors. Then in August 1998, Russia defaulted on its domestic debt, triggering a global flight to liquidity that blew apart every spread LTCM was betting would converge. The fund lost $4.6 billion in months. On September 23, 1998, the Federal Reserve Bank of New York orchestrated a $3.625 billion rescue by fourteen banks and brokerages — not to save LTCM, but to prevent its forced liquidation from cascading through every counterparty on Wall Street. It was the first "too interconnected to fail" moment in modern finance, a dress rehearsal for 2008 that nobody heeded.

## The Founder

### John Meriwether and the Salomon Arbitrage Desk

John Meriwether built the most profitable bond arbitrage operation in Wall Street history at [[Investment Banking|Salomon Brothers]] during the 1980s. His desk pioneered quantitative fixed-income trading — hiring PhDs and mathematicians to identify small mispricings between related bonds and exploit them at scale. By the late 1980s, Meriwether's group was generating a disproportionate share of Salomon's profits.

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Role | Vice Chairman, Salomon Brothers |
| Innovation | Academic quant models applied to bond arbitrage |
| Key recruits | Eric Rosenfeld (Harvard finance PhD), Larry Hilibrand, Victor Haghani |
| Culture | Intensely secretive, fiercely competitive, intellectually elitist |

The 1991 Treasury bond scandal ended Meriwether's Salomon career. His trader Paul Mozer had been submitting false bids in Treasury auctions, cornering the market. Meriwether learned of the violations but didn't immediately escalate them to regulators. When the scandal broke, chairman John Gutfreund, president Thomas Strauss, and Meriwether all resigned. Warren Buffett stepped in to save Salomon from collapse.

Meriwether was fined but not charged criminally. He spent two years in the wilderness, then launched LTCM in February 1994 — taking most of his old Salomon team with him.

## Founding and the Dream (1994)

### The Pitch

LTCM's value proposition was simple and compelling: the smartest people in finance, armed with the most sophisticated models in existence, would identify and exploit price discrepancies that the market had left on the table. Convergence arbitrage — the core strategy — bet that spreads between related securities would narrow over time, as they historically always had.

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Founded | February 1994, Greenwich, Connecticut |
| Initial capital | $1.25 billion — largest hedge fund launch in history |
| Fee structure | 2% management fee + 25% performance fee (above the 2/20 industry standard) |
| Lock-up | Three-year investor lock-up (unusual at the time) |
| Minimum investment | $10 million |
| Founding investors | 80 investors, including bank CEOs, Bear Stearns executives, Merrill Lynch clients |

### The Team

The roster was staggering. No hedge fund before or since has concentrated so much intellectual firepower in one place.

| Principal | Background |
|-----------|------------|
| **John Meriwether** | Founder; former Salomon Brothers Vice Chairman, bond arbitrage desk legend |
| **Myron Scholes** | Nobel laureate (1997); co-developer of the [[Derivatives Revolution#The Black-Scholes Revolution|Black-Scholes]] option pricing model |
| **Robert Merton** | Nobel laureate (1997); pioneered continuous-time finance |
| **David Mullins** | Former Vice Chairman of the Federal Reserve Board — resigned from the Fed to join LTCM |
| **Eric Rosenfeld** | Former Harvard Business School assistant professor; Salomon arbitrage veteran |
| **Larry Hilibrand** | Key trader; one of the highest-paid people on Wall Street at Salomon |
| **Victor Haghani** | Key trader; Salomon fixed-income arbitrage MD |
| **Greg Hawkins** | Partner; ex-Salomon |
| **William Krasker** | Partner; ex-Salomon |
| **Dick Leahy** | Partner |

The presence of Scholes and Merton gave LTCM an aura of scientific infallibility. Mullins leaving the Fed — the institution that would later orchestrate LTCM's rescue — signaled that even regulators believed the models worked. Banks fell over themselves to lend to the fund, often waiving collateral requirements entirely.

## The Strategy

### How It Worked

LTCM's core business was convergence arbitrage in fixed-income markets. The fund identified pairs of securities that should, in theory, converge in price — then bet on that convergence.

| Strategy | Mechanism |
|----------|-----------|
| **On-the-run / off-the-run Treasuries** | Buy older ("off-the-run") Treasury bonds at slightly higher yields; short newer ("on-the-run") bonds trading at a liquidity premium. Wait for convergence. |
| **European sovereign bond convergence** | As the Euro approached, bet that Italian and Spanish government bonds would converge toward German Bund yields |
| **Mortgage-backed securities** | Exploit mispricings in the complex securitization market |
| **Interest rate swap spreads** | Bet on the spread between swap rates and Treasury yields |
| **Equity volatility** | Sell index volatility (implied was higher than realized) |
| **Merger arbitrage** | Buy target company stock, short acquirer stock in announced deals |
| **Emerging market sovereign debt** | Including Russian GKOs (government bonds) |

The individual trades were elegant. The problem was that each one earned tiny returns — measured in basis points. To generate meaningful profits from basis-point spreads, you needed enormous leverage.

### The Leverage Question

| Metric | Figure |
|--------|--------|
| Equity base (end 1997, before capital return) | ~$7.5 billion |
| Balance sheet assets | ~$129 billion |
| Leverage ratio (before capital return) | ~17:1 |
| Equity base (early 1998, after returning $2.7B) | ~$4.7 billion |
| Balance sheet assets (early 1998) | ~$125 billion |
| Leverage ratio (early 1998) | ~25:1 |
| Notional value of derivatives positions | ~$1.25 trillion |
| Share of global interest rate swap market | ~5% |

For context: in 1996, Morgan Stanley had a leverage ratio of 27:1 and Salomon Brothers ran at 42:1. LTCM's on-balance-sheet leverage was not wildly out of line with investment banks. What was different was the massive off-balance-sheet derivatives exposure — the $1.25 trillion in notional value that dwarfed the equity base.

The partners believed diversification protected them. They had trades across government bonds, mortgages, equities, and multiple geographies. Their models showed these positions were largely uncorrelated. What the models couldn't capture was a scenario where all their "diversified" trades moved against them simultaneously — because all of them, at bottom, were the same bet: that spreads would converge and liquidity premiums would shrink.

## The Glory Years (1994-1997)

### Returns

| Year | Net Return (after fees) | Notes |
|------|------------------------|-------|
| 1994 | ~21% | Partial year (fund launched February) |
| 1995 | 43% | Best year — convergence trades paid off handsomely |
| 1996 | 41% | Continued outperformance |
| 1997 | 17% | Returns declining — too much capital chasing too few opportunities |

By 1997, LTCM had become one of the most profitable firms in the history of finance. Its principals were personally worth hundreds of millions of dollars. The fund's reputation was such that Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and other major banks competed to extend credit on favorable terms.

### The Fatal Decision: Returning Capital

In late 1997, Meriwether and his partners made what, in hindsight, was a catastrophic decision. Returns were declining as the fund grew — there simply weren't enough arbitrage opportunities for $7 billion in equity. Rather than scale back their positions, they returned $2.7 billion to investors while keeping the asset base roughly constant.

| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| $7.5B equity, $129B assets (17:1) | $4.7B equity, ~$125B assets (25-27:1) |

The logic seemed sound at the time: the models showed the portfolio was well-hedged, and a smaller equity base meant higher returns on capital. But the effect was to dramatically increase leverage precisely when the fund was expanding into less familiar territory — equity pairs trading, merger arbitrage, emerging market debt. They were straying from the core fixed-income convergence plays that had built the firm.

### Strategy Drift

| Category | Core competence? | Risk |
|----------|-----------------|------|
| On/off-the-run Treasuries | Yes | Understood |
| European sovereign convergence | Yes | Understood |
| Swap spread arbitrage | Yes | Understood |
| Equity pairs / merger arbitrage | **No** | New territory |
| Emerging market sovereign debt | **No** | Tail risk poorly modeled |
| Equity index volatility selling | **No** | Unbounded downside |

The Greenwich headquarters operated with extreme secrecy. No counterparty saw the full picture. Each bank that lent to LTCM knew its own exposure but not the aggregate. This opacity was deliberate — Meriwether feared that revealing positions would allow others to trade against them — but it meant that nobody, including LTCM's own risk managers, could fully grasp the systemic implications of the fund's size.

## The Unraveling (1998)

### Prelude: The Dominoes Start Falling

The first tremors came not from Russia but from Salomon Brothers itself. In early 1998, Sandy Weill — now running Citigroup, which had absorbed Salomon — decided to shut down Salomon's proprietary bond arbitrage desk, the very operation Meriwether had built. Salomon began unwinding positions identical to LTCM's. As Salomon sold, spreads that LTCM was betting would narrow began to widen instead. Other leveraged players holding similar positions felt the pressure.

By May and June 1998, losses were accumulating. The [[Asian Financial Crisis 1997|Asian crisis]] that had erupted in mid-1997 was intensifying, and risk appetite was souring globally.

### August: When the Models Broke

On August 17, 1998, Russia devalued the ruble and declared a moratorium on 281 billion rubles ($13.5 billion) of domestic Treasury debt (GKOs). LTCM's Russian exposure was modest — perhaps $100 million — and the partners believed they had hedged it by shorting rubles. But when Russia defaulted, the banks guaranteeing the ruble hedge shut down, and the Russian government halted further currency trading. The hedge evaporated.

Russia itself wasn't what killed LTCM. What killed LTCM was what happened next.

The Russian default triggered a global "flight to quality" — investors stampeding out of anything remotely risky and into the safest, most liquid instruments on earth: recently issued US Treasury bonds. Every spread that LTCM was betting would narrow simultaneously widened instead. The fund's entire portfolio — conceived as diversified — turned out to be one enormous bet on the convergence of spreads and the compression of liquidity premiums. When liquidity itself became the most prized commodity in global markets, that single bet went catastrophically wrong.

| Date | Event | Loss |
|------|-------|------|
| August 17 | Russia defaults on GKOs, devalues ruble | — |
| August 21 | "Black Friday" for LTCM — worst single day | $550 million |
| — | Merger arb position (Ciena/Tellabs deal doubt) | $160 million that day |
| — | UK positions | $100 million that day |
| — | US bond spread positions | $100 million that day |
| End of August | Monthly loss | $1.8 billion (44% of capital) |
| September 1 | Equity remaining | $2.3 billion |
| September 2 | Meriwether sends letter to investors disclosing losses, invites new capital | — |

The September 2 letter tried to put a brave face on the disaster. Meriwether wrote that the fund's strategies were sound, that the market dislocation was temporary, and that "the opportunity set in these trades at this time is believed to be among the best that LTCM has ever seen." He invited investors to add capital.

Nobody did.

### September: The Death Spiral

Vinny Mattone, an old market contact of Meriwether's, delivered the blunt verdict when called for advice: *"You're finished. When you're down by half, people figure you can go down all the way. They're going to push the market against you. They're not going to roll your trades. You're finished."*

He was right. LTCM's counterparties — the banks that had lent so freely — now began demanding more collateral. Other firms, aware of LTCM's positions through their own dealings, traded against the fund. The very size that had made LTCM profitable now made its positions impossible to exit without moving markets catastrophically.

| Date | Equity Remaining | Effective Leverage |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| January 1, 1998 | ~$4.7 billion | ~25:1 |
| September 1 | $2.3 billion | ~55:1 |
| September 22 | ~$600 million | ~200:1 |
| September 25 | ~$400 million | ~250:1 |

On September 18, LTCM officials contacted Federal Reserve Bank of New York President William McDonough about the firm's financial problems. A Fed team visited LTCM's Greenwich offices two days later. What they found was terrifying: 60,000 trades on the books, $125 billion in balance-sheet assets, $1.4 trillion in notional derivatives exposure — all sitting on a few hundred million dollars of equity.

The Fed's concern was not about LTCM's investors. It was about what would happen if LTCM was forced into a fire sale. Every major bank on Wall Street was a counterparty. A disorderly liquidation would cascade through the system — forced selling would widen spreads further, triggering margin calls at other firms, forcing more selling, in a self-reinforcing spiral that could seize up global credit markets.

## The Bailout (September 23, 1998)

### The Weekend at Liberty Street

On September 22, McDonough invited representatives from sixteen of the world's largest financial institutions to the Federal Reserve Bank of New York's headquarters at 33 Liberty Street in lower Manhattan.

The core group initially comprised four firms who formed working groups to evaluate possible solutions. That evening, the group expanded to thirteen firms. They argued about how much each should contribute and couldn't reach agreement that night.

### The Buffett Bid

On the morning of September 23, as the consortium talks reconvened, Warren Buffett delivered a surprise. He faxed an offer from Sun Valley to buy LTCM's portfolio outright — Berkshire Hathaway would pay $250 million for the fund's net assets and inject $3.75 billion in new capital ($3 billion from Berkshire, $700 million from AIG, $300 million from Goldman Sachs). The partners would be wiped out completely.

McDonough told Meriwether it was likely his best option. But the offer had a 12:30 p.m. deadline, and LTCM's lawyers identified problems with the bid — it was more of an outline than a binding agreement, and the legal structure was ambiguous. By 12:30, the offer lapsed.

### The Consortium

With Buffett's bid dead, the consortium talks resumed with renewed urgency. By approximately 6:00 p.m. on September 23, fourteen firms agreed to inject $3.625 billion in exchange for 90% ownership of the fund.

| Contribution | Firms |
|-------------|-------|
| $300 million each | Bankers Trust, Barclays, Chase Manhattan, Credit Suisse First Boston, Deutsche Bank, Goldman Sachs, Merrill Lynch, J.P. Morgan, Morgan Stanley, Salomon Smith Barney, UBS |
| $125 million each | Société Générale, Lehman Brothers, Paribas |
| **Declined to participate** | **Bear Stearns, Crédit Agricole** |

Bear Stearns' refusal was notable — and deeply ironic. Jimmy Cayne, Bear's CEO, argued his firm's exposure was manageable and saw no reason to contribute. A decade later, Bear Stearns became the first major Wall Street firm to collapse in the 2008 crisis, destroyed by the same kind of leveraged, interconnected exposure that LTCM had exemplified.

### Terms

| Term | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Consortium ownership | 90% of the fund |
| LTCM partners | Retained 10%, stayed to manage the wind-down |
| Compensation | Partners received limited salaries, no bonuses |
| Oversight | Committee of consortium representatives supervised all decisions |
| Fed role | Facilitated and convened — **did not inject public funds** |

The distinction that the Fed facilitated but did not fund the bailout was crucial and hotly debated. Technically, no taxpayer money was at risk. But the Fed's convening power — bringing sixteen firms to Liberty Street and keeping them there until they agreed — was itself a form of state intervention. The implicit message was clear: the Federal Reserve expected these firms to solve this problem, and there would be consequences for those who didn't cooperate.

## The Wind-Down and Aftermath

### Liquidation

Under the consortium's oversight, LTCM's positions were unwound over roughly eighteen months. The irony: the orderly liquidation that the consortium enabled actually vindicated LTCM's thesis. Spreads did eventually converge, and the consortium made a small profit on its $3.625 billion investment, returning the capital by the end of 1999.

The fund was formally dissolved in early 2000.

### Personal Aftermath

| Person | After LTCM |
|--------|-----------|
| **John Meriwether** | Founded JWM Associates in 1999, running similar strategies. The fund lost 44% in the 2007-2009 crisis and was liquidated. Then launched JM Advisors Management in 2010 — a third attempt at the same playbook. |
| **Myron Scholes** | Left LTCM. His and Merton's 1997 Nobel Prize, awarded just months before the collapse, became an enduring symbol of academic hubris meeting market reality. Later co-founded Platinum Grove Asset Management. |
| **Robert Merton** | Left LTCM. Continued academic work at MIT and Harvard. Remained influential in mathematical finance despite the association. |
| **David Mullins** | Largely disappeared from public life after LTCM. The former Fed Vice Chairman's post-crisis absence was perhaps the most telling commentary on the experience. |
| **Victor Haghani** | Joined JWM Associates, then founded Elm Wealth — an advisory firm built on a philosophy of sizing bets conservatively. Wrote *The Missing Billionaires* (2023), arguing that proper risk management matters more than being right about the trade. A direct lesson from LTCM. |
| **Eric Rosenfeld** | Returned to academia and advisory work. |

## Why It Matters

### Too Interconnected to Fail

LTCM was the first modern demonstration of systemic risk through interconnectedness — the "too interconnected to fail" problem that predates and prefigures the more famous "too big to fail" debate of 2008. The danger was not that LTCM was big in absolute terms (its equity base was modest compared to major banks), but that its tentacles reached into every major financial institution simultaneously. A fire sale of LTCM's $125 billion portfolio would have forced counterparties to realize losses, triggering margin calls across the system, compressing liquidity exactly when it was most needed.

This was the mechanism that destroyed [[2008 Financial Crisis|Lehman Brothers]] a decade later — and that the Fed and Treasury failed to prevent in that case.

### Model Risk

LTCM became the defining case study for model risk — the danger that mathematical models built on historical data break down precisely in the tail events they're most needed for.

The models assumed:
- Spreads between related securities would converge (they always had historically)
- Correlations between different trading strategies were low (diversification protected the portfolio)
- Extreme market dislocations were vanishingly improbable (the models assigned near-zero probability to the scenario that actually occurred)

All three assumptions failed simultaneously. Nassim Taleb's later work on "black swans" and fat-tailed distributions — the idea that extreme events are far more common than Gaussian models predict — drew heavily on the LTCM experience. The fund was destroyed by the exact scenario its models said couldn't happen.

### Leverage as Systemic Risk

LTCM demonstrated that gross leverage, not net exposure, determines systemic danger. On paper, the fund was "hedged" — long positions offset by short positions across related securities. Net market risk appeared minimal. But gross exposure was astronomical, and when correlations broke, the hedges stopped working. A fund with $4.7 billion in equity was controlling $125 billion in assets and $1.25 trillion in derivatives — a structure that made it a systemic threat regardless of how carefully the individual positions were paired.

### The Regulatory Gap

LTCM operated with virtually no regulatory oversight. As a hedge fund organized as a limited partnership, it was not required to register with the SEC, disclose its positions, or maintain minimum capital requirements. Its counterparties — the banks — were regulated, but none had visibility into LTCM's aggregate exposure across all its relationships.

The President's Working Group on Financial Markets published a report in April 1999 recommending enhanced disclosure requirements for hedge funds, better risk management practices at banks, and closer monitoring of leverage. Almost none of these recommendations were implemented with sufficient force before the [[2008 Financial Crisis]] proved they had been desperately needed.

### Moral Hazard

The LTCM bailout established a precedent that critics warned about in real time: if a financial entity is sufficiently interconnected, the authorities will step in to prevent its failure. This was the moral hazard argument — the idea that rescuing risk-takers encourages future risk-taking.

The distinction that the Fed used private funds, not public money, was technically correct but practically meaningless. The banks contributed not out of generosity but because the Fed made clear that cooperation was expected. And the banks that contributed knew they would receive similar treatment if their own positions ever became systemically dangerous. The LTCM precedent directly informed the expectations and actions of every major financial institution in 2008.

### Geopolitical Trigger

Russia's sovereign default — a geopolitical event in a country most Western fund managers barely understood — created financial contagion that nearly collapsed the global system. This pattern has repeated: the [[Asian Financial Crisis 1997]] flowed through Thai baht devaluation into Korean, Indonesian, and Russian crises; the 2008 crisis flowed through US housing into European banking; and the pattern persists in how geopolitical shocks propagate through interconnected financial systems.

## The Crowded Trade Problem

One of LTCM's most important — and least appreciated — legacies is the "crowded trade" phenomenon. LTCM was not the only firm running convergence arbitrage. Salomon Brothers' proprietary desk (before its 1998 closure), Merrill Lynch, and dozens of smaller hedge funds held similar positions. When LTCM's losses became known and spreads widened, every firm with similar trades was hit simultaneously. The selling by one firm amplified losses at another, creating a cascade.

This problem — that supposedly diversified market participants often hold identical positions because they use identical models — became a central concern in financial stability analysis and remains one today.

## Cultural Impact

| Work | Significance |
|------|-------------|
| *When Genius Failed* by Roger Lowenstein (2000) | The definitive account. International bestseller, still required reading in business schools. |
| *Inventing Money* by Nicholas Dunbar (2000) | More technical treatment of the mathematical models and their failure |
| Nassim Taleb's *Black Swan* theory | Partly inspired by LTCM's inability to account for fat-tailed distributions |
| Business school case studies | LTCM became the canonical teaching case for risk management, leverage, and model failure at Harvard, Wharton, and every other finance program |
| Financial crisis documentaries | Featured prominently in multiple films about systemic risk |

Warren Buffett captured the lesson in a 1998 talk to students shortly after the collapse: "If you take the 16 of them, they have about as high an IQ as any 16 people working together in one business in the country, including Microsoft. An incredible amount of intellect in one room... They had in aggregate, the 16, had 300 or 400 years of experience doing exactly what they were doing. And then you throw in the third factor that most of them had most of their very substantial net worth in the business. Hundreds and hundreds of millions of their own money up. Super high intellect and working in a field that they knew. Essentially they went broke. That to me is absolutely fascinating."
