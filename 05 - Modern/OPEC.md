# OPEC

The Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries was founded in Baghdad in 1960 by five oil-producing nations — [[Persian Economic History|Iran]], Iraq, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and Venezuela — as a defensive response to the [[Oil Economy#The Seven Sisters Era (1920s-1970s)|Seven Sisters]]' unilateral price cuts that slashed producer revenues without consultation. For its first decade OPEC was largely irrelevant, unable to prevent the majors from setting prices. The transformation came in October 1973, when Arab members weaponized oil during the Yom Kippur War, imposing an embargo that quadrupled prices and demonstrated that producers could act as price-makers rather than price-takers — the first [[Oil Crisis]]. A second shock followed the [[Iranian Revolution]] in 1979, pushing prices to $39/barrel and triggering the [[Petrodollar System]] that recycled OPEC surpluses into Western financial markets. But OPEC's golden age was brief: the 1980s price collapse — driven by conservation, non-OPEC supply (North Sea, Alaska), and Saudi overproduction — exposed the cartel's fundamental weakness, the incentive for each member to cheat on quotas. Internal rivalries deepened as the [[Iran-Iraq War]] pitted two founding members against each other, Iraq's invasion of Kuwait (1990) fractured Arab solidarity, and Saudi Arabia's role as swing producer gave Riyadh de facto veto power over collective policy. The 2020s brought reinvention as OPEC+, incorporating Russia as a production partner — an alliance of convenience between the world's three largest producers (US shale, Saudi Arabia, Russia) where only two coordinate, while the third (the US) free-rides on price discipline it does not join.

## Founding Context

### Why OPEC Was Created

The immediate trigger was two unilateral price cuts by the Seven Sisters in 1959-1960 that reduced posted prices — the basis for calculating government revenue — without consulting producer governments. The cuts followed a global oil glut and reflected the majors' interest in protecting market share. For producer countries, whose budgets depended on posted prices, the cuts were both an economic blow and a sovereignty insult.

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| 1959 price cut | Majors cut posted price by 10% without notice |
| 1960 price cut | Second unilateral reduction |
| Revenue impact | Producer governments' income fell significantly |
| Precedent | Venezuela's 50-50 profit-sharing (1948) showed alternatives existed |
| Key figure | Juan Pablo Perez Alfonzo (Venezuela) — "the father of OPEC" |
| Arab catalyst | Abdullah Tariki (Saudi Arabia) — rare technocrat willing to challenge Aramco |

### The Baghdad Conference (September 1960)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Location | Baghdad, Iraq |
| Duration | Five days |
| Outcome | OPEC charter signed |
| Initial goal | Restore prices to pre-cut levels |
| Mechanism | Collective negotiation with companies |
| Reception | Western companies and governments largely ignored it |

### Early Irrelevance (1960-1970)

| Problem | Why |
|---------|-----|
| No production control | Members couldn't agree on quotas |
| Companies controlled pricing | Posted price system still in majors' hands |
| Surplus capacity | Too much oil chasing market |
| Internal divisions | Monarchies vs. republics, Arab vs. non-Arab |
| Limited leverage | No credible threat of supply restriction |

## The Nationalization Wave

The shift from irrelevance to dominance required producer countries to take control of their own oil — a process that unfolded unevenly across the 1970s, overturning the concession system that had governed Middle Eastern oil since [[BP|D'Arcy's 1901 deal]].

| Country | Date | Method |
|---------|------|--------|
| Libya | 1970-73 | Qaddafi forced terms on independents first |
| Iraq | 1972 | Iraq Petroleum Company nationalized |
| Iran | 1951 (reversed), 1979 | [[1953 Iranian Coup|Mossadegh's nationalization]] reversed by coup; completed after revolution |
| Saudi Arabia | 1976-80 | Gradual acquisition of Aramco |
| Kuwait | 1975 | Kuwait Oil Company nationalized |
| Venezuela | 1976 | PDVSA created from foreign operations |

The nationalization wave transferred ~90% of the world's proven oil reserves from international oil companies (IOCs) to national oil companies (NOCs) — the single largest wealth transfer of the 20th century outside of war.

## The Oil Weapon: 1973 Embargo

### The Embargo

| Event | Date | Effect |
|-------|------|--------|
| Yom Kippur War | October 6, 1973 | Egypt/Syria attack Israel |
| US airlift to Israel | October 14 | Arab anger |
| OAPEC embargo declared | October 17 | Arab OPEC members embargo US, Netherlands |
| Production cuts | Monthly | 5% rolling reductions |
| Price quadruples | Oct 1973 - Mar 1974 | $3 to $12/barrel |

### Why It Worked

| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Market tightness | Demand had caught up with supply by 1973 |
| OPEC market share | ~55% of world oil production |
| Inelastic demand | No short-term substitutes for oil |
| Coordination | Arab states acted together (OAPEC, the Arab subset) |
| Panic multiplier | Actual shortage was ~7-9% but hoarding amplified impact |

### What Changed

The embargo's significance went beyond price. It demonstrated that a commodity cartel could reshape global politics. For the first time since decolonization, developing countries wielded leverage over the industrialized world. The Seven Sisters lost their role as price-setters; OPEC's national oil companies replaced them as the dominant actors in global oil.

## The Price Revolution (1973-1980)

| Year | Nominal Price ($/bbl) | Key Event |
|------|-----------------------|-----------|
| 1970 | $1.80 | Pre-OPEC pricing |
| 1973 (Oct) | $3.00 | Pre-embargo |
| 1974 (Jan) | $11.65 | Post-embargo |
| 1978 | $13.00 | Stable plateau |
| 1979 | $34.00 | Iranian Revolution shock |
| 1980 | $39.00 | Iran-Iraq War |

The price revolution redistributed hundreds of billions of dollars from oil-consuming to oil-producing countries. OPEC members' collective revenue rose from ~$23 billion (1972) to ~$280 billion (1980) — a twelvefold increase that funded sovereign wealth accumulation, massive construction programs, and arms purchases, while simultaneously triggering stagflation in the West and seeding the Third World debt crisis through the [[Petrodollar System]].

## OPEC's Internal Dynamics

### The Cartel Problem

OPEC faces the classic cartel dilemma: collective restraint raises prices for all, but each individual member benefits from cheating — producing above quota while others restrain.

| Incentive | Pressure |
|-----------|----------|
| High prices | Require production discipline |
| Each member wants more revenue | Incentive to overproduce |
| Cheating is hard to detect | Output monitoring imperfect |
| Different cost structures | Low-cost producers can afford price wars |
| Different fiscal needs | High-population states (Iran, Nigeria) need more revenue than Gulf monarchies |

### Saudi Arabia as Swing Producer

| Feature | Significance |
|---------|--------------|
| Largest reserves (conventional) | Can increase or cut without pain |
| Lowest production costs | ~$3-5/barrel |
| Small population (relative to output) | Less fiscal pressure than Iran or Nigeria |
| Spare capacity | 1-2 million bpd buffer |
| Political alignment with US | Moderating influence on price spikes |
| De facto OPEC leader | Sets tone for collective decisions |

### Internal Rivalries

| Rivalry | Dimension |
|---------|-----------|
| Saudi Arabia vs. Iran | Sunni-Shia, Arab-Persian, pro-US vs. anti-US |
| Saudi Arabia vs. Venezuela | Price hawks (Venezuela) vs. market share (Saudi) |
| Iran vs. Iraq | War (1980-88), competing for OPEC influence |
| Kuwait vs. Iraq | Iraq invaded Kuwait (1990), partly over oil pricing disputes |
| Population states vs. Gulf monarchies | High-population members (Iran, Iraq, Nigeria) need maximum revenue; low-population members can tolerate lower prices |

## The 1980s Collapse

### What Happened

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Conservation | Demand fell as efficiency improved |
| Non-OPEC supply | North Sea, Alaska, Mexico came online |
| Nuclear/coal substitution | Reduced oil's share of power generation |
| Saudi overproduction | 1985-86, Saudi Arabia abandoned swing producer role, flooded market |
| Price crash | From $28 (1985) to $10 (1986) |

### Consequences

| Effect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| OPEC revenue collapse | From $280B (1980) to ~$77B (1986) |
| Petrostates destabilized | Soviet Union's fiscal crisis deepened |
| Debt crisis worsened | Oil-exporting developing countries hit twice |
| US shale delayed | Low prices killed early unconventional investment |
| Cheap oil era returns | 1986-2003, oil averaged $20-30/barrel |

## OPEC+ and the Modern Era

### Formation of OPEC+ (2016)

When US shale production surged after 2010 — adding ~8 million bpd of new supply — OPEC alone could no longer balance markets. Saudi Arabia brokered a deal with Russia and other non-OPEC producers to coordinate cuts.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Partners | 10 non-OPEC producers led by Russia |
| Combined share | ~55% of global production |
| Coordination | Joint ministerial monitoring committee |
| Key relationship | Saudi Arabia - Russia axis (MBS - Putin) |
| Weakness | US shale as uncoordinated swing producer |

### The Three-Player Game

| Actor | Strategy |
|-------|----------|
| OPEC+ (Saudi + Russia) | Coordinate cuts to support price |
| US shale | Respond to price signals, no coordination |
| Consumers (IEA) | Strategic petroleum reserves, demand management |

The fundamental asymmetry: OPEC+ bears the cost of restraint while US producers free-ride on higher prices without cutting. This dynamic limits how high OPEC+ can push prices before shale fills the gap.

## Fragmentation (1992-2026)

OPEC's institutional architecture has weakened in the 2019-2026 window through four formal exits in seven years — the densest cluster of departures in the cartel's history. The pattern is consistent enough across cases to be a structural rather than incidental phenomenon: OPEC's quota system is calibrated to support price by restraining the highest-capacity producers, and those producers eventually conclude that the cartel premium is smaller than the volume foregone. Indonesia and Ecuador both suspended-and-resumed before final exit; Qatar left for LNG focus amid the Saudi-led blockade; Angola left because its quota was below its actual production; the UAE — the largest defection in OPEC history — left because its capacity was years ahead of any quota OPEC+ would assign.

| Member | Joined | Left | Capacity arc at exit | Stated reason |
|--------|--------|------|----------------------|---------------|
| Ecuador | 1973 | Suspended Dec 1992; rejoined Oct 2007; withdrew Jan 1, 2020 | Stagnant ~0.5 mb/d | Membership fees too high; quotas restrictive |
| Indonesia | 1962 | Suspended Jan 2009; rejoined Jan 2016; suspended Nov 2016 | Declining (became net importer) | Couldn't meet quota; later quota too restrictive |
| Qatar | 1961 | Jan 1, 2019 | Stable ~0.6 mb/d crude | LNG focus; GCC blockade rift with Saudi (June 2017+) |
| Angola | 2007 | Jan 1, 2024 | Declining ~1.1 mb/d | Quota dispute (1.46 → 1.11 mb/d cut at Nov 2023 meeting) |
| UAE | 1967 (Abu Dhabi) | May 1, 2026 | **Rising — peak of capacity arc (~4.8 mb/d → 5 mb/d 2027)** | "Pure policy change," capacity flexibility |

The UAE case (announced April 28, 2026, effective May 1) is structurally distinct because it is the first major exporter to leave at the peak of its capacity arc rather than the trough. ADNOC's $150 billion 2023-2027 capex program had moved UAE capacity to ~4.8 mb/d against quota-constrained production of ~3.5 mb/d; the Baker Institute estimated unconstrained operation could generate "upward of $50 billion in additional annual revenues." Energy Minister Suhail Al Mazrouei framed it as a "pure policy change" allowing flexibility, denying any political dispute with Saudi Arabia, and citing the [[Strait of Hormuz]] closure as the reason the timing wouldn't shock the market.

The political surround was harder to detach from the announcement. The Saudi-UAE Yemen coalition had fractured in December 2025 over Saudi airstrikes on what Riyadh said were Iranian weapons shipments to UAE-backed Southern Transitional Council separatists. Five days before the OPEC announcement, US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent publicly defended a $20 billion currency swap line for the UAE before a Senate appropriations subcommittee. UAE diplomatic adviser Anwar Gargash, separately on April 28, called the GCC's wartime cohesion "the weakest historically." The OPEC exit reads as the institutional crystallization of a Gulf realignment toward bilateral US anchoring rather than continued multilateral GCC alignment.

For the long arc, the UAE case validates the cartel-fragmentation framing rather than introducing it. Indonesia, Ecuador, and Angola left because they couldn't gain by staying. The UAE is the first major member to leave because it expects to gain by leaving — a different signal entirely. That distinction matters because cartel discipline rarely fails uniformly; it fails first at the highest-capacity members. Once the pattern becomes "high-capacity producers exit," the cartel's structural weakness compounds: each exit lowers the cost of the next exit, the remaining members bear more of the discipline burden, and the institutional architecture moves from coordination at quota-supported price levels toward informal restraint at lower price levels.

The realistic post-fragmentation equilibrium is a Saudi-led informal price floor enforced by spare-capacity threat, not a quota system. That is structurally weaker than OPEC at peak power but is not the absence of coordination. ## The Deeper Point

OPEC's history illuminates the economics of commodity cartels and the politics of resource sovereignty. The organization succeeded in shifting oil rents from Western companies to producer governments — the largest peaceful wealth transfer in modern history. But its members' divergent interests (population size, fiscal needs, geopolitical alignment) have always limited its ability to function as a true cartel. Saudi Arabia's dominance means OPEC is less a cartel of equals than a Saudi-led coordination mechanism with veto power over price.

The deeper lesson connects to this vault's [[Institutions and Progress|institutional]] framework: OPEC didn't just change oil prices — it changed who controlled the world's most important commodity. The nationalization wave it enabled transferred assets from private companies operating under Western legal frameworks to state-owned enterprises operating under national sovereignty. Whether this was progress depends on the institution: Norway's Statoil channeled oil wealth into a sovereign wealth fund; Venezuela's PDVSA became a patronage machine. The resource didn't change; the institutions governing it did. OPEC's real achievement was demonstrating that developing countries could assert sovereignty over their natural resources — a principle that the [[1953 Iranian Coup]] had temporarily reversed and that the nationalization wave permanently established.
